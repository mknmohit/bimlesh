## Section 0 — Executive summary (read first, 5 minutes)

### What this plan is, and is not

This plan covers **Day 1 to Week 6** of home recovery, structured as four phases, then sketches Weeks 7 to 12 and beyond. After Week 6, the surgeon will reassess at OPD and likely clear cane walking, possibly driving, and a slower long-term progression. We do not plan past Week 6 in detail; that is for the surgeon.

This plan is **conservative on purpose.** Two factors make her case different from a textbook unilateral TKR:

1. **Both knees were operated.** She has no "good leg" to absorb compensation. Standard step counts, balance milestones, and walker weaning timelines are pushed back by roughly one to two weeks.
2. **30 years of chronic low-back pain.** Several standard TKR exercises (prone hangs, double knee-to-chest, deep bridges, long supine resting flat) flare her lumbar pain. Lumbar-friendly versions are substituted throughout.

She is also on **apixaban** (Abaxis 2.5) until Day 14, which raises bleeding risk if anyone forces her knee or massages tissue aggressively, and on **teriparatide** (Inj Terifrac) for one year, which means **no high-impact activity ever** (no jumping, running, rope-skipping, aerobics step-class).

**Honest expectation for Day 1 at home:** total exercise about 10 to 15 minutes, spread over 5 short bouts. She just finished a long journey home, she is tired, swollen, and on strong pain medications. Day 1 is a recovery day. We build from there.

### The 10 golden rules

1. **Little and often beats one long session.** Three to five short bouts of 10 to 15 minutes work better than one 60-minute push.
2. **Pain ladder during exercise: 0 to 3 is the safe work zone; a 4 means reduce the range or slow down; a 5 or higher means stop that exercise for the day. If pain stays more than 2 hours after the session, the dose was too big.** This is the "2-2-2 rule" applied in plain English.
3. **Knee straightening matters more than knee bending in Week 1.** A knee that cannot straighten causes a lifelong limp. **Never** put a pillow under the knee; under the calf is fine, under the heel is fine.
4. **Never strain-hold the breath during an exercise** (no Valsalva). She is on apixaban; straining spikes blood pressure and bleeding risk. Hum or count out loud. The one exception is the short 3 to 5-second **inspiratory hold** during incentive spirometry (Exercise 11) — that is a gentle sustained inhale, not a strain.
5. **Every exercise has an "earliest safe day."** See Section 8's lookup table. Skipping ahead to feel faster progress causes swelling flares, not faster progress.
6. **Low-back pain modifies three positions specifically:** no long flat-on-back rest without a small lumbar towel; no prone (face-down) lying; no double knee-to-chest stretch. Single knee-to-chest is fine from Week 2.
7. **Ice 15 to 20 minutes after every exercise session, and elevate legs between sessions.** Non-negotiable in Weeks 1 to 2.
8. **Walker stays until the surgeon clears a change (typically 4 to 6 weeks for bilateral).** Don't rush the cane. Any transition from walker → two sticks → cane must meet objective criteria (see Phase 3/4 in Section 5) AND be approved by the surgeon or physiotherapist. Bilateral patients almost always need the full 6 weeks.
9. **Best exercise time slots:** late morning (10:00 to 11:30 AM, after the 8:30 AM NSAID has absorbed) and mid-afternoon (1:30 to 4:30 PM, NSAID at peak plasma). Avoid exercise within 4 hours of any Ultracet dose (drowsy, fall risk) and after 9 PM (Gabapin-NT sedation starts).
10. **If in doubt, stop and call.** Ice, elevate, call Mrs. Kiran. The cost of an unnecessary call is zero.

### The "2-2-2 rule" for daily safety check

Three checks done at the same time every morning (best time: 9:00 AM, after breakfast meds, before the morning exercise bout):

| Check | Normal | Too much |
|---|---|---|
| Pain 2 hours after yesterday's heaviest session | Back to baseline, or at most 2 points higher | More than 2 points higher → that session was too big |
| Soreness duration after exercise | Fades within 2 hours | Lasts more than 2 hours → cut next session by 30 to 50 % |
| Knee circumference (soft tape across the middle of the kneecap, same time each day) | Slowly decreasing week to week | More than 2 cm increase from yesterday morning → full rest day, ice every 2 hours |

If any of the three is "too much," take ONE complete rest day (ankle pumps, breathing, incentive spirometry, elevation, ice — nothing else), then restart at **half** the previous volume and build back one step per day. Do not try to "catch up" the missed work. The knee cannot absorb it.

### Lifelong rules (set expectations from Day 1)

- **No squatting, no kneeling, no floor-sitting** (no Indian squat toilet, no cross-legged sitting, no vajrasana, no sashtang pranam). The implant cannot tolerate these positions safely. Use a Western commode and a chair for prayer.
- **No high-impact** (no jumping, running, rope-skipping, aerobics step-class). She can walk, cycle, swim. Permanent rule because of the implant + teriparatide.
- **No deep loaded squat past 60°** even when she is fully recovered. Sitting in a chair past 90° is fine; the forbidden version is loaded squatting.
- **Keep both knees moving forever.** Quad sets, gluteal squeezes, ankle pumps, gentle cycling — twice a week, lifelong. Muscles "go to sleep" again with disuse, even years out.

### What should be delayed indefinitely or skipped permanently

A few "standard" TKR items appear in many internet protocols but are explicitly **avoided** in this plan, with reasons:

- **Prone knee hangs** (lying face-down with the knee dangling off the bed for an extension stretch). Loads the lumbar facet joints and reliably flares chronic LBP. **Substitute:** heel-prop extension (Exercise 8), plus optional 1 to 2 kg sandbag on the thigh from Week 3.
- **Long supine periods (more than 30 minutes) with both legs flat and straight.** Increases the lumbar arch and loads the L4–L5 discs. Use a small lumbar roll, or break long supine sessions into 15-minute blocks with a brief seated break. Never overnight without modification.
- **Counted SLR before Day 10.** The hip flexor (iliopsoas) pulls on the lumbar spine and worsens LBP. **Substitute:** quad sets (Exercise 5), short-arc quads (Exercise 26). Counted SLR enters at Day 10 only after the quad lag is resolved.
- **Double knee-to-chest stretch.** Forces full bilateral knee flexion that the implants cannot tolerate yet. Single knee-to-chest (one leg at a time) is fine from Day 8.
- **Deep bridges / full hip lifts.** The mini-bridge (3 to 5 cm lift only, glute-first technique) is the only bridge in this plan. Deep bridges hyperextend the lumbar spine and risk flaring her back.
- **Cat-cow on hands and knees.** Kneeling on the operated knees is permanently forbidden. **Substitute:** seated cat-cow (Exercise 21) from Day 12.
- **Side-lying clamshells before Week 4.** Earlier side-lying loads one knee unevenly and can flare the hip. Supine isometric hip abduction (Exercise 22) starts on Day 9; the side-lying dynamic version waits.
- **Treadmill, elliptical, and any "cardio class" before 12 weeks.** Treadmill at the earliest from Week 5 to 6 with handrail support, only after walker is dropped. HSS specifically warns to delay elliptical until Week 12 because of patellofemoral stress unique to that machine.
- **Pool swimming or tub immersion before Week 6.** The wound must be fully healed and surgeon-cleared. Showering on a stool with a waterproof dressing is the only water exposure permitted before Week 6.
- **Squatting, kneeling, floor-sitting, jumping, running, rope-skipping.** Permanent restrictions for life of the implant.

## Section 3 — Low back pain: how this guide adapts to it

Her chronic low-back pain (30 years since childbirth, "bearable") changes roughly 30 % of a standard TKR program. Here's what is customised specifically for her.

### What's actually causing the pain (after 30 years)

Post-partum low-back pain lasting this long is almost never the original tissue damage anymore. The ligament laxity from pregnancy hormones, any diastasis recti, and pelvic-floor trauma have usually stabilised by year 2 or 3. What persists at year 30 is typically one or more of these four causes:

1. **Deconditioned deep core muscles.** The transverse abdominis (TrA) and multifidus (deep back muscles) lost their firing pattern after childbirth and never retrained. Meta-analyses show core-stabilisation exercises reduce pain 4.4 points on a 10-point scale vs 1.8 for generic physiotherapy.
2. **Sacroiliac joint dysfunction.** The SI joints became more mobile during pregnancy (relaxin hormone). Accounts for 16 to 30 % of chronic LBP; more common in women who have given birth. The glutes normally stabilise the SI joint via the sacrotuberous ligament — and post-partum glutes often never fully reactivate ("glute inhibition"), leaving the SI joints unsupported.
3. **Lumbar facet arthropathy.** Age 53 + 30 years of uneven loading = facet joints wear down. These little joints at the back of the spine are loaded most by extension (arching back, prone lying). This is why we SKIP prone hangs.
4. **Gluteal inhibition.** The buttock muscles don't fire properly, so the lower back muscles (erector spinae) over-work to stabilise. Retraining the glutes offloads the back.

**What this means for her rehab.** The best LBP treatment for her specific case is a combination of **core stabilisation** (3-in-1 brace, pelvic tilts) + **pelvic-floor retraining** (Kegels) + **glute strengthening** (mini-bridges, hip extension) + **hamstring stretching** (tight hamstrings flex the pelvis backward, loading the low back). All are SAFE post-TKR and all INCLUDED in the plan below.

### Double-benefit exercises — help BOTH knees AND low back

Eight exercises directly help her 30-year low-back pain AND contribute to knee recovery. Prioritise these:

| Exercise | Knee benefit | Back benefit | Evidence for LBP |
|---|---|---|---|
| Deep belly breathing (Ex 1) | Lung health + calms nervous system | Co-activates diaphragm with TrA (improved EMG activity and pain) | **Moderate** — 2024 RCT (Shahdadi et al.) showed pain, sleep, and TrA EMG improvements |
| Pelvic tilts (Ex 14) | Gentle core prep for walking | Flattens lumbar curve, decompresses facets | **Moderate** — Williams flexion protocol |
| Abdominal 3-in-1 brace (Ex 15) | Activates core for safer transfers | Wakes up TrA + pelvic floor | **Strong** — 8-week programs show multifidus hypertrophy on MRI |
| Kegels (Ex 16) | Improves continence → fewer night transfers → fewer falls | Best-evidence exercise for postpartum LBP specifically | **Strong** — meta-analysis shows largest effect size in postpartum LBP subgroup (Bi 2022) |
| Single knee-to-chest stretch (Ex 19, from Day 8) | Gentle gravity-assisted knee flexion | Classic Williams flexion exercise | **Moderate** — decades of clinical use |
| Seated hamstring stretch (Ex 20) | Improves knee extension | Tight hamstrings pull pelvis backward → lumbar overload | **Strong** — 2024 meta-analysis of 14 RCTs shows significant LBP reduction |
| Seated cat-cow (Ex 21) | Breaks up seated stiffness | Mobilises lumbar spine in both directions | **Weak but mechanism-based** |

### What we SKIP (that standard guides would include)

- **Prone hangs / face-down knee extension** — increases low-back arch and loads painful facet joints.
- **Long supine sessions with legs straight (> 30 min)** — increases low-back curve and compresses discs.
- **Straight-leg raise (SLR) as a counted exercise before Day 10** — the hip flexor (iliopsoas) pulls on the lumbar spine. Use quad sets + short-arc quads as substitutes for extension work. Counted SLR enters at Day 10 only after the quad lag is resolved and her core brace is reliable.
- **Double knee-to-chest stretch** — forces full bilateral knee flexion her implants can't yet tolerate. Single knee-to-chest is fine (from Day 8).
- **Deep bridges / full hip-lifts.** Mini-bridges (3 to 5 cm lift) only, and only from Day 7.

### What we UPGRADE (because it helps BOTH her knees and back)

- **Pelvic tilts** — usually 10 × 2 per day; we do **10 × 3 to 4 per day** for her. Reduces LBP and activates deep core.
- **Abdominal bracing** — upgraded to the **"3-in-1 brace"**: deep breathing + belly draw-in + Kegel together. Best-evidence core exercise for post-partum LBP.
- **Kegels (pelvic floor)** — added from Day 3. Strongest evidence for post-partum LBP. Safe post-TKR (no knee load).
- **Diaphragmatic (belly) breathing** — extended to 10 breaths and combined with the belly draw-in.
- **Supine hamstring stretch from Week 2** — tight hamstrings worsen low-back pain.
- **Single knee-to-chest stretch from Week 2** — classic LBP pain-reliever; also gives the knee gentle bending practice.
- **Seated cat-cow spinal mobility from Week 2** — substitutes for the standard cat-cow which requires kneeling (forbidden post-TKR).
- **Mini-bridge from Day 7** (with strict glute-first technique; skip if LBP > 3/10 and retry later) — helps glutes support the sacroiliac joint, reducing chronic LBP cause.

### Sleep position — how we resolve the conflict

**The conflict.** Standard TKR rule says "sleep supine with legs STRAIGHT, no pillow under knees." Standard LBP advice says "supine with knees bent, pillow under knees."

**Our solution:**

- **Primary sleep position (Weeks 1 to 2): SUPINE with legs STRAIGHT** (TKR rule wins — knee extension is a "one-way door": once lost, very hard to recover).
- **Modifications within that constraint:**
  - **Small folded hand towel (5 cm thick)** tucked under the LOW BACK at L4–L5 level. Fills the natural curve, does NOT bend the knee.
  - **Calf pillow stack slightly higher** so the knees rest at about 5° to 10° soft flexion (NOT under the knee, but the pillow stops just before the knee). This tiny knee bend reduces low-back arch without creating a flexion contracture.
  - **Semi-Fowler's position (30° to 45° reclined)** using extra pillows behind the upper body is an excellent alternative — removes the low-back arch entirely while keeping knees straight. Use this for the first 5 to 7 nights if comfortable.
- **Side-lying: SAFE from Day 5 to 7 onward for 20 to 30 min periods**, with:
  - A **firm pillow between the knees from mid-thigh to ankle** (keeps knees near-straight AND reduces hip adduction pressure).
  - Knees slightly bent (20° to 30°), NOT fetal position.
  - Sleep on either side (no "operative side" distinction since both are operative).
- **Daytime only — short-bout hook-lying (knees fully bent, feet flat)** for LBP relief: 10 to 20 minutes at a time, then straighten legs. Daytime REST position only, NEVER overnight.
- **NEVER: sleeping with a pillow UNDER the knees overnight** (forbidden, flexion deformity risk).
- **NEVER: prone (face-down)** (both a knee-flexion problem AND a low-back-arch problem).

## Section 4 — Red flags and escalation (when to stop, skip, rest, or call)

This section answers four questions, in order of severity:
1. When should she stop an exercise mid-rep?
2. When should she skip a single exercise or take a complete rest day?
3. When should the family call Mrs. Kiran or Dr Arora?
4. When is it a Max Emergency call?

### Stop the exercise immediately

Stop the exercise (don't finish the set) if any of these happen:

- **Sharp, stabbing, or "deep inside the joint" pain.** Different from muscle tightness or stretching. Sharp = possible soft-tissue injury or implant problem.
- **Pain rises above 5/10 during the movement.** A 4 means slow down or reduce range. A 5 or higher means today's dose is too big.
- **The knee "gives way," buckles, clicks loudly with pain, or feels suddenly unstable.** Sit down immediately. Call surgeon same day.
- **Sudden swelling or visible knee enlargement during the session.** Apply ice 15 min, elevate, recheck. If swollen by more than 2 cm vs morning, full rest tomorrow.
- **Dizziness, lightheadedness, or feeling faint.** Sit or lie down. Sip water. Common after Terifrac (orthostatic). If repeated in a session, skip remaining exercises today.
- **Cramp in foot or calf that does not release with gentle massage in 1 minute.** Stop. Rest 5 min. If cramp is in one calf only and persists, see DVT red flag below.
- **Sudden chest tightness, breathlessness, palpitations.** Sit down. If not eased in 1 minute → emergency call.

### Skip a single exercise (not the whole session)

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Pain on JUST that exercise reaches 5/10 or stays at 4/10 after reducing range | Skip it today, do the others, retry at half dose tomorrow |
| Cramping after just that exercise | Skip it for 24 hours, retry next session |
| Wound feels "tight" only when doing it | Skip it for 48 hours, then retry slowly |

### Take a full rest day

- 2-2-2 rule flagged (any of: pain > 2 above baseline 2 hours later; soreness > 2 hours; knee girth up > 2 cm).
- Fever > 38 °C / 100.4 °F for any reason.
- After a fall (even if she "feels fine," call surgeon AND rest).
- After any new bleeding event (gum, nose, urine, stool, large new bruise).
- Day of dressing change visit (Day 8) and day of suture removal visit (Day 15) — light only.
- 1 day per week as a planned rest day from Week 3 onwards (her body needs it).

### Back-off protocol (when 2-2-2 is flagged)

1. **Today is a rest day.** Only ankle pumps (every 1 to 2 hours), deep breathing (4 sessions), incentive spirometry (10 breaths every 2 hours), elevation (5 sessions × 30 min), ice (every 2 hours × 15 min). NO strengthening, NO ROM, NO standing exercises.
2. **Tomorrow restart at HALF the previous tolerable dose.** Re-apply 2-2-2. If still flagged, take another rest day and call Mrs. Kiran.
3. **Days 3 to 5:** add one set or two reps every 24 hours, provided 2-2-2 stays green.
4. **Do NOT try to "catch up" missed volume.** The knee cannot absorb it.

### Call Mrs. Kiran (7520700700) — same-day, non-emergency

| Symptom | Why |
|---|---|
| Pain at rest > 5/10 for two days in a row | Inflammation cycle, may need medication review |
| Swelling rebound that doesn't reduce after 1 rest day | Possible bursitis or hematoma |
| ROM has dropped 10° or more from yesterday | Possible bleed (apixaban window) or arthrofibrosis starting |
| Wound looks "different", redder, weeping, smelly | Possible early infection |
| Constipation > 4 days despite Duphalac + NaturoLax | Need bowel review |
| Sleep poor 3 nights in a row | Pain medication may need titration |
| Repeated near-falls | Walker fit / footwear / fatigue review needed |
| Patient refuses to do exercises and seems depressed | Mood support is part of recovery |

### Call Dr Arora's office (9650100600) — same-day urgent

| Symptom | Why |
|---|---|
| Fever > 38 °C with chills, even without wound symptoms | Possible deep infection |
| Wound: spreading redness > 2 cm, pus, foul smell, dehiscence | Surgical site infection |
| Sudden severe pain in one knee unresponsive to medication | Possible hematoma, mechanical problem |
| New bruising larger than 5 cm, or rapidly enlarging old bruise | Bleeding (apixaban) |
| Gum bleeding > 5 min, nose bleeding > 10 min, blood in urine/stool, black/tarry stool | Bleeding (apixaban + NSAID) |
| Calf swelling > 2 cm asymmetric, calf tenderness, calf warm/red | Possible DVT |
| Persistent vomiting, can't tolerate fluids | Multiple causes |
| Decreased urine output (< 500 mL in 24 h) | Renal stress (NSAID + tramadol + dehydration) |
| Skin rash, especially after starting any new medication | Drug reaction |
| Numb patch around the operated knee that is new and growing | Possible nerve compression |
| Foot drop (cannot lift toes) on either side | Nerve injury, same-day urgent |

### Go to Max Emergency (011-43033333) immediately

| Symptom | Likely cause |
|---|---|
| **Sudden severe shortness of breath, chest pain, or coughing up blood** | Pulmonary embolism. Call ambulance immediately. |
| **Sudden severe one-sided headache, weakness, or numbness on one side of body, or speech difficulty** | Possible stroke or intracranial bleed |
| **Loss of consciousness, severe lightheadedness with sweating** | Multiple emergencies possible |
| **Sudden severe abdominal pain with vomiting and no bowel motion** | Possible bowel obstruction |
| **Active heavy bleeding from wound that doesn't stop with 5 minutes of firm pressure** | Wound dehiscence or vascular injury |
| **Fever > 39 °C with confusion** | Sepsis |
| **Generalised seizure** (uncommon, but tramadol + nortriptyline + gabapentinoid combination lowers seizure threshold) | Drug interaction |
| **Tremor + sweating + fever + agitation + hyperreflexia** | Serotonin syndrome (tramadol + nortriptyline) |
| **Slow breathing < 10 breaths per minute, very drowsy, hard to wake** | Opioid + gabapentinoid respiratory depression |
| **Acute knee dislocation, "clunk" with inability to bear weight** | Mechanical implant problem |
| **Major fall with head impact, or any fall that produced severe new pain** | Periprosthetic fracture, head injury |

### Apixaban-specific cautions (until evening of Day 14, 03-May-2026)

- **Bleeding watch is highest in this window.** Bruising is expected; firm growing hematoma is not.
- **No forced passive stretching** in Weeks 1 to 2. All knee bending is patient-controlled (active or active-assisted with the towel).
- **No aggressive soft-tissue massage** over thighs or around the incision.
- **Bath sponging only.** Showering on a stool from Day 5 to 7 with waterproof dressing.
- **Skin protection from ice:** wrap in cloth, do not exceed 20 minutes per session, watch for white/blue patches.
- **No new "anti-inflammatory" medicines** (no aspirin, ibuprofen, fish-oil mega-dose, turmeric supplements, etc.) without checking with the team. The PPI Pantocid-DSR is on her list to mitigate GI bleeding risk.
- **No dental procedures** during this window.

### Teriparatide-specific cautions (continuing for 1 year)

- **Orthostatic precaution at every position change.** Sit on the edge of the bed for 30 seconds before standing. Highest dizziness risk in the first 4 hours after the 9 PM injection.
- **No high-impact activity ever.** No jumping, running, rope-skipping, aerobics step-class. Walking, cycling, swimming OK from the appropriate phase.
- **Calcium and vitamin D coverage must continue all year.** Calgrow Forte HD prescribed for 6 weeks; ask Dr Arora at the OPD whether to continue or switch to a maintenance product.
- **Watch for hypercalcemia symptoms** (cumulative with Calgrow + Dee Time 60K + teriparatide stack, peaking around Week 4 to 6): unusual thirst, frequent urination especially at night, nausea, vomiting, constipation, lethargy, confusion, muscle weakness, new bone pain. Serum calcium check at Week 4 to 6.

### Bilateral-TKR specific cautions

- **There is no "good leg."** Don't favor one side; both knees get equal work. A persistent limp early increases low-back compensation and can flare her LBP.
- **Sit-to-stand pushes through ARMRESTS, never pulls on walker.** The walker can tip forward and cause both operated knees to fall.
- **No single-leg stance until Week 5.** Standing on one fresh implant while the other is unable to absorb a wobble is the highest fall-risk move in early rehab. Wait for the safety gate (Phase 4).
- **Stairs use two-feet-per-step for the first 6 weeks.** Lead UP with the less-painful leg, lead DOWN with the more-painful leg.
- **Both knees iced together** is fine when supervised; if alone or in Week 1, alternate sides 15 minutes each within a session.

### Low-back-pain specific cautions

- **Avoid long flat supine without a lumbar towel.** Use the towel for any supine session > 5 minutes.
- **No prone (face-down) lying ever during this recovery.** Substitutes are in the exercise library.
- **Log-roll for all bed transfers** (knees, hips, shoulders move together as one unit). Never twist the spine.
- **Lumbar roll for any seated session > 10 minutes.**
- **Footstool for any standing > 10 minutes** (one foot on the stool, alternate every 1 to 2 minutes).
- **No single-leg lifts (counted SLR) before Day 10.** The hip flexor pulls on the lumbar spine.
- **No double knee-to-chest stretch.** Single only, from Day 8.
- **No deep bridges.** Mini-bridge (3 to 5 cm) only, with glute-first technique, from Day 7.

**Sexual activity:** clear typically at Week 6 to 8. Supported positions only (patient supine with pillow under calves; not kneeling, not deep flexion).

**Total daily exercise:** ~60 to 75 minutes, 5 to 6 days/week (1 to 2 light days/week OK).

### MONTH 3+ — Long-term maintenance

**Forever habits:**
- **Daily walking** — aim for 30 to 45 min continuous by 3 months, 5,000 steps/day by 6 months.
- **Quad sets, SLR, hip abduction** — 2 sessions/week forever (prevents recurrence of weakness).
- **Ankle pump habit** during long sits (car, TV, weddings, religious events).
- **Core 3-in-1 brace** before every lift, transfer, or standing task (for the low back).
- **No squatting, no kneeling, no high-impact.** Forever.
- **Report any new pain, swelling, redness, warmth, or "giving way"** to surgeon immediately — lifelong vigilance.

## Section 9 — Exercise library (32 exercises, beginner-friendly with caregiver scripts)

> Read this section carefully before doing any exercise for the first time. The caregiver (Satendra) should read the exercise aloud during the first few sessions.

> **Universal rules for every exercise:**
> - Pain stays 0 to 3/10 during the movement. A 4 means slow down. A 5 means stop the exercise for the day. Sharp, stabbing, or "deep inside the joint" pain means stop immediately and tell Satendra.
> - Mild muscle tiredness and a gentle pulling feeling are normal and safe.
> - Never hold the breath. Hum, count out loud, or chat between reps.
> - For any "lying on back" exercise, a small rolled hand towel (5 cm thick) under the LOW BACK at belt-line level is allowed for her low back comfort. The towel is **never** placed under the knee.
> - Each exercise uses the same template (17 sub-headings) so the caregiver knows where to find each piece of information.

### Exercise 1 — Deep belly breathing (diaphragmatic breathing)

**Why this exercise.** During surgery the lungs were partly collapsed by the anaesthesia, and lying in bed makes the bottom of the lungs stay closed. Deep belly breathing re-opens that tissue and lowers the risk of a chest infection (pneumonia or atelectasis). The slow breath also calms the pain-alarm part of the nervous system, so her knee pain score usually drops 1 to 2 points after a 10-breath set. Because the diaphragm is tightly linked to the deep belly muscle (transverse abdominis), this single exercise also starts retraining her 30-year weak core. A 2024 RCT (Shahdadi et al., *J Chiropr Med*) showed adding diaphragmatic breathing to core exercises reduced chronic LBP and improved sleep significantly more than core exercises alone.

**Earliest safe start.** Phase 1, Day 1, first waking hour. No earlier exercise is needed before it. It is the foundation.

**Equipment needed.**
- One thin pillow for the head (about 5 to 8 cm thick).
- One small rolled hand towel (5 cm thick) for under the low back, optional.
- A small light book (about 200 g, like a paperback) for the belly-check game.
- A quiet room. TV off, phone in another room.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Lie flat on the back on the bed, face up.
2. Thin pillow under the head, not under the shoulders.
3. Small rolled towel tucked under the low back at belt-line level.
4. Both legs stretched long. Feet relaxed, toes pointing to the ceiling. Do NOT put a pillow under the knees.
5. Arms beside the body, palms facing up, shoulders melted into the bed.
6. Place her RIGHT hand flat on the belly, just below the belly button. Place her LEFT hand flat on the chest, over the breastbone.
7. Close the mouth. Take 2 normal breaths through the nose first so she settles.

**Imagine this.** Imagine the belly is a slow balloon. When you breathe IN, the balloon fills and rises. When you breathe OUT, the balloon empties and falls. The balloon is BELOW the belly button, not in the chest.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Breathe in slowly through the nose** for a count of 4 seconds (count silently "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand, three-one-thousand, four-one-thousand"). The belly rises like a slow balloon and lifts her right hand up. The chest hand should barely move.
2. **Pause at the top** for 1 second. No straining.
3. **Breathe out slowly through softly pursed lips** (like blowing gently on a hot cup of tea, not like blowing out a candle) for 6 seconds. The belly falls and the right hand comes down. Out-breath must be LONGER than in-breath; this is what calms the nervous system.
4. **Pause at the bottom** for 1 second.
5. Repeat from step 1. That is one breath. Do 10 breaths. One set takes about 2 minutes.

**Breathing.** In through nose, out through mouth. Out-breath always longer than in-breath (count 6 out, 4 in).

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* belly hand rising clearly, chest hand barely moving; shoulders relaxed; warm, heavy feeling by breath 4 or 5; face softening; maybe a bit sleepy. All GOOD.
- *Abnormal:* lightheaded or dizzy (too fast or too deep), shoulders shrugging up to the ears each breath (chest breathing), sharp pain at the front of the chest (rare; stop and rest), cough that does not stop in 30 seconds (sit up, sip water, resume later).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Belly hand rises clearly, chest hand barely moves | Chest hand moves more than belly hand (reverse breathing) |
| Shoulders relaxed, flat on bed | Shoulders lifting toward ears |
| Out-breath noticeably longer than in-breath | Both breaths the same length, rushed |
| 6 to 8 breaths take a full minute | 10 breaths done in under 30 seconds (too fast) |
| Face calm, body sinking into bed | Face tense, body rigid, breath held |

**Dosage.**
- 10 breaths per set. 1 set per session.
- 4 sessions per day: on waking, before lunch, late afternoon, at bedtime. Total 40 breaths daily.
- Hold time: 1 second top and bottom. Not more.
- Time per session: ~2 minutes.

**Progression rules.**
- Day 1 to 7: 4 sec in, 6 sec out. 10 breaths × 4 sessions.
- Week 2: lengthen out-breath to 8 sec. Still 4 sec in (classic 4-and-8 calming pattern).
- Week 4 onwards: if comfortable, add a 2-sec hold at the top. Pattern becomes 4 in, 2 hold, 8 out.
- Do NOT progress by making breaths bigger or faster; that causes dizziness. Progress only the length of the out-breath.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Chest rising more than belly* → place the 200 g paperback on her belly just below the navel; the book must rise with the in-breath.
- *Holding the breath hard at the top* → keep top pause short. Apixaban makes BP spikes risky.
- *Breathing too fast (10 in 30 sec)* → count out loud with her.
- *Shoulders lifting* → caregiver places flat hands on shoulders, gentle downward pressure.
- *Belly staying flat (held tight from postpartum habit)* → cue "let the belly be fat. Soft belly."
- *Mouth breathing on in-breath* → in through nose only (unless nose is blocked).

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Put your hand on your belly. Let me put my hand on top. Feel my hand go up and down with your breath."*
- During in-breath: *"Breathe into my hand. Make it go up."*
- If chest moves more than belly: *"Breathe LOWER. Push the belly up, not the chest."*
- If shoulders lift: *"Shoulders down. Soft shoulders."*
- If she's getting faster: *"Slow. Slow breath. Count with me — one, two, three, four."*
- At end of each breath: *"Good. Let it all out. Belly goes down."*

**You know it's working when…** after 10 breaths she reports feeling calmer or a bit sleepy; her face color looks softer/pinker (blood flow improves); her knee pain score drops by 1 to 2 points after a 10-breath set.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* fully safe, no load on either knee. Ideal first exercise of the day, even on a flare day.
- *Chronic LBP:* the small rolled towel under the low back is important. Without it she will feel back ache after 5 to 6 breaths. If she still feels back ache, gently bring BOTH knees up to a slightly bent "hook" position by sliding the heels toward the buttocks about 10 cm. Daytime resting hook-lying only. Return to legs straight when the exercise ends.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* dizziness → slow the breath. Back pain that does not ease with the towel → add a thin flat cushion (1 to 2 cm) under the knees for THIS exercise only, for the 2-minute duration only. Remove immediately after. This is a **daytime exercise exception**, never overnight (overnight knee-pillow causes flexion contracture — see Section 2 rule 1).

**When to skip this exercise today.** Almost never. The safest exercise in the whole book. Skip only if she is actively vomiting, has a fresh nose-bleed (apixaban) that has not stopped in 15 minutes, or is having active chest pain (call Max emergency).

### Exercise 2 — Ankle pumps

**Why this exercise.** When she lies still, blood pools in the calves. The calves are where most post-surgery clots form. A clot that breaks off and travels to the lung (pulmonary embolism) is the most dangerous complication of any joint surgery. Pulling the foot up and pointing it down squeezes the calf muscles, which act like a hand squeezing a tube of toothpaste and push blood upwards. Even on apixaban, clots can still form. AAOS calls this the single most important early exercise. This exercise also pumps fluid out of the swollen knees, reducing the tight, heavy feeling.

**Earliest safe start.** Phase 1, Day 1, hour 1 at home. Actually it began in the hospital recovery room. Continue relentlessly until ALL knee swelling is gone (usually around 3 months).

**Equipment needed.** The bed (or a chair if sitting up). Optional: small rolled towel under the low back.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Lie on the back (or sit in a chair with legs stretched out on a footstool).
2. Head on thin pillow.
3. Both legs long and straight, heels on the bed, toes pointing to the ceiling.
4. Optional small rolled towel under the low back.
5. Hands resting beside the body or folded on belly, whichever is comfortable.
6. Knees and thighs must stay still. Only the ankles will move.

**Imagine this.** Imagine you are pressing a car brake pedal with the ball of your foot, then releasing it to pull your toes up toward your nose. Press. Release. Press. Release. About one full pump every 4 seconds.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Pull both feet up** as if pointing the toes at her own nose. Count 2 seconds. Gentle stretch in the back of the calves.
2. **Push both feet down** as if gently pressing a brake pedal. Count 2 seconds. Calf muscles tighten.
3. **Up again,** 2 seconds.
4. **Down again,** 2 seconds.
5. Keep going. One full pump (up + down) is about 4 seconds.
6. Do 20 pumps per leg (about 1.5 minutes). Can do both feet together or one at a time.

**Breathing.** Normal nasal breathing. Hum a song if she tends to breath-hold.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* gentle stretch in back of calf when foot pulls up; gentle hardening of calf when foot points down; warmth in legs after 15 pumps; nothing at all in the knee.
- *Abnormal:* sudden sharp pain in ONE calf (not both); one calf visibly larger or warmer or redder than the other (possible DVT — stop and call doctor); cramp in foot or calf; pain in the knee (she's moving the thigh too — steady the thigh).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Whole foot moves — pulls up and pushes down | Only toes wiggle; ankle doesn't move |
| Calf visibly hardens each time foot points down | Calf stays floppy |
| Knee and thigh stay flat and still | Leg lifts off bed during the pump |
| Slow and rhythmic, ~4 sec per full pump | Fast and jerky, ~1 sec per pump |
| Breathing normally | Breath held; face red |

**Dosage.**
- 20 pumps per leg per session.
- Every 1 to 2 hours while awake (8 to 12 sessions daily in Phase 1).
- Hold time: none, this is a rhythm.
- Sessions per day: 8 to 12 first 2 weeks; 6 to 8 Weeks 3 to 4; 3 to 4 from Week 5 until all swelling gone.

**Progression rules.** This exercise does not get harder. It is maintenance, not strengthening. Continue through all 3 months of swelling. The day swelling finally disappears, drop to 50 pumps per leg once in the morning. Keep this minimum habit for 6 months after surgery.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Only the toes wiggle* → caregiver places flat hand on top of foot; she should feel the whole ANKLE moving.
- *Knee or thigh lifts with each pump* → place flat hand on front of thigh.
- *Holding the breath (especially when pointing down)* → tell her to hum.
- *Going too fast (1-sec pumps)* → fast pumps don't move as much blood. 4-sec cycle is best.
- *Skipping sessions because "the leg feels fine"* → clots form quietly. Discipline matters here.
- *Doing it only in the morning* → DVT risk is highest when lying still for 2+ hours. Do it before every rest session.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Pretend your feet are pressing a car brake. Press — release. Press — release. Only feet move."*
- If toes-only: *"Whole ankle. Move from the ankle, not just toes."*
- If knee moves: *"Knee stays still. I'll keep my hand here on your thigh — don't move the thigh."*
- If she's going fast: *"Slow. Say one-two with me. One — two. Count."*
- If she holds breath: *"Hum a song. Any song. Humming means breathing."*
- If she asks when to stop: *"20 pumps. Let's count — 1, 2, 3..."*

**You know it's working when…** the calf muscle visibly hardens with each pump (you can see it move under the skin); a gentle warmth feels in the legs after 20 pumps; swelling gradually reduces over weeks.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* totally safe; both legs work together.
- *Chronic LBP:* the small rolled low-back towel makes this very comfortable. If her back aches toward the end of a 20-count session, she can bend one knee slightly (10 cm heel-slide toward buttocks) and pump the other foot. Swap sides after 20 pumps.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* cramp in the foot → stop, massage, sip water (dehydration causes cramps, especially with Gabapin). Knee pain → check the thigh is truly still. Calf pain in one leg → STOP, compare the two calves, call doctor.

**Caregiver also tracks (once daily, morning):** measure each calf's thickness at the fattest part with a soft tape and write it down. Any difference of more than 2 cm between the two calves is a red flag — tell the surgeon.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Never skip entirely. Even on a total rest day, do at least 4 sessions of 20 pumps per leg. Non-negotiable. Only exception: fresh sharp pain in ONE calf (stop and call doctor).

### Exercise 3 — Ankle circles

**Why this exercise.** Ankle pumps only move the foot up and down. Ankle circles move the foot in all directions, so the whole ankle joint stays free and doesn't stiffen from two weeks of bed-rest. Loose ankles matter later when she starts standing and walking; a stiff ankle makes her wobble on the walker, which is a fall risk for both operated knees. Circles also add variety so her calves don't get tired of the same up-down motion, and give an extra boost to the calf-pump effect.

**Earliest safe start.** Phase 1, Day 2. Day 1 is recovery from the journey home; circles pair naturally with ankle pumps from Day 2 onwards.

**Equipment needed.** Same as ankle pumps: the bed, head pillow, optional low-back towel.

**Starting position (exact).** Same as ankle pumps: lying on the back, both legs long and straight, heels on the bed, feet relaxed. Thighs and knees must stay still, only the ankle will move.

**Imagine this.** Imagine the big toe is a pen, and you are drawing a big slow circle on the ceiling. The circle should be BIG (whole foot moves) and SLOW (one circle per ~4 seconds).

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Start with the right foot.** Big toe is the pen.
2. **Circle 1 — clockwise.** OUT (1) → DOWN (2) → IN (3) → UP (4). One circle = 4 seconds.
3. **9 more clockwise circles**, all the same direction.
4. **Reverse:** 10 anti-clockwise circles. UP (1) → IN (2) → DOWN (3) → OUT (4).
5. **Switch to left foot.** 10 clockwise, then 10 anti-clockwise.
6. Breathe normally. Do not hold the breath.

**Breathing.** Normal nasal breathing.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* gentle pulling all around the ankle — front, back, inside, outside. Soft clicks or quiet pops in the ankle are NORMAL and safe (joint fluid moving). No feeling at all in the knee. Some warmth in the foot after 10 circles is good.
- *Abnormal:* sharp stab on the inside of the ankle (stop, may be a cramp coming on); painful popping with swelling (rare; stop and tell doctor); knee pain during circles (she's moving the thigh — steady it).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Big, slow circle (one circle per ~4 sec) | Tiny fast circles (toe wiggles only) |
| Whole ankle joint moves | Only toes move |
| Clockwise first, then anti-clockwise | Random direction changes |

**Dosage.**
- 10 circles clockwise + 10 anti-clockwise, each foot.
- 3 sessions per day, often paired with ankle-pumps sessions.
- Time per session: ~3 to 4 minutes for both feet.

**Progression rules.** Not a progressive exercise. Keep circles SLOW and BIG. Small fast circles do little.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Tiny fast circles with only the toe moving* → cue "big circle, like stirring a big pot of dal."
- *The thigh rolls side to side with the circle* → keep thigh still; only the foot moves. A hand on the front of the thigh helps.
- *Changing direction randomly* → complete all 10 in one direction before reversing.
- *Going too fast and getting dizzy* → slow down and look at a fixed point on the ceiling.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Draw a big circle with your big toe. BIG circle, not small. Slow."*
- If circles are small: *"Bigger. Whole ankle moves."*
- If she gets dizzy (rare): *"Stop. Rest. Look at one spot on the ceiling."*

**You know it's working when…** the ankle feels looser; warmth in the foot.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* safe from Day 1.
- *Chronic LBP:* if foot-lifting at all during the circle makes the low back tighten, keep both heels lightly touching the bed (very small circles close to the bed). Smaller but still effective.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* cramp in the foot arch (very common in rural diet with low potassium + Gabapin) → stop, gently rub the arch, drink 150 mL water, resume with smaller circles.

**When to skip this exercise today.** If her foot cramped badly in the last session within 3 circles → skip this session, do only ankle pumps, retry circles later after she has had some water and potassium-rich food (banana, buttermilk).

### Exercise 4 — Leg (limb) elevation

**Why this exercise.** After a knee replacement, the knees collect fluid (oedema) for many weeks. When she sits or stands, gravity pulls fluid DOWN into the knees. When she lies flat, it stops going down but doesn't really leave either. Only elevation — knees higher than the heart — makes gravity work FOR her, draining fluid OUT. This reduces stiffness, throbbing, night-time ache, and morning heaviness. NHS and orthopaedic guidelines call leg elevation "non-negotiable" for the first 2 weeks. Ankles must be 15 to 30 cm above the heart.

**Earliest safe start.** Phase 1, Day 1, first afternoon home. Continue every 2 to 3 hours in Phase 1, then 2 to 3 times daily through Phase 2, then as needed whenever the knees feel tight (usually till Week 12).

**Equipment needed.**
- 4 firm pillows of similar size (about 10 cm thick each).
- 1 big rolled bath towel (~10 cm thick when rolled) for low-back support.
- 1 thin head pillow (5 to 8 cm).
- A firm bed or a long wooden bed/takhat covered with a mattress (sofa is not firm enough).
- Timer or phone (to end session after 20 to 30 min).
- Ice pack (wrapped in thin cotton cloth).

**The ONE BIG RULE — NEVER place a pillow DIRECTLY UNDER the knee.** A pillow under the knee bends the knee. Keeping the knee bent for hours/days makes the tissues behind the knee short. A short knee cannot straighten, ever. This is "flexion contracture" and is the #1 cause of permanent limping after knee surgery.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. **Build the pillow stack on the bed before she lies down.** Stack the 4 pillows at the foot end in a gentle ramp going UP toward the foot. Stack should be ~25 to 30 cm tall at its highest point. Top surface wide enough to hold BOTH calves side by side.
2. **Shape:** the pillows must support her from MID-CALF to the HEEL on both legs. The pillow stack must NOT go under the back of the knee. A small 4 to 5 cm gap between the knees and the top of the pillow is fine.
3. **Head and upper body:** lie flat with one thin pillow under the head. Place the rolled bath towel under the low back at belt-line level.
4. **Legs:** both legs stretched long. Heels and calves rest on the pillow stack. Feet relaxed, toes up. She should see her toes when she looks down.
5. **Check the angle:** her ankles should be 15 to 30 cm above her chest level. Use a measuring tape the first time so Satendra knows what the stack should look like.
6. **Arms:** beside the body, palms up, shoulders soft.

**Imagine this.** Imagine a gentle slide. Hips at the bottom (on the bed), feet at the top (in the air, above the heart). Gravity pulls the swelling DOWNWARD off the feet and knees, back toward the heart. No muscle work — just gravity doing its job.

**Step-by-step movement.** This is a REST exercise, not a movement exercise. The "movement" is building the correct position, then staying in it with small foot activities.
1. **Settle in for 1 minute.** Take 3 slow belly breaths (Ex 1).
2. **Every 3 to 5 minutes during elevation, do 10 ankle pumps** (Ex 2). Combines elevation with the calf-pump effect and drains fluid faster.
3. **Stay in position 20 to 30 minutes.** She can close her eyes, listen to the radio, or do her Kegels (Ex 16).
4. **Getting out:** do NOT jerk up. Bend one knee slowly to slide that foot off the stack. Then the other. Then log-roll onto her side, push up to sitting with the arms.

**Breathing.** Normal nasal breathing. A few belly breaths (Ex 1) at the start and end.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* within 5 minutes the throbbing in the knees eases. After 15 minutes the knees feel lighter and less hot. Some tingling in the feet from resting position is normal and goes away in 1 to 2 minutes after getting up.
- *Abnormal:* low-back ache (towel not thick enough, or elevation too high — reduce the stack height by one pillow). Tingling that doesn't go away in 5 minutes after getting up (reduce elevation time next session). Calf pain in ONE leg (stop, compare the calves, possible DVT).

**GOOD position vs BAD position.**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Pillows support calf and heel; back of knee is in air | Pillow under back of knee — flexion contracture risk |
| Ankles 15 to 30 cm above the chest | Stack only 10 cm tall — gravity doesn't help |
| Heels supported, no gap at the end of the stack | Feet hang off the end (knee bends backward) |
| Lumbar towel in place; back relaxed | Back arched; aches in 10 minutes |
| Ankle pumps every 3 to 5 minutes | Lying still for 30 minutes — no calf pump |

**Dosage.**
- Duration: 20 to 30 minutes per session.
- Sessions: Phase 1 (Week 1) — 5 sessions/day. Phase 2 (Week 2) — 3 to 4 sessions/day. Phase 3 (Weeks 3 to 6) — 2 sessions/day. Phase 4, only if knees feel tight.
- Ankle pumps during elevation: 10 pumps every 3 to 5 minutes.
- Ice pack on the knees during elevation for the first 15 minutes of each session (always wrap ice in a thin cotton cloth).
- Total daily elevation time, Week 1: ~2.5 hours across 5 sessions.

**Progression rules.** Elevation dose goes DOWN as swelling goes down, not up. Use the knee-circumference tape (mid-kneecap, same time each morning). While the tape measurement is still going down week by week, keep elevating. Once the measurement has been stable for 2 weeks, drop to 1 session per day. Keep at least one 20-min session daily until Week 12.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Pillow directly under the back of the knee* → most common and most harmful. Bends the knee. Pillow must support the calf, NOT the knee.
- *Only one pillow, stack too low* → ankles must be 15 to 30 cm above the chest.
- *Feet hanging off the end* → heels must be supported; otherwise knees bend backward (hyperextension).
- *Doing it on a soft sofa* → pillow stack collapses. Use a firm bed.
- *Forgetting the low-back towel* → flat-back supine with legs up over-arches her low back.
- *Holding still the whole time* → calf pump needs movement. Pump ankles every 3 to 5 minutes.
- *Falling asleep in this position for 2 hours* → after 45 min, back starts hurting. Cap each session at 30 min.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- Setting up: *"Lie back. I'm going to put the pillows under your thighs and calves — nothing behind your knees."*
- Checking: *"Can I pass my flat hand behind your knee? If yes, position is perfect."*
- If she complains of pins-and-needles: *"I'm moving the pillow down a bit. Tell me if that helps."*
- If she complains of low back pain: *"I'm putting a small towel under your lower back. Better?"*
- During the session: *"Pump your feet — 10 times. Let's count: 1, 2, 3..."*
- At the end: *"Slide one foot off, then the other. Roll onto your side. Push up with your arms."*

**You know it's working when…** within 5 to 10 minutes the throbbing eases; the knee feels lighter and less hot; over 1 to 2 weeks the morning knee circumference shrinks.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* both legs elevate the same way on the same stack. No "operated leg" vs "good leg" decision here.
- *Chronic LBP:* the thick low-back towel is critical. Without it her back will ache within 10 minutes and she will abandon the exercise. She may also slightly bend both knees (5 to 10 cm heel-slide toward buttocks) during elevation if the back still complains, but keep the knees higher than the heart.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* back aches → taller rolled towel at L4–L5 (her belt line). Knees ache from being fully straight for 30 minutes → add a small soft rolled washcloth (2 cm) behind each knee for the **last 10 minutes of that elevation session only** (daytime only, NEVER overnight, NEVER for more than 10 minutes per session — this is a temporary comfort measure, not a permanent position; see Section 2 rule 1). Tingling in the feet → lower the stack by one pillow.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Almost never. Skip only if she has severe low-back spasm at rest and any elevation makes it worse (rare — try with higher low-back towel first).

### Exercise 5 — Quadriceps sets (static thigh squeeze)

**Why this exercise.** The quadriceps is the big muscle on the front of the thigh. It straightens the knee. After a knee replacement, swelling physically blocks this muscle from firing — a phenomenon called "arthrogenic muscle inhibition." If the muscle stays inhibited beyond 7 days, she develops "extensor lag" (knee doesn't fully straighten even when the thigh tries) which causes a lifelong limp and early failure of the implant. Quad sets are the single most important muscle re-education exercise. AAOS recommends starting them in the recovery room within hours of surgery and doing them hourly. This is one of the first things the hospital physio had her do.

**Earliest safe start.** Phase 1, Day 1, first morning home. Already practised in hospital from the day after surgery.

**Equipment needed.**
- The bed. Thin head pillow. Optional low-back towel.
- 1 small folded hand towel (3 cm thick when folded, like a 250 g packet of biscuits) under each HEEL. Makes the exercise easier to feel. Optional on good days.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Lie on the back, face up, thin pillow under head.
2. Low-back towel in place.
3. BOTH legs long and flat on the bed. Toes to the ceiling.
4. Small folded towel under each HEEL (heels 3 cm off the bed; back of the knee presses DOWN clearly into the mattress — good feedback).
5. Arms relaxed by her sides.
6. Look at her own knees (lift the head slightly if needed).

**Imagine this.** Imagine a big paper cup placed under the back of your knee. Squash it flat by pushing the back of the knee straight DOWN into the bed. Hold it squashed.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Start with one leg at a time.** Right leg first.
2. **Tighten the front of the right thigh** as if trying to push the back of the right knee HARD DOWN into the bed. The kneecap will slide up toward her hip by a tiny amount (1 cm or so). This sliding is NORMAL and shows the quad is firing.
3. **Squeeze for 5 seconds.** Count out loud.
4. **Release slowly** for 2 seconds. Feel the thigh go soft.
5. **Rest 2 seconds** with the thigh relaxed.
6. **Repeat 10 times.** That is one set for the right leg.
7. **Switch to the left leg.** Same pattern.
8. **Breathe normally throughout.** Never hold the breath. Hum or count out loud.

**Breathing.** Steady through the nose. Cue: hum or count.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* front of the thigh hardens like a firm ball of dough. Kneecap slides up a tiny bit. Back of the knee presses firmly into the bed. Tired, warm feeling in the thigh after 8 to 10 squeezes.
- *Abnormal:* a "dead" thigh that will not tighten at all (see Common mistakes); sharp pain inside the knee (stop); kneecap doesn't move at all even after a few tries (very inhibited quad — try the feedback tricks).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Front of thigh hardens like a rock | Thigh stays soft; only buttock or heel push |
| Kneecap slides up 1 cm toward hip | Kneecap stays still |
| Back of knee presses firmly into bed | Back of knee stays bent / not pressing down |
| 5-second hold; counted out loud | Brief flex; breath held |
| Both legs done, equal effort | Only one leg done; "good leg" favored |

**Dosage.**
- 10 repetitions per set.
- 5-second hold per rep (AAOS allows 5 to 10 sec).
- 3 sets per leg per session from Day 4 onwards (start at 1 set × 5 reps on Day 1).
- 3 sessions per day.
- Rest between sets: 30 seconds.
- Total per day when fully progressed: 90 quad sets per leg.

**Progression rules.**
- Day 1 to 3: 5 reps × 1 set × 3 sessions, 5-sec hold.
- Day 4 to 7: 10 reps × 2 sets × 3 sessions, 5-sec hold.
- Week 2: 10 × 3 × 3, 5-sec hold. Add SLR ATTEMPT at end of each set ("try" to lift heel 5 cm with the knee straight; do not count reps yet).
- Week 3 onwards: 10 × 3 × 3, 10-sec hold. SLR becomes counted: 5 to 10 reps per leg.
- The test that you can drop the counted quad set: when she can do 10 SLRs per leg without any "lag" (heel lifts AND knee stays straight), the basic quad set has done its job. Keep doing quad sets for another 2 weeks just to be sure, then use them only as a warm-up.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Only pressing down with the heel, not tightening the thigh* → cue "hard thigh first, THEN push the back of the knee down."
- *Holding the breath* → hum or count.
- *Tensing the buttock and abs instead of the thigh* → put her hand on the front of the thigh, must feel like a rock.
- *Giving up after 2 bad reps* → arthrogenic inhibition means the first few reps feel impossible, then it suddenly "clicks." Try 5 reps before declaring the muscle dead.
- *Moving the whole leg (lifting it)* → that's an SLR, not a quad set. The leg stays flat on the bed.
- *Going through pain 4+/10* → reduce hold to 3 sec and reassess. If still painful, skip this session, ice, call physio.
- *Forgetting the other leg* → both knees were operated. Both quads need work. Start with the stronger leg first to learn the pattern, then the weaker one.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Place your hand on your right thigh. Push the back of the knee into the bed. Make the thigh hard like a rock."*
- If kneecap doesn't move: *"Try harder. Imagine squashing a paper cup under your knee. Squash."*
- During the hold: *"Hold. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Now release. Soft thigh."*
- If she's tense in the wrong place: *"Soft buttock. Soft belly. Only the front of the thigh works."*
- If she gets discouraged: *"This muscle is asleep. We're waking it up. Each squeeze is a poke. Keep poking."*

**You know it's working when…** the kneecap starts to move 1 cm toward the hip with each squeeze; the thigh feels firm under your hand; over Week 1 to 2, the gap between the back of the knee and the bed gradually closes; SLR becomes possible by Day 7 to 10.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* both legs are operated; there is no "good leg." Both need equal work. If one leg is clearly weaker (often the leg with the bigger pre-op deformity — in her case the right with 14° varus), give it 20 % more reps for the first 2 weeks.
- *Chronic LBP:* lying flat with legs straight is NOT ideal for her back. Use the low-back towel. If the back still aches, she can do this exercise with the non-working leg BENT at the knee (foot flat on the bed, "hook-lying") while the working leg is straight. Alternate which leg is bent.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* very painful → reduce hold to 3 sec; reduce sets to 1. Inflammatory arthritis flare → skip for 24 hours, use ice, try again with fewer reps.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip only on a complete rest day (2-2-2 rule flagged — see Section 4). Even then, 1 set of 5 reps × 1 session is fine if she wants to.

### Exercise 6 — Gluteal squeezes (buttock clench)

**Why this exercise.** The gluteus maximus (the big buttock muscle) is the second most important muscle for safe walking after the quadriceps. It extends the hip, stabilises the pelvis, and offloads the lumbar spine. Post-surgery, like the quad, it goes "to sleep" because of pain inhibition. After childbirth, her glutes are likely already deconditioned ("glute inhibition") — this is one of the four causes of her chronic LBP. Gluteal squeezes wake the muscle without moving either knee, so they are perfectly safe from Day 1 and they help her low back.

**Earliest safe start.** Phase 1, Day 1.

**Equipment needed.** Bed. Thin head pillow. Optional low-back towel.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Lie on the back, both legs long and flat on the bed.
2. Thin pillow under head.
3. Low-back towel in place.
4. Arms beside the body.
5. Knees and hips relaxed.

**Imagine this.** Imagine you are squeezing a small pencil between the two buttocks. Squeeze it tight. Hold. Let go.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Squeeze BOTH buttocks together** as if pinching that pencil between them. Feel the buttocks harden.
2. **Hold for 5 seconds.** Count out loud.
3. **Release fully** for 3 seconds. Buttocks go soft.
4. Repeat 10 times. That is one set.

**Breathing.** Steady through the nose. Hum or count out loud.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* both buttocks visibly tighten (you can see the slight lift); a firm pressure across the back of the hips; a gentle press of the back into the bed (the lumbar curve flattens slightly — good for her low back).
- *Abnormal:* sharp low-back pain (the squeeze should EASE the back, not hurt it; reduce the hold to 3 sec and check the lumbar towel position); buttocks won't tighten at all (try cueing the heel-press first: "press both heels down into the bed, then squeeze the buttocks").

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Both buttocks visibly tighten and lift slightly | One side tightens more than the other |
| 5-sec hold with steady breathing | Brief flex; breath held |
| Low back relaxes / flattens slightly into bed | Low back arches up off the bed |
| Belly stays soft | Belly tightens (she's bracing the wrong muscle) |

**Dosage.**
- 10 reps × 2 to 3 sets per session.
- 3 sessions per day.
- 5-sec hold per rep, 3-sec rest between reps.
- 30-sec rest between sets.

**Progression rules.**
- Day 1 to 3: 5 reps × 1 set × 3 sessions, 3-sec hold.
- Day 4 to 7: 10 reps × 2 sets × 3 sessions, 5-sec hold.
- Week 2 onwards: 10 × 3 × 3, 5-sec hold.
- Week 3 onwards: stays the same; the squeeze becomes a warm-up before mini-bridge and standing hip extension.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *One buttock tightens more than the other* → place a hand on each buttock; she should feel both press into your palms equally.
- *Low back arches up* → too hard a squeeze. Reduce the hold to 3 sec; cue "low back stays touching the bed."
- *Belly tightens* → caregiver places a hand on the belly; it should stay soft.
- *Holding the breath* → hum or count.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Squeeze your buttocks like you're holding a pencil between them. Tight. Hold — 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Release."*
- If one side weaker: *"More on the right. Squeeze both equally."*
- If back arches: *"Keep the low back down. Soft belly. Tight buttocks only."*

**You know it's working when…** both buttocks tighten visibly and equally; her low back feels flatter into the bed during the squeeze; over 2 weeks the squeeze becomes stronger and easier to find.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* fully safe; no knee load.
- *Chronic LBP:* this is a HELPFUL exercise for her back. Strengthening the glutes reduces low-back compensation. The squeeze itself flattens the lumbar curve, which decompresses the facet joints.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* sharp LBP during the squeeze → check the lumbar towel position (may be too thick or too thin). Reduce hold to 3 sec.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Almost never. Safe even on a flare day.

### Exercise 7 — Heel slides (active knee bending in bed)

**Why this exercise.** The single most important Day-1 exercise for restoring knee FLEXION (bending). The patient bends the knee actively by sliding the heel along the bed toward the buttock. No assistance (yet); no force. The active version is safer than passive bending in Week 1 because she controls how far it goes — she stops before pain. NHS, AAOS, and OSU all rank this as the foundational early flexion exercise. Target by Day 14: 90° both knees.

**Earliest safe start.** Phase 1, Day 1. Already done in hospital. Continue at home.

**Equipment needed.**
- The bed.
- Thin head pillow.
- Low-back towel.
- (Optional, from Day 8) a folded smooth-fabric bedsheet or plastic plate under the heel to reduce friction.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Lie on the back, both legs long and flat on the bed.
2. Thin pillow under head.
3. Low-back towel in place.
4. Arms beside the body.
5. Toes pointing to the ceiling.

**Imagine this.** Imagine the heel is dragging a thread along the bed toward the buttock. Slow drag in. Slow drag out. The thread doesn't break.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Start with the right leg.** The other leg stays straight and still.
2. **Slide the right heel slowly along the bed toward the right buttock,** bending the knee. Stop when she feels a strong stretch (NOT pain). Pain means too far.
3. **Hold for 3 seconds at the bent end.**
4. **Slide the heel slowly back to straight.** Knee fully straight at the end.
5. **Hold straight for 2 seconds.**
6. Repeat 5 to 10 times on the right leg, then switch to the left leg.

**Breathing.** Slow nasal breathing. Exhale gently as the heel slides in (away from straight); inhale as the heel slides back to straight.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* a gentle pulling at the back of the knee or in the front of the knee at the end of the bending; a mild stretch — not pain. The thigh muscle works to control the movement.
- *Abnormal:* sharp pain at the front of the knee (stop, reduce range); a "click" with pain (stop, tell doctor); the heel coming off the bed (that's a small SLR — keep the heel touching the bed).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Heel slides smoothly along the bed | Heel lifts off the bed |
| Knee bends progressively, no jerks | Sudden jerks or stops mid-slide |
| She stops at the first strong stretch | She forces past pain |
| Knee returns to fully straight at the end | Knee stays slightly bent (extension lost) |
| Both legs done equally | One leg favored |

**Dosage.**
- 5 reps × 1 set per leg on Day 1.
- Build to 10 reps × 3 sets per leg by Day 7.
- 2 to 3 sessions per day.
- 3-sec hold at the bent end, 2-sec hold straight.

**Progression rules.**
- Day 1: 5 reps × 1 set, 1 session.
- Day 2 to 3: 5 to 8 reps × 2 sets, 2 sessions.
- Day 4 to 7: 10 reps × 3 sets, 3 sessions.
- Day 8 onwards: continue at 10 × 3 × 3 + add Exercise 18 (assisted heel slide with towel) for the slowest knee.
- Week 3+: continue daily; the towel-assisted version becomes the main flexion driver.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Heel lifts off the bed* → keep the foot dragging. Use a smooth bedsheet or plastic plate under the heel from Day 8.
- *Forcing past pain* → cue "stop at the first strong stretch. Pain means too far."
- *Knee doesn't fully straighten at the end* → essential to extend fully; cue "push the back of the knee down to the bed."
- *Other leg moves too* → caregiver places a hand on the other thigh to keep it still.
- *Holding the breath* → hum or talk; exhale on the slide in.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Slide the heel slowly toward your bottom. Stop when it feels tight."*
- During the slide: *"Slow. Slow. Don't push past the pain."*
- At the bent end: *"Hold here. 1, 2, 3."*
- During the slide back: *"Now back to straight. All the way to straight. Push the knee down."*
- If the heel lifts: *"Heel stays on the bed. Drag it like a thread."*

**You know it's working when…** the heel slides closer to the buttock each week; by Day 7 she reaches ~80°; by Day 14 ~90°; by Week 4 ~110°.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* both knees get equal work. Don't let one knee rest while the other works hard; alternate sets.
- *Chronic LBP:* the bending of one hip pulls slightly on the lumbar spine. Use the low-back towel. If LBP > 3/10 during the slide, slow the speed and hold the bent end for only 1 sec.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* reduce range; do half a heel slide. From Day 8, switch to Ex 18 (towel-assisted) for the slow-bending knee.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if the knee is visibly more swollen than yesterday morning (use the daily measurement). Resume tomorrow at half the previous reps.

### Exercise 8 — Passive knee extension (heel prop)

**Why this exercise.** The single most important exercise for preventing flexion contracture. The knee must straighten fully. If it doesn't, every step she takes for the rest of her life will be a small limp, the knee will wear out faster, and the back will compensate (worsening her LBP). Passive extension uses gravity and the relaxed weight of the leg to slowly straighten the knee. The patient does NOT actively push — she relaxes. The goal is a flat, straight leg, with the back of the knee as close to the bed/firm surface as possible.

**Earliest safe start.** Phase 1, Day 2. Day 1 is recovery from the journey home; starting the heel-prop on Day 2 is safe and prevents the extension loss that otherwise starts building rapidly.

**Equipment needed.**
- A firm bed or sofa edge.
- 1 small folded towel (3 cm thick) for under the HEEL. The whole calf must be in the air; only the heel rests.
- (Optional, from Week 3) a 1 to 2 kg sandbag or rice bag for the thigh.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Lie on the back, head on thin pillow, low-back towel in place.
2. Place the small folded towel under the heel of the working leg, so the heel is 3 cm off the bed and the calf is in the air.
3. The other leg stays flat on the bed.
4. Arms relaxed by her sides.
5. Eyes can close. She RELAXES the working leg completely. Do NOT let her tense the thigh.

**Imagine this.** Imagine the leg is an empty noodle. The heel is on a small pillow. The middle of the leg slowly drops toward the bed under its own weight. Just gravity. No pushing.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Get into the starting position.** Heel on the small towel.
2. **Relax the leg fully.** No squeezing. The thigh muscle is OFF.
3. **Stay relaxed for 5 to 10 minutes.** Use this time to do Ex 1 (deep belly breathing) or Ex 16 (Kegels).
4. The back of the knee slowly drops toward the bed under gravity. By the end of the session, the gap between the back of the knee and the bed should be smaller than at the start.
5. **End:** gently slide the towel away from under the heel. Sit up via log-roll.

**Breathing.** Slow belly breathing throughout (Ex 1). The breath helps the body relax, which helps the knee straighten.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* a very mild stretch at the back of the knee or behind the calf; no pain; the leg feels heavy and tired in a good way after 10 minutes.
- *Abnormal:* sharp pain in the front of the knee (stop, the towel may be too thick); cramp in the calf or hamstring (stop, gently bend the knee, rub the cramp); back ache (towel too high, lower it).

**GOOD position vs BAD position.**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Heel only on the small towel; calf is in the air | Whole calf supported; back of knee not stretched |
| Thigh muscle relaxed (soft thigh) | Thigh muscle tensed (defeats the purpose) |
| Back of knee gently drops toward the bed over the session | No change in knee position over 10 min |
| Other leg stays flat and quiet | Other leg also propped (only one at a time) |

**Dosage.**
- Day 1: 3 min per leg × 1 session.
- Day 2: 3 min per leg × 2 sessions.
- Day 3: 5 min per leg × 2 sessions.
- Day 5 to 7: 10 min per leg × 2 sessions.
- Day 8 to 14: 10 min per leg × 3 sessions.
- Week 3 onwards: 15 min per leg × 1 to 2 sessions, optional 1 to 2 kg sandbag on the thigh (NEVER on the kneecap).

**Progression rules.**
- Time per session: 3 → 5 → 7 → 10 → 15 min.
- Optional weight from Week 3: 1 to 2 kg sandbag on the thigh, 5 to 10 cm above the kneecap. Never on the kneecap. Never on the shin.
- Stop progression once the back of the knee fully touches the bed in a relaxed leg (full extension achieved).

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Towel under the calf instead of just the heel* → corrects to: only the heel on the towel, calf in the air.
- *She tenses the thigh* → cue "soft thigh, soft leg, like a noodle. Let it drop."
- *She raises the leg up* → completely opposite to the goal. Cue "let the knee drop, not lift."
- *Doing 30 minutes in one go* → 10 minutes is the limit per session for Week 1 to 2.
- *Pillow under the back of the knee* → forbidden. Defeats the entire purpose.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- Set-up: *"Just the heel on the towel. The whole calf is in the air."*
- During: *"Soft leg. Let it drop. No squeezing. Breathe slowly."*
- Check: *"Try to slide my finger between the back of your knee and the bed. If my finger barely fits, you're winning."*
- End: *"Slowly bend the knee. Slide the towel out."*

**You know it's working when…** the gap between the back of the knee and the bed gets smaller week by week. By Day 7 the gap should be reduced. By Day 14 the back of the knee should touch the bed easily.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* do one leg at a time the first week; both legs together is fine from Day 8 (use two small towels under both heels).
- *Chronic LBP:* the low-back towel is essential. Without it, lying flat with one leg propped flares the lumbar arch. If back ache > 3/10 during the session, sit up, take a 5-min break, restart with a thicker low-back towel.
- *Apixaban window (Weeks 1 to 2):* no caregiver forcing or pushing the knee down. All pressure comes from gravity and the relaxed weight of the leg only. The patient controls everything, consistent with the anti-bleeding rules.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* sharp pain at the FRONT of the knee → reduce towel thickness from 3 cm to 2 cm. Cramp at the back of the knee or calf → bend the knee, rub the cramp, sip water, restart.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Almost never. The "extension door" is one-way. Even 3 minutes a day prevents contracture.

### Exercise 9 — Seated knee extension (long-arc quad / LAQ)

**Why this exercise.** Once she can sit out of bed for 15 minutes (Day 4), seated knee extension becomes the workhorse for restoring full knee straightening with the muscle ACTIVELY contracting through range. It is harder than the lying-down quad set because gravity pulls the leg down; she has to lift it through the last 30° to fully straight. This exercise builds the eccentric and concentric quad control needed for safe walking, stair-climbing, and sit-to-stand. From Day 4 to Week 2 we use an "assisted" version (the opposite leg helps lift). From Week 3, no assist.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 4 (assisted, opposite leg helping). True LAQ (no assist) from Day 10. Loaded LAQ (with 0.5 kg ankle weight) from Week 4.

**Equipment needed.**
- A firm sturdy chair with a seat 46 to 50 cm high, with armrests.
- A footstool that supports the working calf when the knee is bent at 90°.
- Optional from Week 4: a 500 mL water bottle taped to the ankle (≈ 500 g) for resistance.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Sit upright in the chair, back against the back-rest, lumbar roll behind L4–L5.
2. Both feet flat on the floor; knees bent at ~90°.
3. Hands on armrests for support.
4. Look forward.
5. Both legs aligned: hips, knees, ankles in a straight line (no leg twisting).

**Imagine this.** Imagine kicking a small football slowly straight ahead. The leg slowly straightens out from under the chair. Hold at the top. Lower slowly.

**Step-by-step movement (assisted version, Day 4 to Day 9).**
1. Place the LEFT foot UNDER the right shin (so the left leg gently lifts the right leg up).
2. **Slowly straighten the right knee** with the help of the left foot pushing the shin upward. Lift to as straight as comfortable (target 0° to 75° in Week 1 to 2).
3. **Hold for 3 seconds at the top** (knee as straight as it can go).
4. **Slowly lower** for 3 seconds back to 90° start position.
5. Repeat 5 to 10 times on the right leg, then switch legs.

**Step-by-step movement (true LAQ, Day 10+).**
1. Without help from the other foot, **slowly straighten the right knee** by tightening the front of the thigh.
2. **Hold for 3 seconds at the top.**
3. **Slowly lower** over 3 seconds.
4. Repeat 5 to 10 times, then switch legs.

**Breathing.** Exhale on the LIFT (the harder phase); inhale on the lower. Cue: "blow out as you straighten."

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* the front of the thigh works clearly; a stretch behind the knee at the top of the lift; mild tiredness in the thigh after 8 to 10 reps; controlled lowering, no jerks.
- *Abnormal:* sharp pain at the front of the knee (stop, reduce range); the leg "drops" at the end of the lower (quad fatigue — reduce reps); back pain (lumbar roll position).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Slow lift (~2 to 3 sec to top), 3-sec hold | Fast jerky lift |
| Knee fully straightens at the top | Knee stays slightly bent (extension lag) |
| Slow controlled lower (~3 sec) | Leg drops uncontrolled |
| Back stays against the chair, lumbar roll in place | Back arches forward; she leans back to "kick" the leg up |
| Both legs done equally | One leg favored |

**Dosage.**
- Day 4: 5 reps × 1 set per leg, assisted.
- Day 5 to 7: 6 reps × 2 sets per leg, assisted.
- Day 8 to 9: 8 reps × 2 sets per leg, assisted.
- Day 10+: 8 reps × 2 sets per leg, true LAQ.
- Week 3: 8 reps × 3 sets per leg, true LAQ, full range 0° to 90°.
- Week 4: 8 reps × 2 sets per leg with 0.5 kg ankle weight.
- Week 5 to 6: 10 reps × 3 sets with 1 kg.

**Progression rules.**
- Range first, then resistance. Get 0° to 90° before adding weight.
- Hold time at the top can extend to 5 sec from Week 3 (terminal extension hold).
- Add weight only when current dose feels easy with no lag.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Fast jerky lift* → cue "slow as a snail. Count to 3 going up."
- *Extension lag (knee stops 5 to 10° short of fully straight)* → reduce range; spend more time on quad sets first.
- *Back arches* → check the lumbar roll. Cue "back stays glued to the chair."
- *She leans back to swing the leg up* → caregiver places hand on her sternum, gentle backward pressure prevents lean-back.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Sit tall. Lumbar roll behind your back. Feet flat. Both knees at 90°."*
- During the lift (assisted): *"Use your other foot to help lift. Slow. Up to straight. 1, 2, 3."*
- At the top: *"Hold. Knee fully straight. 1, 2, 3."*
- During the lower: *"Slowly down. Don't drop. 1, 2, 3."*
- If extension lag: *"Try harder at the top. Lock the knee straight."*

**You know it's working when…** by Week 2 she can lift the leg to full straight without help; by Week 3 the lift is smooth; by Week 4 she can manage 0.5 kg weight.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* both legs are operated. Alternate legs to share the work.
- *Chronic LBP:* the lumbar roll is essential. Without it, leaning forward to lift the leg flexes the spine and flares the LBP.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* sharp pain at the front of the knee → reduce range; do half the lift only. Hamstring cramp → stop, stretch, sip water.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if seated tolerance < 10 min today (rest day). Resume tomorrow at half dose.

### Exercise 10 — Seated knee flexion (active heel-slide-under-chair)

**Why this exercise.** The active flexion partner to seated knee extension. Sitting in the chair, she slides the foot BACK under the chair (bending the knee) actively. This deepens flexion beyond what passive heel slides can achieve, because gravity helps. By Week 2, this exercise should reach 90°+; by Week 4, 110°+.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 4.

**Equipment needed.**
- A firm chair, seat 46 to 50 cm high.
- Floor surface that allows the foot to slide easily (smooth tile or a folded smooth bedsheet under the foot if the floor is rough).

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Sit upright in the chair, lumbar roll behind L4–L5.
2. Both feet flat on the floor, knees at ~90°.
3. Hands on armrests.

**Imagine this.** Imagine pulling the foot back to hide it under the chair. Slow drag back. Slow drag forward.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Start with the right foot.** The other foot stays still.
2. **Slide the right foot SLOWLY backward** under the chair, bending the knee. The heel drags or lifts slightly, the toes lead the way.
3. **Stop at the first strong stretch** (NOT pain). Hold for 3 seconds.
4. **Slide the foot forward** to the start position.
5. Repeat 5 to 10 times on the right, then switch.

**Breathing.** Exhale on the slide back (deeper bend); inhale on the slide forward.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* a stretch at the front of the knee or in the kneecap area; mild pulling at the side of the knee.
- *Abnormal:* sharp pain at the back of the knee (stop, the implant is being over-stretched); a "pop" in the knee with pain (stop, tell doctor); lower back pain (lumbar roll position, or she's leaning forward).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Slow controlled slide back | Jerky or forced movement |
| She stops at first strong stretch | Forces past pain |
| Spine stays upright, lumbar roll in place | Leans forward to "help" the bend |
| Both legs done equally | One leg favored |

**Dosage.**
- Day 4: 5 reps × 1 set per leg.
- Day 5 to 7: 8 reps × 2 sets per leg.
- Day 8 to 14: 10 reps × 2 sets per leg.
- Week 3+: 10 reps × 3 sets per leg.
- Hold at the bent end: 3 to 5 seconds.

**Progression rules.**
- Range progresses naturally as flexion improves.
- From Week 3, can add gentle "overpressure" by leaning forward at the bent end (only if LBP allows).

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Forcing past pain* → stop at the stretch; pain means too far.
- *Leaning forward to bend more* → keep the spine upright; the bend should come from the knee, not the spine.
- *Other foot moves too* → keep it planted.
- *Lumbar roll falls out* → caregiver re-positions every 5 min.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Sit tall. Slide the right foot back slowly under the chair. Stop when it's tight."*
- At the bent end: *"Hold. 1, 2, 3. Now slide forward."*
- If she leans forward: *"Sit tall. Spine straight. Only the knee bends."*
- If she forces past pain: *"Stop. Stretch is okay. Pain is not."*

**You know it's working when…** the foot slides further back each week; by Day 14 the heel is roughly under the chair (90° flexion); by Week 4 she can slide further than at pre-op.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* alternate legs.
- *Chronic LBP:* the lumbar roll is essential. Avoid leaning forward to bend deeper.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* reduce range; or do this exercise lying down (heel slide, Ex 7) for 24 hours instead.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if knee is visibly more swollen than yesterday morning. Resume tomorrow.

### Exercise 11 — Incentive spirometry

**Why this exercise.** Anaesthesia and lying still for 24 to 48 hours partially collapse the bottom of the lungs (atelectasis). Atelectasis is the seed of post-operative pneumonia. The incentive spirometer is a small plastic device with a tube and a piston that rises as you inhale slowly. It teaches her to take slow, deep, sustained breaths that re-expand the alveoli (air sacs) at the bases of the lungs. AARC, ERAS Society, and every major TKR protocol include incentive spirometry in Days 1 to 7. Inhaling slowly with a target gives sustained maximal inspiration, which is more effective than just deep breathing alone.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 1 at home (continued from hospital).

**Equipment needed.**
- The incentive spirometer (the hospital sent one home).
- A chair or semi-reclined bed. She should be sitting up at 45° to 90°, NOT lying flat.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Sit upright in a chair, OR sit up in bed at 45° to 90° (use pillows to prop up).
2. Hold the spirometer upright in front of her.
3. Lumbar roll behind L4–L5 if seated.
4. Feet flat on the floor (or footstool).

**Imagine this.** Imagine sucking a thick milkshake through a straw — but very, very slowly. Pull the air in and try to keep the piston floating at the target line as long as possible.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Exhale fully** through the mouth (empty the lungs).
2. **Place the mouthpiece** between the teeth and seal the lips around it.
3. **Inhale SLOWLY** through the spirometer. Watch the piston rise. Try to keep it at the marked target line. Inhale as deeply as possible.
4. **At the top of the inhale, HOLD the breath for 3 to 5 seconds.** This is the most important step. The hold is what re-expands the alveoli.
5. **Remove the mouthpiece. Exhale gently** through the mouth (lips pursed).
6. **Rest for 30 seconds.** Breathe normally.
7. Repeat for 10 sustained breaths.

**Breathing.** This IS the breathing exercise. Slow, deep, sustained inhale. Hold. Slow exhale.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* a feeling of the lungs expanding; a slight stretch at the bottom of the chest; mild lightheadedness if she does too many in a row (rest 30 sec between breaths); a small productive cough is expected and good (clears secretions).
- *Abnormal:* dizziness that doesn't ease in 30 sec (slow down); sharp chest pain (stop, call doctor); persistent cough that won't settle in 1 minute (sit up taller, sip water).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Slow inhale, piston rises smoothly to target, 3 to 5-sec hold | Fast inhale, piston shoots up and crashes down |
| Lips sealed around mouthpiece | Air leaks around the lips |
| Sitting upright at 45°+ | Lying flat (defeats the purpose) |
| 30-sec rest between breaths | Back-to-back breaths → dizziness |

**Dosage.**
- 10 sustained breaths per session.
- Every 1 to 2 hours while awake for the first 7 to 10 days.
- 30-second rest between breaths.

**Progression rules.**
- Aim for the target line (or higher) by Day 3.
- After Day 7 to 10, taper: 10 breaths × 4 sessions per day.
- Stop entirely after Day 14 if she has no cough, no chest tightness, and lungs sound clear (caregiver listens with ear on her back).

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Lying flat to do it* → sit up at 45° minimum. Lying defeats the purpose.
- *Fast inhale (piston shoots up and crashes down)* → cue "slow as a snail. Hold the piston up."
- *No hold at the top* → the hold is the most important part. 3 to 5 sec.
- *Air leaks around the lips* → check the seal.
- *Doing 10 in a row without rest* → causes dizziness. 30 sec between each.
- *Skipping sessions because "I feel fine"* → atelectasis is silent. Discipline matters.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Sit up. Empty your lungs first. Now slowly suck the air in. Watch the piston."*
- During the inhale: *"Slow. Keep going. Higher. Get to the line."*
- At the top: *"Hold. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Now breathe out gently."*
- Between breaths: *"Rest 30 seconds. Breathe normally."*
- If dizzy: *"Slow down. Wait 1 minute before the next breath."*

**You know it's working when…** the piston reaches the target line easily by Day 3 to 5; she has no cough or wheeze; lungs sound clear when caregiver listens with ear pressed to her back.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* fully safe; no knee load.
- *Chronic LBP:* sitting upright at 90° with lumbar roll is back-friendly. If she can only manage 45° propped in bed, that's still effective.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* dizziness → slow down, longer rest. Productive cough that doesn't settle → see Ex 32 (chest physiotherapy).

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip a session if she vomits or has acute chest pain. Otherwise non-negotiable for first 10 days.

### Exercise 12 — Walker walking (step sequence and posture)

**Why this exercise.** Walking is the goal of the entire recovery. Walker walking is the safest way to walk in the first 4 to 6 weeks for bilateral. The walker takes weight off the knees and provides 4 stable points of support. Wrong walker technique is a major cause of falls; right technique is easy to learn but must be practised. AAOS, AAHKS, and HSS all recommend walker for the first 4 to 6 weeks for bilateral TKR.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 1 (transfers only — bedroom to bathroom). Build to longer walks gradually.

**Equipment needed.**
- The walker (rubber-tipped feet, no wheels for first 4 weeks). Height adjusted so the handles are at her hip-bone level (greater trochanter) when she stands tall.
- Closed-back, rubber-soled shoes (no flip-flops, no slip-ons).
- Lumbar roll in her gait (can be skipped during walking).
- Husband walking BEHIND her at hip height with a hand near her belt (ready to catch).

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Stand inside the walker frame. Both feet inside (not outside).
2. Stand tall: shoulders back, head up, looking forward (NOT down at the feet).
3. Hands on the walker grips, elbows slightly bent (10 to 15°).
4. Weight balanced through both feet, NOT pushing forward into the walker.

**Imagine this.** Imagine the walker is a moving frame that you push forward, then walk into. Walker forward → step in → walker forward → step in. Slow, rhythmic.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Walker forward** about 25 to 30 cm (one foot's length) ahead.
2. **Step forward with the LESS-PAINFUL leg** first (her right or left, whichever feels stronger today).
3. **Step forward with the OTHER leg** to meet the first.
4. Both feet inside the walker frame again. Pause for 1 second.
5. **Walker forward again.** Repeat.

**Breathing.** Normal nasal breathing. Avoid breath-holding (apixaban). Hum if needed.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* a controlled rhythm; weight equally on both feet at the end of each step; mild tiredness in the thighs after 2 to 3 minutes.
- *Abnormal:* sharp pain in either knee (stop, sit, call); a feeling of being unbalanced (sit immediately); chest tightness (stop, breathe).

**GOOD walking vs BAD walking.**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Stand tall, head up, looking forward | Hunched over, looking at feet |
| Push down on walker grips (don't pull up) | Hangs body weight on the walker |
| Walker forward 25 to 30 cm — small step | Walker forward 50 cm or more — too far |
| Both feet land inside the frame | Steps outside the frame (fall risk) |
| Equal weight on both feet | Favors one leg (no good leg in bilateral) |
| Walker rubber tips planted before stepping | Walker still moving when she steps |
| Husband walking behind, near her belt | Husband far away, not watching |

**Dosage.**
- Day 1: bathroom and back only. 2 to 3 short trips. ~100 to 150 steps total.
- Day 2 to 3: 3 to 5 short trips, ~200 to 300 steps total.
- Day 4 to 7: 4 to 5 trips, one 3-min continuous walk by Day 7.
- Day 8 to 14: 6 to 8 indoor trips, one 5- to 10-min continuous walk.
- Week 3: 4 walks × 5 to 8 min indoors.
- Week 4: 4 walks × 10 to 15 min, possible walker → two-sticks transition late in the week.
- Week 5 to 6: outdoor walks 10 to 30 min on smooth concrete with caregiver.

**Progression rules.**
- Time first, then distance, then surface (smooth → uneven), then aid (walker → two sticks → cane).
- No surface change without first being confident on smoother surface.
- Walker → two sticks ONLY if all criteria met (Section 5 Phase 3).

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Looking down at her feet* → cue "head up, look forward, like driving." Place a small bright object at eye level across the room.
- *Hanging weight on the walker* → cue "push DOWN on the walker, don't hang." Caregiver gently presses her shoulder up to remind.
- *Stepping outside the walker frame* → cue "both feet inside the frame at every stop."
- *Walker too far ahead* → 25 to 30 cm is the right distance; further = fall risk.
- *Walking too fast* → cue "small steps. Slow."
- *Pulling up on the walker to stand from sit* → forbidden. Push armrests of the chair, never pull walker. The walker can tip.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- Before walking: *"Stand tall. Both feet inside the walker. Look at me, not at your feet."*
- During: *"Walker forward. Stronger leg first. Other leg meets. Stop. Walker forward again."*
- If she leans on the walker: *"Stand up straight. Don't hang on the walker. Push down."*
- If she's looking down: *"Head up. Look at the picture on the wall."*
- Stopping: *"Walker forward, then both feet meet. Pause."*
- Sitting down: *"Back up to the chair. Feel the chair with the back of your knees. Both hands to the armrests. Sit."*

**You know it's working when…** by Day 7 she walks 3 minutes continuously without rest; by Day 14, 5 to 10 minutes; by Week 3, indoor walks of 8 to 10 min; by Week 4, walker → two sticks trial.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* both knees are equal. Don't favor one. If one is more painful, walk slowly but step with both equally.
- *Chronic LBP:* keep the head up and stand tall. Don't hunch (that flares the back). Use the lumbar roll when sitting between walks.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* one knee weak today → use a partial-weight-bearing pattern: walker bears more weight on her hands, less on the knees. Pain > 4/10 → stop, sit, ice.

**When to skip walks today.** Skip indoor walks if a 2-2-2 rule criterion is flagged (full rest day). Bathroom transfers always continue (use commode if needed). Skip outdoor walks if rain/wet surfaces.

### Exercise 13 — Sit-to-stand (from an armchair, bilateral TKR version)

**Why this exercise.** Sit-to-stand is the most common functional movement (toilet, chair, bed). For bilateral TKR, it is also the highest-risk transfer because both knees take her body weight at the same time. The wrong technique (pulling on the walker, leaning forward without armrest push) reliably causes falls. The right technique uses ARMRESTS to push, walker for balance only, and a tall chair (≥ 46 cm) to reduce knee bend. Practised daily until automatic.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 3 (formal technique drill); transfers happen from Day 1.

**Equipment needed.**
- A firm armchair, seat 46 to 50 cm high, with armrests. Add a cushion if too low.
- Walker positioned just outside the chair, in front.
- Closed-back rubber-soled shoes.
- Husband nearby (in front, ready to catch shoulders).

**Starting position (exact, sitting).**
1. Sit upright in the chair, back against the back-rest, lumbar roll in place.
2. Both feet flat on the floor, slightly behind her knees (not far forward).
3. Hands resting on the armrests.
4. Walker positioned just outside the chair, in front, within easy reach.

**Imagine this.** Imagine standing up by pushing the floor away from you with your hands on the armrests. The walker is just for balance after you stand — not for pulling up.

**Step-by-step movement (sit to stand).**
1. **Scoot forward** to the front edge of the chair (small bottom-shuffles).
2. **Place feet flat, slightly tucked back** (heels under knees or just behind).
3. **Lean the chest forward** ("nose over toes") — keep the spine straight, no rounded back. Lumbar roll stays in place.
4. **Hands on the armrests, palms down.**
5. **Push HARD with both arms on the armrests** as you stand. Both legs straighten together.
6. **Stand fully upright** before moving the hands to the walker.
7. **Now move both hands to the walker** for balance.

**Step-by-step movement (stand to sit).**
1. **Back up** to the chair until the back of both calves touches the chair.
2. **Reach back with one hand at a time** to the armrests (NOT to the walker).
3. **Both hands on the armrests.**
4. **Lean forward slightly** ("nose over toes") to control the descent.
5. **Lower SLOWLY** (3 sec) into the seat, hands controlling the descent.
6. **Sit, then slide back** to the back of the chair.

**Breathing.** Exhale as you stand (the hard phase); inhale as you sit. Cue: "blow out as you stand."

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* a hard quad squeeze at the start of the stand; the arms doing significant work; arrival at standing without dizziness.
- *Abnormal:* dizziness on standing (sit back down, sip water, try again in 1 min — orthostatic hypotension); knee buckle (sit immediately, call physio); sharp pain in one knee (stop, ice, lower dose tomorrow).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Hands on ARMRESTS to push up | Hands on WALKER to pull up (walker tips!) |
| "Nose over toes" lean before pushing | Sits straight up without leaning forward |
| Both legs straighten equally | One leg leads, the other lags (favors a side) |
| Stands fully upright before reaching walker | Reaches walker mid-stand (loses balance) |
| Slow controlled descent (3 sec) | Drops into the chair |

**Dosage.**
- Day 3: 3 practice reps × 1 set, with caregiver coaching.
- Day 4 to 7: 5 reps × 1 set per session, naturally during the day's transfers.
- Day 8 to 14: 5 to 10 reps per session, 2 to 3 sessions/day.
- Week 3+: just integrate into normal daily life (target 20+ sit-to-stands per day naturally).

**Progression rules.**
- Same chair until the stand is reliable.
- Lower chair (44 cm, 42 cm) only after standing from the high chair feels effortless and the SLR has no lag.
- Without using arms (chair only, no armrests) only from Week 5+ if quad strength permits.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Pulling on the walker* → caregiver places her hands on the armrests every time. "Armrests, never walker."
- *Sitting straight up without "nose over toes"* → cue "lean forward first. Nose over toes. THEN push."
- *One leg straightens before the other (favoring)* → cue "both legs equally. Both knees straighten together."
- *Drops into the chair* → cue "slow down. 3 seconds to sit."
- *Reaches walker before standing fully* → cue "stand all the way up first. Then walker."

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Scoot forward. Feet behind the knees. Hands on the armrests."*
- Before standing: *"Lean forward. Nose over toes."*
- During the stand: *"Push! Both arms push! Stand all the way up!"*
- Once standing: *"Now move your hands to the walker. Balance."*
- Sitting down: *"Back up to the chair. Feel the chair with the back of your knees. Hands to the armrests, not the walker. Lean forward. Slow down."*

**You know it's working when…** by Day 7 she stands without help; by Day 14 she stands from any chair ≥ 46 cm without dizziness; by Week 3 she stands from a normal 18-inch chair without using arms (sometimes); by Week 6 from any chair without arms.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* CRITICAL. Never pull on the walker. The walker is a 4-legged stool, not a hoist. It tips forward when you pull up on it. Always armrests.
- *Chronic LBP:* "nose over toes" is fine; the spine stays straight. Lumbar roll stays in the chair so she returns to a neutral spine when sitting back down.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* very weak today → caregiver uses a transfer belt (gait belt) around her waist, holds the back of the belt to assist the stand. Dizziness → wait 30 sec at standing before walking.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Never skip — she has to transfer multiple times a day. On a flare day, do it more carefully and slowly, but still do it.

### Exercise 14 — Pelvic tilts (posterior pelvic tilt)

**Why this exercise.** Pelvic tilts are the gateway to deep-core activation. They flatten the natural lumbar curve into the bed, decompressing the facet joints (one of her four LBP causes). They are also the bridge to the abdominal 3-in-1 brace (Ex 15). Williams flexion exercises (1955) showed pelvic tilts reduce chronic LBP through facet decompression. For her, pelvic tilts are upgraded to 3 to 4 sessions per day.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 2.

**Equipment needed.**
- The bed.
- Thin head pillow.
- Optional low-back towel (REMOVED for this exercise — she needs to feel the back press into the bed without padding).

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Lie on the back, head on thin pillow.
2. Both knees bent, feet flat on the bed, knees and feet hip-width apart. This is the "hook-lying" position.
3. Arms beside the body.
4. Low-back towel REMOVED (you want to feel the back press into the bed).
5. Notice the natural arch of the lower back — there is some space between the back and the bed.

**Imagine this.** Imagine your lower back is a flat hand pressing INTO the mattress to hide. Squeeze the buttocks slightly, tuck the tailbone forward, and the lower back flattens onto the bed. Hold. Release.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Take a slow belly breath in** (Ex 1).
2. **As you breathe out**, gently tilt the pelvis: imagine pulling the belt-buckle UP toward the chin and the tailbone DOWN toward the heels. The lumbar curve flattens; the lower back presses into the bed.
3. **Hold the tilt for 3 to 5 seconds.** Continue breathing normally.
4. **Release SLOWLY** as you breathe in. The natural arch returns.
5. Rest for 2 seconds.
6. Repeat 10 times. That is one set.

**Breathing.** Exhale on the tilt (the work); inhale on the release.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* the lower back presses gently into the bed; a mild squeeze in the lower belly and the buttocks; relaxed shoulders; no strain anywhere.
- *Abnormal:* sharp low-back pain (stop, the tilt is too forceful — reduce); cramp in the belly (over-bracing — relax); knee pain (the bent-knee position is too deep — straighten the knees a bit).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Lower back presses gently into the bed | No change — back stays arched |
| Tailbone tucks toward heels | Hips lift off the bed (that's a bridge, not a tilt) |
| Belly stays soft, slight tightening only | Belly bulges out (Valsalva), or sucks in hard |
| Breath flows steadily | Breath held |

**Dosage.**
- Day 2: 5 reps × 2 sets, twice today.
- Day 3: 10 reps × 2 sets, twice today.
- Day 4 to 7: 10 reps × 2 sets, 3 sessions per day.
- Week 2 onwards: 10 reps × 3 sets, 3 to 4 sessions per day.

**Progression rules.**
- Hold time: 3 sec → 5 sec → 8 sec by Week 3.
- Combine with deep belly breathing from Day 4 (one slow breath per tilt).
- Combine with Kegels from Day 4 (squeeze pelvic floor as you tilt).
- This becomes the foundation for the 3-in-1 brace (Ex 15).

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Lifting the hips off the bed* → that's a bridge. Cue "hips stay DOWN. Only the tailbone tucks."
- *Breath held* → exhale on the tilt; cue "blow out as you tuck."
- *Over-bracing the belly* → cue "soft belly. The squeeze is in the lower belly only."
- *No change in the back position* → place a hand under her low back; she should feel her back press into your hand.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Knees bent. Feet flat. Notice the gap under your low back."*
- During the tilt: *"Press your low back into the bed. Tuck your tailbone."*
- At the hold: *"Hold. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Now release."*
- If hips lift: *"Hips stay on the bed. Only the tailbone tucks."*
- Combine with breath: *"Breathe in. Now breathe out — and tilt. Press the low back down."*

**You know it's working when…** her low back gradually feels less stiff in the morning; the morning LBP score drops by 1 to 2 points after a 10-rep set; by Week 3 the tilt is automatic and easy.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* fully safe; no knee load (knees are bent in hook-lying, no bend deeper than 90°). Both knees stay equal.
- *Chronic LBP:* this is one of her primary LBP exercises. Williams flexion gold-standard.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* if hook-lying with both knees bent is uncomfortable, do it with one knee bent and the other straight; alternate. If tilting too hard hurts the back, reduce the hold to 3 sec and the squeeze depth to "just barely flat."

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip only if she cannot tolerate the hook-lying position (rare). Otherwise do every day.

### Exercise 15 — Abdominal 3-in-1 brace (TrA + diaphragm + Kegel)

**Why this exercise.** The single best evidence-based exercise for postpartum chronic LBP. It combines three deep-core actions: drawing in the lower belly (transverse abdominis activation), diaphragmatic breathing, and pelvic-floor squeeze (Kegel). Together they re-build the deep "corset" that childbirth disrupted. Meta-analyses show 8 to 12 weeks of this combined work increases multifidus thickness on MRI and reduces chronic LBP by 4+ points. It is also a safer transfer brace — a co-contracted core protects the spine during sit-to-stand.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 3.

**Equipment needed.**
- The bed.
- Thin head pillow.
- Optional small rolled towel under the low back.
- A small light book (~200 g) for the belly-check.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Lie on the back, head on thin pillow.
2. Both knees bent in hook-lying (feet flat, knees and feet hip-width apart).
3. Low-back towel optional.
4. Place one hand FLAT on the lower belly (just below the belly button). The other hand on the chest (over the breastbone).

**Imagine this.** Imagine three actions happening at once: belly button GENTLY pulled toward the spine (10 % effort, NOT a hard suck), Kegel squeeze (lift up internally, like stopping the flow of urine), and slow nasal exhale. All three together, gently, for 5 to 10 seconds.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Settle in.** 1 minute of normal nasal breathing.
2. **Inhale slowly** through the nose (4 sec), letting the belly rise.
3. **Begin the exhale slowly** through pursed lips. As you exhale:
   - **Action 1 — TrA draw-in:** GENTLY pull the lower belly toward the spine (10 %, not a hard suck). The hand on the lower belly should feel a slight inward movement.
   - **Action 2 — Kegel:** lift the pelvic floor up and in (like stopping urine mid-flow).
   - **Action 3 — Diaphragm:** continue the slow exhale.
4. **Hold the brace for 5 to 10 seconds.** Continue breathing normally during the hold.
5. **Release SLOWLY.** All three actions relax together.
6. Rest for 5 seconds.
7. Repeat 5 to 10 times. One set.

**Breathing.** This IS a breathing exercise. The hard part is breathing normally WHILE holding the brace. Many people hold the breath when they brace; that defeats the purpose. Cue: "breathe normally during the hold."

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* the lower belly gently flattens (10 %, not a hard suck); the pelvic floor lifts; a gentle "compression" all around the lower trunk; the upper belly and shoulders stay relaxed.
- *Abnormal:* the upper belly puffs out (she's bracing the wrong muscle — the rectus abdominis); shoulders shrug up (chest breathing); back arches up off the bed (over-bracing).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Lower belly draws in 10 %; soft belly above | Hard suck-in or upper-belly bulge |
| Pelvic floor lifts internally | Buttocks squeeze instead |
| Breathes normally during the hold | Breath held |
| Held 5 to 10 sec at a time | 1-sec quick squeezes |
| Spine stays neutral; lumbar roll engaged | Back arches or flattens forcefully |

**Dosage.**
- Day 3: 5 reps × 1 set, 5-sec hold, once today (learn).
- Day 4 to 7: 10 reps × 1 set, 5-sec hold, once today.
- Week 2: 10 reps × 1 set, 5- to 8-sec hold, twice daily.
- Week 3+: 10 reps × 2 sets, 8- to 10-sec hold, twice daily; PLUS use as a transfer-brace before every sit-to-stand and bed transfer.

**Progression rules.**
- Hold time: 5 sec → 8 sec → 10 sec.
- Add to functional movements: brace before lifting anything, before sit-to-stand, before walking.
- Ultimate progression: she can hold the brace automatically during all daily activities (Week 6+).

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Hard belly suck instead of gentle draw-in* → 10 % effort. Cue "barely feel it. Gentle."
- *Holding the breath during the brace* → cue "breathe normally. Talk to me while you hold."
- *Squeezing buttocks instead of pelvic floor* → "internal lift, not external squeeze."
- *Upper belly bulges out* → wrong muscle (the rectus). Cue "lower belly only. Below the belly button."

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Three things at once, gently. Belly draws in 10 %. Pelvic floor lifts. Breathe out slowly."*
- During the hold: *"Hold. Keep breathing. Talk to me. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5."*
- If she puffs the upper belly: *"Lower belly only. Below the belly button. Soft above."*
- If she holds the breath: *"Breathe. Say a word."*

**You know it's working when…** by Week 2 she can hold the brace for 8 sec while talking; by Week 4 the LBP morning score drops by 2 to 3 points; by Week 6 the brace is automatic during transfers.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* fully safe; no knee load.
- *Chronic LBP:* this is the gold-standard exercise for her specifically. Strongest evidence.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* if she gets dizzy from the breath-hold, slow down. If the brace causes any LBP, reduce the depth (less draw-in).

**When to skip this exercise today.** Almost never. Safe even on a flare day; reduce reps to 5 if very tired.

### Exercise 16 — Kegels (pelvic floor contraction)

**Why this exercise.** Pelvic-floor training has the single largest effect size of any exercise for postpartum chronic LBP (Bi 2022 meta-analysis). Her 30-year LBP almost certainly has a pelvic-floor weakness component. Kegels are also the only exercise on this list that improves urinary continence, which matters for her: night-time toilet trips on apixaban + Gabapin-NT are a fall risk. Stronger pelvic floor = fewer night trips = fewer falls. Safe from any position. No knee load. Can be done invisibly during walker walks, in the chair, in bed.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 3.

**Equipment needed.** None.

**Starting position (exact).** Any position. Easiest to learn lying down (hook-lying, knees bent), then transfer the skill to seated and standing.

**Imagine this.** Imagine you are stopping the flow of urine mid-stream. Lift the muscles UP and IN. Hold. Release. There is no visible movement — it is all internal.

**Step-by-step movement (slow Kegel).**
1. **Inhale slowly** through the nose.
2. **Exhale slowly. As you exhale, squeeze and lift the pelvic-floor muscles** (the muscles that stop urine flow). Imagine an internal elevator going UP.
3. **Hold for 5 seconds.** Continue breathing normally.
4. **Release SLOWLY** over 2 seconds. The "elevator" comes back DOWN.
5. **Rest for 3 seconds.**
6. Repeat 10 times. One set.

**Step-by-step movement (fast Kegel — from Week 2).**
1. **Quickly squeeze and lift** for 1 second.
2. **Quickly release** for 1 second.
3. Repeat 10 times in a row. One set.

**Breathing.** Slow nasal. Exhale on the squeeze.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* an internal lift in the pelvis; no visible movement outside; no buttock or thigh muscle squeeze; a gentle relaxation when releasing.
- *Abnormal:* sharp pelvic pain (stop, may indicate prolapse — tell doctor); the buttocks squeeze too (you're using the wrong muscle); breath held.

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Internal lift only — no visible movement | Buttocks squeeze; thighs squeeze |
| Breathes normally during the hold | Breath held |
| Releases fully between reps | Stays partially squeezed |
| 5-sec hold (slow) or 1-sec (fast) consistently | Variable hold times |

**Dosage.**
- Day 3: 10 slow Kegels (5-sec hold) × 2 sessions per day.
- Day 4 onwards: 10 slow + 10 fast Kegels × 2 sessions per day.
- Week 3+: 15 slow + 15 fast Kegels × 2 sessions per day.

**Progression rules.**
- Hold time (slow Kegels): 5 sec → 8 sec → 10 sec by Week 4.
- Number of fast Kegels: 10 → 15 → 20 by Week 4.
- Position progression: lying → seated → standing → during transfers.
- Final progression: integrated into the 3-in-1 brace (Ex 15).

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Squeezing the buttocks too* → place her hand on a buttock; it should NOT tighten during a Kegel.
- *Squeezing the thighs* → thighs should stay relaxed.
- *Holding the breath* → breathe normally during the hold.
- *Not releasing fully* → "completely relax between reps."

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- This is a private exercise. Caregiver support is verbal only, not physical.
- At the start: *"Imagine stopping the flow of urine. Lift up inside. Hold. Release."*
- If she's struggling: *"Try it next time you go to the bathroom — stop the flow for 1 second, then continue. That's the muscle."*
- During the hold: *"Hold. Keep breathing. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Release."*

**You know it's working when…** night-time bathroom trips reduce; the LBP morning score drops; she can hold a 10-sec Kegel by Week 4.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* fully safe; no knee load.
- *Chronic LBP:* highest-evidence exercise for her specifically.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* sharp pelvic pain may indicate prolapse — tell doctor. Otherwise no modifications needed.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Never. Can be done invisibly during walker walks or while sitting in the chair watching TV. Make it a habit, not a chore.

### Exercise 17 — Hamstring sets (isometric "dig heel down")

**Why this exercise.** The hamstrings are the muscles at the back of the thigh that bend the knee. Like the quadriceps, they go partially "to sleep" after surgery. Activating them isometrically (without moving the joint) is safe and balances the front-of-thigh work. Strong hamstrings reduce the risk of knee buckling and protect against compensation patterns. From Day 3, they are part of the "wake up" routine for the leg.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 3.

**Equipment needed.**
- The bed.
- Thin head pillow.
- Optional small rolled towel under the low back.
- A small folded hand towel (3 cm thick) under each HEEL for feedback.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Lie on the back, head on thin pillow.
2. Both legs long and straight, low-back towel optional.
3. Small folded towel under each HEEL.
4. Arms beside the body.
5. Toes pointing to the ceiling.

**Imagine this.** Imagine you are pressing the heel down INTO the bed (or the small towel) without moving the leg. The back of the thigh tightens.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Start with the right leg.**
2. **Press the right heel firmly DOWN into the towel/bed** as if you are trying to push the bed away with the heel. The back of the thigh tightens.
3. **Hold for 5 seconds.** Count out loud.
4. **Release slowly** for 2 seconds.
5. Repeat 5 to 10 times on the right, then switch legs.

**Breathing.** Steady through the nose. Hum or count.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* the back of the thigh hardens (you can feel it with your hand under her hamstring); the heel digs into the towel; no knee bending; no buttock squeeze.
- *Abnormal:* sharp pain at the back of the knee (stop, may indicate hamstring injury or implant problem); cramp in the hamstring (stop, gently bend the knee, sip water).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Back of thigh tightens (palpable) | Back of thigh stays soft |
| Heel digs into the towel | Knee lifts off the bed |
| No knee bending | Knee bends (becomes a heel slide) |
| 5-sec hold; counted out loud | Brief flex |

**Dosage.**
- Day 3: 5 reps × 1 set per leg, once today.
- Day 4 to 7: 5 reps × 2 sets per leg, once today.
- Day 8 to 14: 8 reps × 2 sets per leg, once daily.
- Week 3+: 10 reps × 2 sets per leg, once daily.

**Progression rules.**
- Hold time: 5 sec → 8 sec → 10 sec by Week 3.
- The exercise stays isometric until Week 4, then progresses to standing hamstring curl (separate exercise added in Week 4).

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Knee lifts off the bed* → that's a heel slide, not a hamstring set. Cue "knee stays still. Only the heel pushes down."
- *No tightening at the back of the thigh* → place a hand under her hamstring; she should feel it firm up.
- *Buttock squeezes too* → some buttock activation is OK; if it's the only thing happening, focus harder on the back of the thigh.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Press your heel down hard into the towel. Make the back of the thigh tight."*
- During the hold: *"Hold. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Release."*
- If knee lifts: *"Knee stays on the bed. Only the heel digs down."*

**You know it's working when…** the back of the thigh tightens visibly under your hand; over 2 weeks, the squeeze becomes stronger.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* alternate legs.
- *Chronic LBP:* this exercise is back-friendly. Tight hamstrings worsen LBP; activating them carefully helps.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* sharp pain at the back of the knee → reduce the press intensity. Cramp → stop, bend the knee, sip water.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip on a flare day. Restart at half the previous reps the next day.

### Exercise 18 — Assisted heel slide (with towel)

**Why this exercise.** The active heel slide (Ex 7) is the foundation, but from Day 8 onwards we may need a little extra range. The assisted heel slide uses a towel looped around the foot to gently pull the heel toward the buttock — but the patient still controls the movement. Force is never used. This is the substitute for "passive ROM" stretching that the apixaban window forbids. Freise et al. (KSSTA 2020) showed active heel slide with towel-assist is more effective than CPM machines for early TKA flexion.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 8.

**Equipment needed.**
- The bed.
- Thin head pillow.
- Optional low-back towel.
- A long bath towel (~150 cm).

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Lie on the back, both legs long and straight.
2. Thin pillow under head, low-back towel in place.
3. Loop the bath towel around the BALL of the foot (forefoot), holding both ends in her hands.
4. The other leg stays straight and still.

**Imagine this.** Imagine the towel is a soft rope that helps the foot drift back to the buttock. She pulls gently — never hard. The pull stops the moment she feels a strong stretch.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Loop the towel around the ball of the right foot.**
2. **Slide the right heel slowly back along the bed** toward the right buttock, bending the knee.
3. **Use the towel to gently assist the movement** — pull just enough to deepen the bend by 5° to 10° beyond what she could do alone.
4. **Stop at the first strong stretch** (NOT pain).
5. **Hold for 10 seconds at the bent end.**
6. **Slowly slide the heel back to straight,** still holding the towel for control.
7. Repeat 5 to 10 times, then switch legs.

**Breathing.** Exhale on the slide back; inhale on the slide forward.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* the same kind of stretch as a regular heel slide, plus a bit more depth at the end; mild pulling at the front of the knee.
- *Abnormal:* sharp pain at the back of the knee (stop, the pull is too strong); pain that lingers after the rep (reduce the pull next time).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Towel pulls gently — patient controls force | Caregiver pulls forcefully (forbidden in apixaban window) |
| Stops at first strong stretch | Forces past pain |
| 10-sec hold at the bent end | Bounces in and out |
| Heel slides smoothly | Heel lifts off the bed |

**Dosage.**
- Day 8 to 14: 5 reps × 1 set per leg, once daily, on the slowest-bending knee.
- Week 3+: 8 reps × 2 sets per leg, once daily, both knees.
- Hold at bent end: 10 sec.

**Progression rules.**
- Hold time can extend to 20 sec by Week 4.
- Range progresses naturally as flexion improves.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Caregiver pulls hard* → forbidden in Weeks 1 to 2 (apixaban window). Patient controls force always.
- *Forcing past pain* → stop at stretch.
- *Heel lifts off the bed* → keep the heel dragging.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Loop the towel around your foot. You hold both ends. You pull, gently."*
- During the slide: *"Slow. Stop when it's tight."*
- At the hold: *"Hold. 10 seconds. Breathe."*
- *"Caregiver does not pull. You control everything."*

**You know it's working when…** flexion improves by 2° to 5° per week.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* alternate legs.
- *Chronic LBP:* keep the other leg straight and the back relaxed.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* reduce the pull; do regular heel slides instead.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip on a flare day; do active heel slides only.

### Exercise 19 — Single knee-to-chest stretch

**Why this exercise.** Classic Williams flexion exercise — gold standard for chronic LBP. Brings one knee toward the chest to stretch the lumbar erector spinae and decompress the lumbar facet joints. For her, it doubles as a gentle gravity-assisted knee flexion exercise. From Day 8 (when flexion is ≥ 80°), it becomes one of the best double-benefit exercises. Important: SINGLE knee only, never both knees together (forbidden in this plan because it forces full bilateral knee flexion the implants can't tolerate).

**Earliest safe start.** Day 8.

**Equipment needed.**
- The bed.
- Thin head pillow.
- Low-back towel REMOVED (the stretch flattens the back; no towel needed).

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Lie on the back, both knees bent in hook-lying (feet flat).
2. Thin pillow under head.
3. Low-back towel REMOVED for this exercise.
4. Arms relaxed by her sides.

**Imagine this.** Imagine bringing one knee toward your chest by hugging it. The hands go BEHIND the THIGH (above the knee), not behind the kneecap. The other foot stays flat on the bed.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Start with the right knee.**
2. **Lift the right foot off the bed** and bring the knee toward the chest.
3. **Place both hands BEHIND the right THIGH** (above the kneecap). NEVER behind the kneecap directly.
4. **Gently pull the knee toward the chest** — only as far as comfortable. The other foot stays flat on the bed.
5. **Hold for 20 to 30 seconds.** Breathe normally throughout.
6. **Release slowly.** Lower the foot back to the bed.
7. **Switch legs.** Same pattern.

**Breathing.** Slow nasal breathing throughout the hold. The back relaxes more with each exhale.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* a gentle stretch in the low back (especially the side opposite the bent leg); a mild pull in the back of the bent leg's thigh; relaxation of the lumbar muscles.
- *Abnormal:* sharp pain in the knee (the hands are behind the kneecap, not the thigh — fix); pain in the bent knee (range too deep — pull less); back spasm (release immediately).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Hands BEHIND thigh, above kneecap | Hands behind the kneecap (knee compression) |
| Other foot stays flat on bed | Other knee also bent toward chest (forbidden — DOUBLE knee-to-chest) |
| 20- to 30-sec hold with steady breathing | Bouncing the knee toward the chest |
| Release slowly | Drops the leg |

**Dosage.**
- Day 8: 3 reps × 20-sec hold per leg.
- Day 9 to 14: 3 reps × 20-sec hold per leg.
- Week 3+: 3 reps × 30-sec hold per leg.
- Once daily.

**Progression rules.**
- Hold time: 20 sec → 30 sec → 45 sec by Week 4.
- Range deepens naturally as flexion improves.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Hands behind the kneecap (knee compression)* → fix immediately; hands behind the THIGH only.
- *Both knees bent toward chest (DOUBLE knee-to-chest)* → forbidden in this plan. Single only.
- *Bouncing the knee* → smooth steady hold.
- *Holding the breath* → breathe normally; the stretch deepens with each exhale.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Lift one knee toward your chest. Hands behind the THIGH, not the knee."*
- During the hold: *"Hold. Breathe. Each breath, the back relaxes a bit more."*
- *"NEVER both knees together. One at a time."*
- Release: *"Slowly lower the foot back to the bed."*

**You know it's working when…** the morning LBP score drops; the lumbar feels less stiff after a session.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* one leg at a time only. Both legs stretching together is a forbidden movement.
- *Chronic LBP:* this is one of her gold-standard exercises.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* knee pain → hands behind the thigh, not the knee. Back spasm → release and switch to seated cat-cow (Ex 21).

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if the bent knee can't reach 80° comfortably (need adequate flexion). Resume when flexion is back.

### Exercise 20 — Seated hamstring stretch (with belt or towel)

**Why this exercise.** Tight hamstrings pull the pelvis backward, increasing lumbar load — one of the four causes of her LBP. A 2024 meta-analysis (14 RCTs) showed hamstring stretching significantly reduces chronic LBP. For knee recovery, hamstring length also helps full knee extension (a tight hamstring can hold the knee in slight flexion). Double-benefit exercise. Done seated to avoid lumbar load (better than standing toe-touch for her).

**Earliest safe start.** Day 6.

**Equipment needed.**
- A firm chair with a footstool (or a long bed where she can sit with one leg out).
- A long bath towel or belt (~150 cm).
- Lumbar roll for the chair.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Sit on the edge of a firm chair OR sit on the bed with both legs in front.
2. Lumbar roll behind L4–L5.
3. The right leg is straight, heel on the footstool (or extended on the bed). The other leg is bent (foot on the floor or knee bent at 90°).
4. Wrap the towel around the BALL of the right foot, holding both ends in her hands.
5. Sit tall, spine straight.

**Imagine this.** Imagine pulling the toes of the straight leg gently toward the face using the towel. The stretch is in the back of the straight thigh.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Sit tall, spine straight, lumbar roll in place.**
2. **Right leg straight, heel resting on the footstool.**
3. **Loop the towel around the ball of the right foot.**
4. **Pull the towel gently toward her face** — flexing the foot. The toes come toward the nose. The leg stays straight.
5. She feels a stretch at the BACK of the right THIGH.
6. **Hold for 30 seconds.** Breathe normally.
7. **Release the pull SLOWLY.**
8. **Switch legs.** Same pattern.

**Breathing.** Slow nasal breathing throughout. The stretch deepens slightly with each exhale.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* a clear stretch at the back of the straight thigh (the hamstring); the leg stays straight; the spine stays upright; no back pain.
- *Abnormal:* back pain (she's leaning forward — sit taller); knee pain (the leg is over-straightened — let it bend slightly); hamstring cramp (release).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Sits tall, spine straight, lumbar roll in place | Leans forward to "reach the toes" (forbidden — flares LBP) |
| Towel pulls the toes gently toward face | Pulls hard, forcing the stretch |
| Straight leg stays straight | Knee bends to make it easier |
| Stretch in back of thigh | Stretch in the back (means she's leaning) |

**Dosage.**
- Day 6: 30 sec × 3 reps per leg, once daily.
- Day 8: 30 sec × 5 reps per leg.
- Week 3+: 45 sec × 3 reps per leg, once daily.

**Progression rules.**
- Hold time: 30 sec → 45 sec → 60 sec by Week 4.
- Range deepens naturally as hamstrings lengthen.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Leaning forward to reach the toes* → STOP. Sit tall. Use the towel to pull the foot, don't lean.
- *Forcing the stretch* → gentle pull only.
- *Knee bends* → keep the leg straight; if hamstring is very tight, bring the foot lower (not on a stool, just on the floor) so the stretch is easier.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Sit tall. Lumbar roll behind your back."*
- During the stretch: *"Pull the towel gently. Keep the leg straight. Stretch in the back of the thigh, not the back."*
- If she leans forward: *"Sit up straight. Don't lean. Use the towel."*

**You know it's working when…** hamstring length improves; the LBP morning score drops; full knee extension becomes easier.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* alternate legs; never stretch both at the same time.
- *Chronic LBP:* the spine MUST stay upright. Any leaning forward flares LBP.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* if back pain → sit taller, use a thicker lumbar roll. If hamstring cramp → release, sip water.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if she cannot tolerate sitting upright > 5 min today. Restart tomorrow.

### Exercise 21 — Seated cat-cow (spinal mobility in a chair)

**Why this exercise.** The standard cat-cow exercise requires kneeling on hands and knees — forbidden post-TKR (kneeling on the operated knees is a permanent restriction). The seated version captures the same spinal mobility benefit (alternating spinal flexion and extension) without any knee load. It mobilises the lumbar spine in both directions, helping with stiffness and the morning LBP. Mechanism-based recommendation; weak but reasonable evidence.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 12.

**Equipment needed.**
- A firm chair with a footstool.
- Lumbar roll REMOVED for this exercise (the spine needs to move freely).

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Sit upright in the chair, on the FRONT edge (so the back doesn't touch the back-rest).
2. Both feet flat on the floor (or footstool), knees at 90°.
3. Hands resting on the thighs.
4. Spine in neutral position (natural curves).
5. Look forward.

**Imagine this.** Imagine your spine is a string of beads. You alternately ROUND it forward (cat — pulling the navel back, dropping the head forward) and ARCH it backward (cow — lifting the chest, looking up slightly). Smooth, slow flow.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Start in neutral.** Take 1 belly breath.
2. **Cat (round forward):** as you exhale, slowly round the spine forward — pull the belly button back toward the spine, drop the head, tuck the tailbone. The shoulders come forward.
3. **Pause for 1 second** at the rounded end.
4. **Cow (arch backward):** as you inhale, slowly reverse — lift the chest, look up slightly, tilt the tailbone back, gently arch the spine backward.
5. **Pause for 1 second** at the arched end.
6. Continue the slow flow: cat → cow → cat → cow.
7. Do 5 to 8 cycles. One set takes about 1 minute.

**Breathing.** Coordinated with movement: exhale on cat (round), inhale on cow (arch).

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* a gentle wave of movement through the spine; a release of stiffness; no sharp pain anywhere; mild stretching across the upper back, mid-back, and lower back at different parts of the cycle.
- *Abnormal:* sharp pain in the lower back at the arched end (reduce range of arch); dizziness (slow down); knee pain (sit further back so the knees aren't supporting weight).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Smooth slow flow through the spine | Jerky or rigid movement |
| Cat: rounded fully; cow: arched gently | Only one phase done, no flow |
| Coordinated breath (exhale cat, inhale cow) | Breath held |
| Spine moves; hips stay seated | Hips lift off the chair |

**Dosage.**
- Day 12: 5 cycles × 2 sessions per day.
- Week 3+: 8 cycles × 2 sessions per day.

**Progression rules.**
- Range of motion deepens naturally with practice.
- Cycle count: 5 → 8 → 10 by Week 4.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Jerky movement* → cue "smooth wave. Slow."
- *Only doing one phase* → both directions are needed for full benefit.
- *Breath held* → coordinate with the movement.
- *Hips lift off the chair* → stay seated; only the spine moves.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Sit on the front of the chair. Spine straight. Look forward."*
- During cat: *"Breathe out. Round the spine. Drop the head. Tuck the tailbone."*
- During cow: *"Breathe in. Lift the chest. Look up a little. Arch the spine."*
- *"Smooth. Like a wave. No stops, no jerks."*

**You know it's working when…** spinal stiffness eases; the LBP morning score drops; she feels looser after the session.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* fully safe; no knee load (knees stay at 90°).
- *Chronic LBP:* good back-friendly mobility exercise.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* sharp pain at the arched end → reduce arch range. If standing version is needed, stand at the kitchen counter for support and do a smaller version.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if she cannot tolerate sitting upright > 10 min today. Restart tomorrow.

### Exercise 22 — Supine hip abduction (isometric)

**Why this exercise.** Activates the gluteus medius (the muscle on the side of the hip), which stabilises the pelvis during single-leg activities and stabilises the SI joint via its attachment to the pelvic bones. SI joint dysfunction is one of the four causes of her LBP. The isometric version (no leg movement, just pressing outward) is safe in Phase 2; the dynamic side-lying version waits until Week 4. Glute medius weakness is a major cause of "Trendelenburg" gait (hip drops on the swinging side) — particularly important for bilateral patients to avoid.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 9.

**Equipment needed.**
- The bed.
- Thin head pillow.
- Optional low-back towel.
- Two firm pillows (or husband's hands as resistance).

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Lie on the back, both legs long and straight.
2. Thin pillow under head, low-back towel optional.
3. Place a firm pillow on the OUTSIDE of each thigh, OR place your hands on the outside of each thigh.
4. Arms relaxed by her sides.

**Imagine this.** Imagine pushing your thighs OUTWARD against the pillows (or hands), like trying to spread your legs apart against resistance. The thighs don't actually move — the pillows resist them.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Squeeze the OUTSIDE of both thighs against the pillows** (push outward).
2. **Hold for 5 seconds.** Count out loud.
3. **Release slowly** for 2 seconds.
4. Repeat 8 times. One set.

**Breathing.** Steady through the nose. Hum or count.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* the muscles on the side of the hip (gluteus medius) tighten; some tightness on the outside of the thigh (TFL/IT band area); no knee pain.
- *Abnormal:* sharp pain in the hip (stop, may indicate trochanteric bursitis); back pain (lumbar towel position).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Both side-of-hip muscles tighten | One side dominates |
| Legs stay still (isometric only) | Legs actually move outward |
| 5-sec hold steady | Brief flex |

**Dosage.**
- Day 9: 8 reps × 1 set, 5-sec hold.
- Day 11+: 8 reps × 2 sets, 5-sec hold.
- Week 3+: 10 reps × 2 sets, 8-sec hold.
- Once daily.

**Progression rules.**
- Hold time: 5 sec → 8 sec → 10 sec.
- Week 4 progression: side-lying hip abduction (dynamic, top leg lifts; see Phase 3).

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *One side dominates* → cue "press both sides equally."
- *Legs actually move outward* → it's isometric; legs should stay still.
- *Holding the breath* → hum or count.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Press the outsides of your thighs out against the pillows. Both sides equally."*
- During the hold: *"Hold. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Release."*

**You know it's working when…** the side-of-hip muscles activate clearly; gait becomes more stable.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* both legs work together; safe.
- *Chronic LBP:* helpful for SI joint stability.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* hip pain → reduce force; check the pillow position.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip on a flare day; resume next day.

### Exercise 23 — Standing weight shift (in walker)

**Why this exercise.** First standing exercise of the recovery (Day 6). Weight-shifting from one leg to the other inside the walker frame teaches her to load each operated leg without committing to single-leg stance. It builds the proprioception (joint position awareness) and balance that single-leg stance will require at Week 5. For bilateral patients, the early weight shifts also assess which knee accepts weight more easily — useful for the daily plan.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 6.

**Equipment needed.**
- Walker.
- Closed-back rubber-soled shoes.
- Husband nearby (in front, ready to catch).

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Stand inside the walker frame, both feet inside.
2. Stand tall, head up.
3. Hands on the walker grips.
4. Weight equally on both feet.

**Imagine this.** Imagine you are gently rocking your weight from one leg to the other, like a slow metronome. Right — left — right — left. Smooth and small at first.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Stand tall in the walker, both feet inside.**
2. **Slowly shift weight to the RIGHT leg** (lift the left foot off the floor by 1 cm if comfortable, but keep it ready to land).
3. **Hold for 2 seconds.**
4. **Shift weight back to BOTH feet equally.**
5. **Slowly shift weight to the LEFT leg.**
6. **Hold for 2 seconds.**
7. **Return to both feet equally.**
8. Repeat 10 shifts each way (20 shifts total). One set.

**Breathing.** Normal nasal breathing. Hum if needed.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* awareness of the working leg taking weight; mild thigh activation; balance maintained throughout.
- *Abnormal:* sharp pain in the loading knee (stop, the knee not ready); imbalance (sit immediately); dizziness (sit, sip water).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Smooth slow shifts, 2-sec hold | Quick rocking back and forth |
| Both feet inside the walker | Feet outside the frame |
| Stands tall, looks forward | Hunched, looking down |
| Equal time on each side | One side favored |

**Dosage.**
- Day 6: 10 shifts each way × 1 set, once today.
- Day 8+: 10 shifts each way × 2 sets, once daily.
- Week 3+: 15 shifts each way × 2 sets, once daily.

**Progression rules.**
- Hold time per shift: 2 sec → 5 sec by Week 3.
- Lift the unloaded foot higher (5 cm) by Week 3 — this is a graded weight-shift hover inside the walker, NOT the formal single-leg stance (Exercise 30), which remains gated to Week 5.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Quick rocking* → slow down; 2-sec hold each side.
- *Hunched over walker* → stand tall.
- *Feet outside walker* → both feet inside the frame.
- *Favoring one side* → equal time both sides.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Stand tall in the walker. Both feet inside. Hands on the grips."*
- During shifts: *"Slow shift to the right. Hold. 1, 2. Now shift to the left. Hold. 1, 2."*
- If she's quick: *"Slow. Like a slow swing."*

**You know it's working when…** by Day 9 she can hold each shift for 2 sec confidently; by Week 3 the shift becomes smooth and lifting one foot off the floor for 1 sec is easy.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* CRITICAL. The walker is the safety net. Don't shift outside the walker frame.
- *Chronic LBP:* stand tall; don't hunch.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* knee pain on one side → smaller shifts; or skip shifting to that side today.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if she cannot stand confidently for 2+ minutes today.

### Exercise 24 — Supported standing hip extension

**Why this exercise.** Activates the gluteus maximus in standing — closer to functional gait than the supine glute squeeze (Ex 6). The hip extension motion is what powers walking forward; weak glute max = compensatory low-back overuse. Helps her LBP. For bilateral patients, this exercise builds the foundation for confident walker → cane transition.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 6.

**Equipment needed.**
- A sturdy kitchen counter or table edge to lean on (waist height).
- Walker beside her for balance.
- Closed-back rubber-soled shoes.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Stand facing the kitchen counter, both hands resting on the counter for support.
2. Walker placed beside her on the operating-leg side as a backup.
3. Stand tall, head up, look forward.
4. Both feet hip-width apart, both knees slightly soft (NOT locked).

**Imagine this.** Imagine slowly lifting one leg STRAIGHT BACK behind you, like a kickback. The leg stays straight, the back stays still, the buttock squeezes.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Stand tall, both hands on the counter.**
2. **Squeeze the right buttock and slowly lift the right leg STRAIGHT BACK** behind you (5 to 15 cm).
3. **Keep the leg straight** (don't bend the knee).
4. **Keep the back upright** (don't arch the back to lift the leg higher).
5. **Hold for 2 seconds at the back.**
6. **Slowly lower** the leg back to the start.
7. Repeat 5 to 10 times on the right, then switch legs.

**Breathing.** Exhale on the lift; inhale on the lower.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* the right buttock muscle (glute max) tightens; the leg lifts a small amount; the supporting leg holds her steady.
- *Abnormal:* sharp lower-back pain (she's arching the back to lift higher; reduce range); knee pain in supporting leg (stop); imbalance (sit immediately).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Buttock squeezes; leg lifts 5 to 15 cm straight back | Back arches to fake the lift |
| Leg stays straight | Knee bends |
| Back stays upright | Trunk leans forward |
| 2-sec hold at the back | Quick swings |

**Dosage.**
- Day 6: 5 reps × 1 set per leg, once today.
- Day 9+: 6 reps × 2 sets per leg, once daily.
- Week 3+: 10 reps × 2 sets per leg, once daily.

**Progression rules.**
- Range: small (5 cm) → medium (10 cm) → larger (15 cm) by Week 3.
- Hold time: 2 sec → 5 sec by Week 3.
- Week 4: add a soft theraband around the ankle for resistance.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Arching the back to lift higher* → cue "small kickback. Don't arch the back."
- *Knee bends* → keep the leg straight.
- *Leaning forward* → stand tall.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Stand tall. Both hands on the counter. Squeeze the right buttock. Slowly lift the leg straight back. 5 cm. Don't arch your back."*
- During the lift: *"Hold. 1, 2. Slowly down."*
- If she arches: *"Smaller lift. Don't arch the back. Just squeeze the buttock."*

**You know it's working when…** by Day 11 the right buttock fires clearly; by Week 3 the lift is smooth and 10 cm.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* both legs are operated. Alternate sides. The supporting leg gets the same load each time.
- *Chronic LBP:* CRITICAL — keep the back upright. Arching the back to fake a higher lift will flare her LBP.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* sharp LBP → smaller range; check posture. Knee pain in supporting leg → fewer reps or skip.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if standing tolerance < 2 min today.

### Exercise 25 — Mini-bridge (small hip lift, 3 to 5 cm)

**Why this exercise.** The mini-bridge strengthens the glutes (one of the four LBP causes) and the hamstrings. It is the safest bridge variant for someone with chronic LBP — the standard "deep bridge" hyperextends the lumbar spine, which loads her facet joints and reliably flares her back. The mini version (lift only 3 to 5 cm) gives the glute strength without the lumbar load. Glute-first technique is critical: she initiates the lift with the BUTTOCKS, not by arching the back.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 7. SKIP if she has any LBP > 3/10 with the introduction; retry on Day 9.

**Equipment needed.**
- The bed.
- Thin head pillow.
- Low-back towel REMOVED for this exercise.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Lie on the back, both knees bent in hook-lying (feet flat on the bed, knees and feet hip-width apart).
2. Thin pillow under head.
3. Low-back towel REMOVED.
4. Arms beside the body.

**Imagine this.** Imagine you are gently pressing the soles of your feet down into the bed to lift just the buttocks 3 to 5 cm off the bed. Tiny lift. Squeeze the buttocks first, then the lift happens.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Settle into hook-lying.** Take 1 belly breath.
2. **Squeeze both buttocks tightly** (gluteal squeeze, Ex 6).
3. **As you exhale, lift the hips just 3 to 5 cm off the bed** by pressing the feet down. The lower back stays flat — DO NOT arch it.
4. **Hold for 3 seconds at the top.** Continue breathing normally.
5. **Lower SLOWLY** over 3 seconds.
6. Rest for 2 seconds.
7. Repeat 5 to 8 times. One set.

**Breathing.** Exhale on the lift; inhale on the lower.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* the buttocks tighten clearly; a small lift of the hips (you can barely see it from the side); the lower back stays flat against the bed (or barely off it); no back pain.
- *Abnormal:* sharp lower-back pain (she's arching the back and lifting too high — STOP, reduce range or skip); cramp in the hamstring (over-using the hamstring instead of the glutes — see Common mistakes).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Tiny lift, 3 to 5 cm | Big arch lift (10+ cm) |
| Buttocks fire FIRST, then the lift | Back arches first (lumbar load) |
| Lower back stays flat | Lower back arches up |
| 3-sec hold; controlled lower | Quick up and down |

**Dosage.**
- Day 7: 5 reps × 1 set, 3-sec hold.
- Day 9 (if Day 7 was tolerated): 6 reps × 2 sets, 5-sec hold.
- Week 3+: 8 reps × 2 sets, 5- to 8-sec hold.
- Once daily.

**Progression rules.**
- Hold time: 3 sec → 5 sec → 8 sec.
- Range: stays at 3 to 5 cm. NEVER progress to a deep bridge — her LBP cannot tolerate it.
- Week 4: progression is to single-leg bridges (one foot lifts off the bed, other leg straight) at 3 to 5 cm.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Big arch lift* → cue "tiny lift only. 3 to 5 cm. The lower back stays flat."
- *Back arches first (instead of glutes firing)* → "squeeze the buttocks BEFORE you lift."
- *Hamstring takes over* → cue "front of the buttocks, not back of the thigh. Squeeze the buttock cheek."
- *Quick reps* → slow down. 3 sec up, 3 sec hold, 3 sec down.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Knees bent. Feet flat. Squeeze your buttocks first."*
- During the lift: *"Tiny lift. Just 3 cm. Don't arch the back. Hold. 1, 2, 3."*
- During the lower: *"Slowly down. 1, 2, 3."*
- *"If your back hurts, stop and tell me."*

**You know it's working when…** the buttocks fire clearly; by Week 3 the lift is smooth; the morning LBP score drops by 1 to 2 points.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* both feet take equal load; safe.
- *Chronic LBP:* CRITICAL — strict glute-first technique. If LBP flares, STOP. Restart at half range in 2 days.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* skip for 2 days, retry at HALF range (lift only 1 to 2 cm). If still flares, omit this exercise from the plan and replace with more glute squeezes (Ex 6) and standing hip extension (Ex 24).

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip on a flare day. Skip permanently if it consistently flares LBP > 3/10 — replace with Ex 6 and Ex 24.

### Exercise 26 — Short-arc quadriceps (SAQ with rolled towel)

**Why this exercise.** Bridges the gap between the static quad set (Ex 5) and the full straight-leg raise (SLR). The patient bends the knee over a rolled towel (forming a fulcrum), then slowly straightens the knee. Only the lower leg moves — the thigh stays still. This isolates the quad in a partial-range concentric contraction, which is much easier than a full SLR. Mizner et al. (JBJS 2005) showed quad activation deficit at POD 7 is ~20 %; SAQ accelerates the recovery.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 5.

**Equipment needed.**
- The bed.
- Thin head pillow.
- Low-back towel.
- A firm rolled-up bath towel (or rolled blanket) about 15 to 20 cm in diameter, placed under the knee.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Lie on the back, both legs long.
2. Thin pillow under head, low-back towel in place.
3. Place the firm rolled towel under the right knee (so the right knee is bent over the roll at about 30°).
4. The other leg stays long and flat.
5. Arms beside the body.

**Imagine this.** Imagine the lower leg is a slow lever. The knee is the hinge. You straighten the leg by lifting just the lower leg (foot and shin) up. The thigh stays on the towel.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Right knee bent over the rolled towel.**
2. **Tighten the front of the thigh.**
3. **Slowly lift the lower leg (foot and shin) up** by straightening the knee. The thigh stays on the towel.
4. **Aim to fully straighten the knee at the top.**
5. **Hold for 3 seconds at the top** (knee fully straight).
6. **Slowly lower** over 3 seconds back to the starting bent position.
7. Repeat 5 to 10 times on the right, then switch.

**Breathing.** Exhale on the lift; inhale on the lower.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* the front of the thigh works hard; the lower leg lifts smoothly; the knee straightens fully at the top; mild thigh tiredness after 5 reps.
- *Abnormal:* the lower leg won't fully straighten (extension lag — keep working on this); sharp pain at the front of the knee (stop); back pain (lumbar towel position).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Slow lift (~3 sec) | Fast jerky lift |
| Knee fully straightens at the top | Knee stops 5 to 10° short (lag) |
| Slow controlled lower | Leg drops uncontrolled |
| Thigh stays on the towel | Thigh lifts off the towel (it becomes an SLR) |

**Dosage.**
- Day 5: 5 reps × 2 sets per leg, once today.
- Day 6 to 9: 5 to 8 reps × 2 sets per leg, once daily.
- Day 10+: 8 reps × 2 sets per leg.
- Week 3+: 10 reps × 3 sets per leg.

**Progression rules.**
- Hold time at the top: 3 sec → 5 sec by Week 3.
- Once SAQ is reliable with full extension at the top, progress to true SLR (Ex 5 progression — Day 10).
- Week 4: add 0.5 kg ankle weight if SLR is established.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Extension lag* → don't progress; stay with this exercise until the knee fully straightens at the top.
- *Thigh lifts off the towel* → that's an SLR, not SAQ. Cue "thigh stays on the towel."
- *Quick lift* → slow down.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Towel under the knee. Tighten the thigh."*
- During the lift: *"Slowly lift the lower leg. Straighten the knee. Lock it straight at the top."*
- At the hold: *"Hold. 1, 2, 3. Slowly down."*

**You know it's working when…** the knee fully straightens at the top by Day 9 to 10; SLR becomes possible without lag.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* alternate legs.
- *Chronic LBP:* the lumbar towel is essential; the supine position with both legs straight (the working leg over the towel, the other leg flat) is acceptable for short bouts.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* sharp knee pain → reduce range; do half lifts only.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if the thigh feels too sore from yesterday; do quad sets (Ex 5) instead.

### Exercise 27 — Mini-squats with walker (15° to 30° depth)

**Why this exercise.** Mini-squats are the first functional strengthening exercise for the quadriceps in a closed-chain (foot fixed) position — closer to walking, sit-to-stand, and stairs than the open-chain seated knee extension (Ex 9). For bilateral TKR, the walker provides the safety frame. Depth is strictly limited to 15° to 30° (a "knee bend," not a real squat) — deeper bends overload the implant and the patellofemoral joint.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 10.

**Equipment needed.**
- Walker placed in front of her.
- Closed-back rubber-soled shoes.
- Husband nearby (in front, ready to catch).

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Stand inside the walker frame, both feet inside, hip-width apart.
2. Stand tall, head up.
3. Hands on the walker grips.
4. Look forward.

**Imagine this.** Imagine slowly bending the knees to peek under a low table — but only a tiny bend. Knees go forward over the toes (NOT past the toes), buttocks go back slightly. Push back up.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Stand tall in the walker, both feet inside, hip-width apart.**
2. **Slowly bend both knees** about 15° to 30° (a small dip) by sending the buttocks back AND the knees forward. Keep the back straight.
3. **Stop at the comfortable bend** (NOT a deep squat).
4. **Hold for 2 seconds.**
5. **Slowly straighten the knees back to standing.**
6. **Pause for 2 seconds.**
7. Repeat 5 to 8 times. One set.

**Breathing.** Exhale on the up (the harder phase); inhale on the down.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* both quads working; both knees bending equally; balance maintained; the walker steady; mild thigh tiredness after 5 reps.
- *Abnormal:* sharp knee pain (stop, reduce depth); knee buckle (sit immediately); imbalance (sit immediately); back pain (she's leaning forward — sit back, keep the back straight).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Small bend (15° to 30°) | Deep squat (forbidden — implant load) |
| Both knees bend equally | One knee bends more than the other |
| Knees track over toes (don't go past toes) | Knees collapse inward |
| Slow controlled (2 sec down, 2 sec hold, 2 sec up) | Quick up and down |
| Both feet inside the walker | Feet outside the frame |

**Dosage.**
- Day 10: 5 reps × 1 set, once today.
- Day 11 to 12: 6 reps × 2 sets, once daily.
- Day 13+: 8 reps × 3 sets, once daily.
- Week 3: depth deepens to 40°; 8 reps × 3 sets.
- Week 4: 10 reps × 3 sets.

**Progression rules.**
- Depth: 15° → 30° (Week 2) → 40° (Week 3) → 60° (Week 5+ if comfortable). NEVER deep squat.
- Hold at the bottom: 2 sec → 5 sec by Week 4 (becomes a wall-sit-style hold).
- Sets: 1 → 2 → 3.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Going too deep* → cue "small bend only. Like peeking under a coffee table."
- *Knees collapse inward* → cue "knees track over toes."
- *Leaning forward (back rounds)* → cue "back stays straight. Send the buttocks back, not down."
- *Quick reps* → slow down.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Stand tall in the walker. Both feet inside. Look forward."*
- During the bend: *"Slowly bend the knees. Send the buttocks back a little. Small bend."*
- At the bottom: *"Hold. 1, 2. Slowly back up."*
- *"If you feel a buckle or sharp pain, stop."*

**You know it's working when…** by Day 13 she does 8 reps × 3 sets confidently; by Week 3 she can deepen to 40°; by Week 4 she manages 10 reps without effort.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* both knees take equal load. Walker is essential safety net. NEVER do this without the walker until cleared by surgeon.
- *Chronic LBP:* keep the back straight; don't lean forward.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* sharp pain → reduce depth. Knee buckle → STOP, do not retry until cleared by physio.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if standing tolerance is poor today (rest day).

### Exercise 28 — Calf raises at walker

**Why this exercise.** Strengthens the calves (gastrocnemius and soleus), which provide the push-off in walking. After 2 weeks of bed rest, the calves are weak — calf raises restore push-off strength and gait quality. For bilateral TKR, calf raises also load the foot/ankle gently, helping with the swelling drainage chain (calf pump).

**Earliest safe start.** Day 11.

**Equipment needed.**
- Walker in front, OR a sturdy kitchen counter to hold.
- Closed-back rubber-soled shoes.
- Husband nearby.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Stand inside the walker frame (or facing the kitchen counter), both feet flat, hip-width apart.
2. Stand tall, head up.
3. Hands on the walker grips (or counter).
4. Look forward.

**Imagine this.** Imagine slowly rising onto your tiptoes, then slowly lowering. Both feet rise together. Smooth and controlled.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Stand tall, both feet inside the walker.**
2. **Slowly lift BOTH heels off the floor** at the same time, rising onto the balls of the feet (tiptoe).
3. **Hold for 2 seconds at the top.**
4. **Slowly lower both heels** back to the floor.
5. **Pause for 2 seconds.**
6. Repeat 5 to 10 times. One set.

**Breathing.** Exhale on the rise; inhale on the lower.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* the calves tighten and tire; both feet rise equally; balance is maintained.
- *Abnormal:* cramp in one calf (stop, sip water, sit); imbalance (sit immediately); knee pain (uncommon — stop, sit).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Both heels rise equally | One heel rises higher than the other |
| Slow rise (~2 sec), 2-sec hold, 2-sec lower | Quick bounce |
| Stays inside the walker frame | Loses balance, leans forward |
| Stands tall, head up | Hunched over walker |

**Dosage.**
- Day 11: 5 reps × 1 set, once today.
- Day 12+: 8 reps × 2 sets, once daily.
- Week 3+: 10 reps × 3 sets, once daily.
- Week 6: progress to single-leg calf raise (one leg at a time, hands for balance).

**Progression rules.**
- Reps and sets increase as above.
- Single-leg calf raise (much harder) only from Week 6 with hand support.
- Eventually, no hand support (Week 12+).

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *One heel rises higher* → cue "both heels rise together."
- *Quick bounce* → slow down.
- *Hunched over walker* → stand tall.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Stand tall. Both feet inside the walker. Slowly rise onto your tiptoes."*
- At the top: *"Hold. 1, 2. Slowly lower."*

**You know it's working when…** by Day 13 she does 8 reps × 2 sets confidently; by Week 3 the rise is smooth and balanced; by Week 6 she manages a single-leg calf raise with hand support.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* both calves work together; safe.
- *Chronic LBP:* stand tall.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* cramp → stop, sip water, do gentle calf stretch. Imbalance → smaller rise.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if standing tolerance is poor today.

### Exercise 29 — Step rehearsal (4 cm block)

**Why this exercise.** Step rehearsal is the bridge between mini-squats (closed-chain knee bend) and stair climbing (alternating leg loading). She places one foot on a small block (~4 cm tall, like a thick book), then steps back down. No body-weight push-up yet — that comes in Phase 3. The exercise builds the motor pattern of step climbing and assesses single-leg loading capacity safely.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 12.

**Equipment needed.**
- A thick book (~4 cm tall) or a low aerobic step. Stable, non-slip surface on top.
- Walker in front for balance.
- Closed-back rubber-soled shoes.
- Husband nearby.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Stand inside the walker frame, both feet inside.
2. Place the thick book about 30 cm in front of her.
3. Hands on the walker grips.
4. Stand tall.

**Imagine this.** Imagine you are going to step ONTO the book, stand briefly, then step back down. No full weight-shift onto the book leg yet — the walker takes most of the weight.

**Step-by-step movement (Day 12 to Day 17 — rehearsal only, no push-up).**
1. **Stand inside the walker.**
2. **Lift the right foot and place it ON the book** (foot fully on the book).
3. **Pause for 2 seconds** (DO NOT push body weight up onto the book — the walker carries the weight).
4. **Step the right foot back down** to the floor inside the walker.
5. Repeat 5 times on the right, then switch to the left foot.

**Step-by-step movement (Day 17 onwards — real step-up).**
1. **Stand inside the walker.**
2. **Lift the right foot and place it ON the book.**
3. **Push the body weight UP onto the right leg**, lifting the left foot off the floor. Stand briefly on the right (book) leg.
4. **Step the left foot down to the floor.**
5. **Step the right foot back down** to the floor inside the walker.
6. Repeat 5 reps on the right, then switch.

**Breathing.** Exhale on the step up; inhale on the step down.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* the right thigh works as it accepts the weight; balance maintained; the walker provides safety.
- *Abnormal:* sharp knee pain (stop, reduce); imbalance (sit); knee buckle (STOP, sit, do not retry).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Foot fully on the book | Toes only on the book (slips) |
| 2-sec pause | Quick step on and off |
| Walker stays in place | Walker tips forward |
| Both legs done equally | One leg favored |

**Dosage.**
- Day 12: 5 reps × 1 set per leg, rehearsal only.
- Day 13 to 14: 6 reps × 2 sets per leg, rehearsal only.
- Day 17+ (real step-up): 5 reps × 2 sets per leg.
- Week 3: progress to a real 4-inch / 10 cm step; 5 reps × 2 sets per leg.
- Week 4: progress to 6-inch / 15 cm step.

**Progression rules.**
- Step height: 4 cm → 10 cm → 15 cm.
- Sets and reps increase as above.
- Add step-down (eccentric, same step) at Week 5 — separate exercise.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Toes only on the book* → place the whole foot on the book.
- *Walker tips forward* → walker stays in place; she lifts her foot, not her body.
- *Pushing too hard with the leg before Day 17* → no push-up in rehearsal phase.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Stand inside the walker. Place your right foot fully on the book. Hold."*
- During the rehearsal: *"Don't push up. Walker takes the weight. Just place the foot."*
- During the real step-up (Day 17+): *"Push UP onto the right leg. Stand briefly on the book. Step down with the left."*

**You know it's working when…** by Day 17 she can do a real step-up onto the book; by Week 3 she steps up onto a 10 cm step; by Week 4 onto a 15 cm step.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* both legs need equal practice. Don't favor one.
- *Chronic LBP:* stand tall.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* knee pain → reduce to rehearsal-only for 2 days. Imbalance → smaller step.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if balance feels off today or knee is more swollen.

### Exercise 30 — Single-leg stance (inside walker — bilateral safety gate)

**Why this exercise.** Single-leg stance is the **critical safety gate for bilateral TKR.** Loading the entire body weight on one fresh implant — while the other knee is unable to absorb a wobble — is the highest fall-risk move in the early rehab plan. We delay it to Week 5 specifically. Even then, we start INSIDE the walker frame with the husband behind. The exercise builds the proprioception and confidence needed for cane walking, stairs without rails, and outdoor walking.

**Earliest safe start.** Week 5 (Day 29). Do NOT start if any of: extension lag, SLR lag, swelling > yesterday morning, walker not yet stable for 30 sec double-leg stance.

**Equipment needed.**
- Walker.
- Closed-back rubber-soled shoes.
- Husband STANDING BEHIND her, hands ready to catch at her waist.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Stand inside the walker frame, both feet inside, hip-width apart.
2. Hands on the walker grips.
3. Husband standing behind her, close enough to catch.
4. Stand tall, head up, look forward.

**Imagine this.** Imagine slowly lifting one foot off the floor — just 1 to 2 cm — and balancing on the other leg. The walker is right there for safety. The husband is right behind. There is no hurry.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Stand tall in the walker, both feet inside.**
2. **Slowly shift weight to the LEFT leg.**
3. **Slowly lift the right foot 1 to 2 cm off the floor.**
4. **Hold for 5 seconds.** Continue breathing.
5. **Lower the right foot** back to the floor.
6. Pause for 5 seconds with both feet down.
7. **Switch sides:** shift to the right, lift the left.
8. Repeat 3 times each leg. Build to 10 sec hold by end of Week 5.

**Breathing.** Steady through the nose. Talk to her husband if she tends to breath-hold.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* the supporting leg activates strongly; the body wobbles slightly (this is normal and expected); balance is maintained with the walker.
- *Abnormal:* sharp pain in the supporting knee (stop, the knee not ready); knee buckle (STOP, sit, do not retry until cleared by physio); imbalance that needs the walker hard (delay to next week).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Foot lifts 1 to 2 cm; small lift only | Foot lifts 10+ cm (unsafe early on) |
| 5-sec hold (build to 10 sec) | Quick lift and drop |
| Stays inside walker | Outside walker frame |
| Husband behind, watching | Alone (forbidden in Week 5) |
| Both legs done equally | Only the "good" leg done (no good leg in bilateral) |

**Dosage.**
- Day 29 (start of Week 5): 5 sec × 3 reps per leg, once today.
- Day 30 to 35: build to 10 sec × 3 reps per leg, once daily.
- Week 6: 30 sec × 3 reps per leg, eyes open, no hand support (by Day 42).

**Progression rules.**
- Hold time: 5 sec → 10 sec → 20 sec → 30 sec.
- Hand support: walker (Week 5) → kitchen counter touch (Week 6) → no support (Week 7+).
- Eyes open only until Week 12. Eyes closed (much harder) is for the long-term phase.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Lifting the foot too high* → 1 to 2 cm only at the start.
- *Quick lift and drop* → hold for the full count.
- *Holding the breath* → talk to the husband during the hold.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- Before starting: *"I'm right behind you. Both hands on the walker. Stand tall."*
- During the lift: *"Slowly lift the right foot. Just 1 cm. Hold. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5."*
- During the lower: *"Slowly down. Both feet on the floor."*
- *"Tell me if you feel any buckle or pain. Stop immediately."*

**You know it's working when…** by end of Week 5 she holds 10 sec confidently on each leg; by Day 42 she holds 30 sec eyes open with no hand support.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* THIS IS THE HIGHEST FALL-RISK EXERCISE IN THE PLAN. Husband must be present always. NEVER attempt alone in Week 5. Walker is mandatory for Week 5; counter-touch from Week 6.
- *Chronic LBP:* stand tall.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* sharp pain or buckle → STOP. Do not retry until cleared by physio.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if she has any swelling > yesterday morning, OR any extension lag, OR any SLR lag, OR if husband is not present.

### Exercise 31 — Tandem stance (heel-to-toe)

**Why this exercise.** Tandem stance (one foot directly in front of the other, heel-to-toe like on a balance beam) is a graded balance challenge that is intermediate between double-leg stance and single-leg stance. It is safer than single-leg stance for early balance work because the back leg still bears some weight. For bilateral TKR, tandem stance starts at the kitchen counter (hands for safety), and progresses to no-hand stand.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 13.

**Equipment needed.**
- A sturdy kitchen counter or wall.
- Closed-back rubber-soled shoes.
- Husband nearby.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Stand sideways to the counter, both feet on the floor, hands on the counter for support.
2. Stand tall, head up.

**Imagine this.** Imagine standing on a tightrope. Place one foot directly in front of the other so the heel of the front foot touches the toes of the back foot. Hold the position. Both hands on the counter for safety.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **Stand sideways to the counter, both hands on it.**
2. **Place the right foot DIRECTLY IN FRONT of the left foot** so the right heel touches the left toes. Both feet on the same imaginary line.
3. **Stand tall, weight equally distributed.**
4. **Hold for 10 seconds.** Continue breathing.
5. **Step the right foot back to side-by-side.**
6. **Switch:** left foot in front. Hold 10 sec.
7. Repeat 3 times each side.

**Breathing.** Steady through the nose.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* small wobbles which the counter helps with; both knees stable; balance challenged but maintained.
- *Abnormal:* sharp knee pain (stop); imbalance that needs the counter hard (smaller stride or skip).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Heel-to-toe alignment | Feet wide apart (not on the line) |
| 10-sec hold | Quick stand and step out |
| Hands on counter for safety | No hand support (forbidden Week 2 to 3) |
| Both sides done | Only one side done |

**Dosage.**
- Day 13: 10 sec × 3 reps per side, once today.
- Day 14+: 10 to 15 sec × 3 reps per side, once daily.
- Week 3: 15 sec × 3 reps per side; one hand only on counter.
- Week 4: 20 sec × 3 reps per side; finger-touch only on counter.
- Week 5+: no hand support; tandem walking 3 m × 3 reps (Week 6+).

**Progression rules.**
- Hold time: 10 sec → 30 sec.
- Hand support: 2 hands → 1 hand → finger touch → no touch.
- Eventually progress to tandem walking (Week 6+).

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Feet wide apart* → cue "heel touches toes. Same line."
- *Quick stand and step out* → hold for the full count.
- *No hand support too soon* → use the counter until truly stable.

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Stand sideways to the counter. Both hands on it. Right foot directly in front of left. Heel touches toes."*
- During the hold: *"Hold. 1, 2, 3... 10. Step back."*

**You know it's working when…** by Week 3 she holds 15 sec with one hand; by Week 4 with finger-touch; by Week 5 with no hand.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* counter support is mandatory Week 2 to 3. Husband present.
- *Chronic LBP:* stand tall.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* knee pain → reduce stride (heel near toes, not touching). Imbalance → both hands on counter.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if balance is poor today; do standing weight shifts (Ex 23) instead.

### Exercise 32 — Chest physiotherapy (percussion + huffing)

**Why this exercise.** Anaesthesia + bed rest + reduced cough effort post-surgery causes secretions to pool in the lungs, especially at the bases. If she has any cough, wheeze, or rattly chest, chest physiotherapy clears the secretions and prevents pneumonia. The technique is gentle clapping (percussion) over the chest wall to loosen secretions, then huffing (forced exhale) to move them upward and out. Standard post-surgical care for any patient at risk of chest infection. AARC clinical practice guideline.

**Earliest safe start.** Day 1 if she has any cough, wheeze, or rattly chest. Otherwise, 3 times a week as preventive measure.

**Equipment needed.**
- Cup-shaped hand position (not a flat hand). Husband's hand.
- A pillow to hug.
- Tissue or basin for any secretions she brings up.

**Starting position (exact).**
1. Sit upright in a chair OR sit up in bed at 60° to 90°. If seated in a chair, lumbar roll behind L4–L5 (recommended for sessions lasting more than 5 minutes, given her chronic LBP).
2. Hug the pillow against the chest (provides comfort and supports the wound area if any chest soreness).
3. Husband sits beside her, ready to perform the percussion.

**Imagine this.** Imagine the husband's cupped hand is bouncing on the back of the chest, like a gentle horse-trot rhythm. Not painful — just rhythmic taps. The vibrations help loosen the sticky secretions.

**Step-by-step movement.**
1. **She sits upright, hugs the pillow.**
2. **Husband makes a CUPPED hand** (not flat). Imagine cupping a small bowl with the hand.
3. **Husband gently CLAPS the cupped hand on the upper back** (over the shoulder blades, then below them). The clap is rhythmic, about 2 claps per second. NOT painful.
4. **Continue for 1 to 2 minutes** on each lung area (upper back, mid-back, lower back below shoulder blades, sides).
5. **After percussion, she takes 3 deep belly breaths** (Ex 1).
6. **Then she does a HUFF:** take a medium breath in, then huff the air out forcefully through an open mouth (like fogging up a mirror). Repeat 3 huffs.
7. **If secretions come up, spit into the basin or tissue.** Do NOT swallow them.
8. **Rest for 1 minute.** Repeat the cycle 2 to 3 times if needed.

**Breathing.** During percussion: normal breathing. After: deep belly breaths + huffs.

**What she should feel.**
- *Normal:* gentle vibration through the chest; mild urge to cough (good — bring it up); secretions may come out (good); no pain.
- *Abnormal:* sharp pain at the surgical sites or the chest (stop, percussion may be too hard); breathlessness (stop, sit upright, breathe normally); blood in secretions (stop, call doctor).

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep.**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Cupped hand (hollow sound when clapping) | Flat hand (sharp slap, painful) |
| Rhythmic 2 claps per second | Random fast slapping |
| All lung areas covered | Only one spot done |
| Huff after percussion | No huff (secretions stay loose but not moved out) |

**Dosage.**
- If she has cough/wheeze: 10 minutes per session, 1 session per day, until cough resolves.
- Preventive (no symptoms): 5 minutes per session, 3 times per week, for the first 2 weeks.

**Progression rules.**
- Stop entirely once she has had no cough, no wheeze, and clear chest sounds for 5 days running.

**Common mistakes (with fixes).**
- *Flat hand instead of cupped* → causes pain. Cupped only.
- *Slapping too hard* → gentle, rhythmic.
- *Skipping the huff* → the percussion only works if followed by huffs to move the secretions out.
- *Doing it lying down* → must be sitting upright (gravity helps).

**Caregiver assistance — exact phrases.**
- At the start: *"Sit upright. Hug the pillow. I'll clap your back gently."*
- During percussion: *"Tell me if it hurts. I'll go softer."*
- After percussion: *"Take 3 deep breaths. Now huff — like fogging up a mirror. Huff. Huff. Huff."*
- *"If anything comes up, spit it into the basin. Don't swallow."*

**You know it's working when…** secretions come up; cough becomes more productive (wet, with secretions); within 3 to 5 days, the cough clears.

**Safety notes.**
- *Bilateral TKR:* fully safe; no knee load.
- *Chronic LBP:* sitting upright with lumbar roll is back-friendly.
- *Modifications if pain occurs:* chest pain → STOP, gentle percussion only, call doctor if persists. Blood in secretions → STOP, call doctor.

**When to skip this exercise today.** Skip if she has no cough, no wheeze, and clear chest sounds. Otherwise do 1 session per day until clear.

### Pre-exercise pain plan

1. **Scheduled NAC-SR (diclofenac SR) twice daily at 8:30 AM and 8:30 PM — never skip.** This provides the background analgesia.
2. **Do NOT take extra NSAID before exercise** — steady-state plasma levels are already high enough.
3. **Ultracet BEFORE exercise — only if:**
   - Pain at rest is > 4/10, OR
   - The session is the day's hardest (introducing new exercises, bridging, stretching, etc.), AND

## Section 11 — Functional skills (quick reference) and ice/elevation/pillow technique

### Functional skills — the 7 basics

These are the daily-life skills she must do safely from Day 1. Each has a single-rule summary.

#### Bed mobility (log roll)

The spine MUST move as one unit. No twisting.

1. **Pre-brace abs** (Ex 15 — 3-in-1 brace).
2. **Pillow between knees** (firm, mid-thigh to ankle).
3. **Roll knees, hips, shoulders together as ONE unit** to the side.
4. **Lower both legs together** off the side of the bed.
5. **Push up with the bottom elbow, then the top hand.**
6. **Pause 30 seconds at the bed edge** (orthostatic check — sit before standing).

Reverse for getting in: sit at edge of bed, log-roll back to supine, lift both legs together onto the bed.

#### Sit-to-stand (see Exercise 13)

Nose over toes. Brace abs. Push through ARMRESTS (never walker). Slow, controlled.

#### Walker walking (see Exercise 12)

Walker forward 25 to 30 cm → less-painful leg first → other leg meets. Inside the walker frame. Small steps. Stand tall, head up.

#### Stair climbing (from Week 3 onwards)

UP: lead with the less-painful leg.
DOWN: lead with the more-painful leg.
**Both feet on the same step for the first 6 weeks** (step-to pattern).
Handrail on both sides if available.
Husband BELOW her always (catches if she falls forward).

#### Toilet

Western: raised seat (5 to 7 cm add-on) + side armrests; total seat height 48 to 52 cm. Push armrests on the way up.
Indian squat: NEVER squat. Use a commode chair OVER the pan.

#### Car (passenger seat for the first 6 to 8 weeks)

Passenger seat, push it all the way back. Add a firm cushion for height + a plastic sheet over the seat for easy sliding.
Swing both legs in together (don't twist — log-roll).
Long journeys: stop every 90 minutes for a 10-step walker walk + 20 ankle pumps.

#### Bathing

Week 1 to 2: sponge bath, wound covered.
Week 2+: seated bath on a plastic chair, wound covered by a waterproof dressing.
After suture removal (Day 15): normal seated bath.
Tub or pool immersion: Week 6+ (after surgeon clearance).

### Ice technique (detailed)

**What you need:**
- Zip-lock plastic bag OR clean cloth bag.
- Ice cubes or crushed ice (~2 cups).
- 2 to 3 spoons water (optional — helps the bag mould to the knee).
- Thin cotton cloth or thin towel for wrapping.

**Never:**
- Direct ice on skin.
- Ice on an open wound.
- More than 20 minutes per session.

**How to make:**
1. Ice + water in the bag.
2. Push the air out, seal.
3. Wrap the bag in ONE thin cloth.

**How to use:**
1. Lie on back, legs elevated (Ex 4 position).
2. Place ice pack on the knee — cover front, sides, behind if possible.
3. Timer: 15 to 20 minutes.
4. Remove. Check skin — should be pink and cold, not white or with red blotches.
5. Pat dry.

**When:**
- After every exercise session — 15 minutes.
- Any time the knee feels hot, throbbing, or swollen.
- Before sleep (helps sleep).
- Weeks 1 to 2: 4 to 6 times per day.
- Weeks 3 to 4: 2 to 3 times per day.

**Stop if:** skin is white/waxy, red blotches (sensitivity), itching/burning.

### Leg elevation (see Exercise 4 for full instructions)

The single sentence to remember: **pillow under CALF and HEEL, never under the knee.** Foot 15 to 30 cm above the heart.

### The small lumbar roll (for her LBP)

**What:** rolled bath towel, ~10 cm thick, rolled tight, tied with string. Or a McKenzie-style commercial lumbar roll (₹500 to ₹900 from Amazon).

**How to use:**
- When sitting in firm chair > 10 minutes.
- When lying supine (during passive knee extension, leg elevation, breathing) — if her back feels arched.
- Position: fills the natural curve at L4–L5 (just above the pelvic rim).
- Test: she should feel "supported into the chair," not pushed forward.

**Remove when:**
- Doing quad sets (back must be flat against the bed).
- During pelvic tilts (she needs to feel the back press into the bed).
- During mini-bridge (back must press into the bed).
- During sleep ONLY if she doesn't need it for comfort (if back feels fine without it, remove).

### Pillow placement rules (the golden rules)

| Situation | Pillow goes... | Pillow does NOT go... |
|---|---|---|
| Sleeping supine | Under calf and ankle (calf pillow stack) | Under the BACK OF THE KNEE — ever |
| Sleeping on side (Day 5+) | A FIRM pillow between the calves and ankles (mid-thigh to ankle) | Between the thighs only (knees still bend uncomfortably) |
| Leg elevation (Ex 4) | Under calf and heel (the ramp shape) | Under the back of the knee |
| Passive extension (Ex 8) | Under the heel only (calf in the air) | Under the calf or knee |
| Quad sets (Ex 5) | Under the head (thin), and optionally a folded towel under each heel | NEVER under the knee |
