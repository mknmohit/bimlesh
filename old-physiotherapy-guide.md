## SECTION 0 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (read first, in 2 minutes)

### What's different about THIS guide vs generic TKR guides

Generic TKR rehabilitation guides list 15–20 exercises "to start on Day 1." That is unrealistic for a patient who:
- Just finished a 4–8 hour car journey from Patparganj to rural UP the night of 19-Apr,
- Is exhausted, swollen, still on strong pain medications,
- Has **both knees operated** (no "good leg" to compensate),
- Has **30 years of chronic low back pain** that changes how she can lie, sit, and move,
- Has **inflammatory arthritis** (soft tissues heal 20–30 % slower than in osteoarthritis),
- Is on **Apixaban** (so any fall is more dangerous for 14 days) and **Teriparatide** (so no high-impact ever).

**The honest truth: Day 1 at home is a recovery day. Total exercise ≈ 10–15 minutes.** Then we build slowly.

### The 10 golden rules

1. **Little and often wins.** 3–5 short bouts (10–15 min each) beats one long session.
2. **Stop if pain rises above 4/10** during an exercise, or lasts more than 2 hours afterwards.
3. **Extension matters more than bending.** A knee that cannot straighten fully causes lifelong limping. Never place a pillow directly under the knee.
4. **Never hold the breath during an exercise.** She's on Apixaban — holding breath raises blood pressure and bleeding risk.
5. **Every exercise has an "earliest safe day."** Don't skip ahead to feel progress faster — that causes swelling flares.
6. **Low back pain changes 3 positions specifically**: no pure supine legs-straight for long periods (use a small lumbar roll), no prone lying, and knee-to-chest instead of deep flexion stretches.
7. **Ice 15–20 min after every session.** Elevate legs between sessions. Non-negotiable.
8. **Walker stays for 4–6 weeks.** Don't rush to cane or unaided walking.
9. **Best exercise times:** 10:00–11:30 AM (after morning NSAID absorbs) and 2:30–4:30 PM (after lunch analgesia peak). AVOID exercise within 4 hours of any Ultracet dose (fall risk from drowsiness) and anytime after 9 PM (Gabapin-NT sedation).
10. **If in doubt — STOP, ice, elevate, call Mrs. Kiran.** The cost of calling unnecessarily is zero.

### The "2-2-2 rule" for knowing if you did too much

Three objective checks you do EVERY day, same time (best is 9:00 AM before breakfast cluster):

| Check | Normal | Too much |
|---|---|---|
| **Pain 2 hours after exercise** (ask her on a 0–10 scale) | Back to her baseline, or ≤ 2 points higher | **> 2 points higher 2 hours later** = dose was too big |
| **Soreness duration** | Fades within 2 hours | **Soreness lasting > 2 hours** = cut next session's volume by 30–50 % |
| **Knee size** (measure with soft tape across the middle of the kneecap, same time daily) | Gradually decreasing week by week | **> 2 cm increase from yesterday's morning** = full rest day + ice Q2h |

If ANY of the three is "too much," take ONE complete rest day (only ankle pumps, breathing, elevation, ice), then restart at **HALF** the previous volume. Build back up 1 step per day. **Do not try to "catch up" missed volume.**

## SECTION 2 — LOW BACK PAIN: HOW THIS GUIDE ADAPTS TO IT

Your chronic low back pain (30 years since childbirth, "bearable") changes roughly 30 % of a standard TKR program. Here's what we've customized specifically for you.

### What's actually causing the pain (after 30 years)

Post-partum low back pain lasting this long is almost never the original tissue damage anymore. The ligament laxity from pregnancy hormones, any diastasis recti, and pelvic floor trauma have usually stabilized by year 2–3. What persists at year 30 is typically one or more of:

1. **Deconditioned deep core muscles** — the transverse abdominis (TrA) and multifidus (deep back muscles) lost their firing pattern after childbirth and never retrained. Meta-analyses show core stabilization exercises reduce pain 4.4 points on a 10-point scale vs 1.8 for generic physiotherapy.
2. **Sacroiliac joint dysfunction** — the SI joints became more mobile during pregnancy (relaxin hormone). Accounts for 16–30 % of chronic LBP; more common in women who have given birth. The glutes normally stabilize the SI joint via the sacrotuberous ligament — and post-partum glutes often never fully reactivate ("glute inhibition"), leaving the SI joints unsupported.
3. **Lumbar facet arthropathy** — age 53 + 30 years of uneven loading = facet joints wear down. These little joints at the back of the spine are loaded most by extension (arching back, prone lying). This is why we SKIP prone hangs.
4. **Gluteal inhibition** — the buttock muscles don't fire properly, so the lower back muscles (erector spinae) over-work to stabilize. Retraining the glutes offloads the back.

**What this means for her rehab:** The best LBP treatment for her specific case is a combination of **core stabilization** (3-in-1 brace, pelvic tilts) + **pelvic floor retraining** (Kegels) + **glute strengthening** (mini-bridges, hip extension) + **hamstring stretching** (tight hamstrings flex the pelvis backward, loading the low back).

These are all SAFE post-TKR and all INCLUDED in the plan below.

### What we SKIP (that standard guides would include)

- **Prone hangs / face-down knee extension** — increases low-back arch and loads painful facet joints.
- **Long supine sessions with legs straight** — increases low-back curve and compresses discs.
- **Straight-leg raise (SLR) as a counted exercise in Weeks 1–2** — pulls pelvis into arch. We use quad sets + towel-under-heel as a substitute for extension work, and introduce SLR only once your core brace is reliable.
- **"Double knee-to-chest"** stretch — forces full bilateral knee flexion which your implants can't yet tolerate. Single knee-to-chest is fine (from Week 2).
- **Deep bridges / full hip-lifts.** Mini-bridges (3–5 cm lift) only, and only from Week 2.

### What we UPGRADE (because it helps BOTH your knees and back)

- **Pelvic tilts** (flattening lower back into bed) — usually 10 × 2 per day, we do **10 × 3–4 per day** for you. Reduces low-back pain AND activates deep core.
- **Abdominal bracing** — upgraded to the **"3-in-1 brace"**: deep breathing + belly draw-in + Kegel together. Best-evidence core exercise for postpartum LBP.
- **Kegels (pelvic floor)** — added from Day 1. Strongest evidence for postpartum LBP. Safe post-TKR (no knee load).
- **Diaphragmatic (belly) breathing** — same exercise as the respiratory physio, but we extend it to 10 breaths and combine it with the belly draw-in.
- **Supine hamstring stretch from Week 2** — tight hamstrings worsen low back pain. Gentle version with a towel around the foot, while the other knee stays slightly bent.
- **Single knee-to-chest stretch from Week 2** — classic LBP pain-reliever; also gives the knee gentle bending practice.
- **Seated cat-cow spinal mobility from Week 2** — substitutes for the standard cat-cow which requires kneeling (forbidden post-TKR).
- **Mini-bridge from Week 3** (with strict glute-first technique) — helps glutes support the sacroiliac joint, reducing chronic LBP cause.

### Sleep position — how we resolve the conflict

**The conflict:** Standard TKR rule says "sleep supine with legs STRAIGHT, no pillow under knees." Standard LBP advice says "supine with knees bent, pillow under knees."

**Our solution:**

- **Primary sleep position (Weeks 1–2): SUPINE with legs STRAIGHT** (TKR rule wins — knee extension is a "one-way door": once lost, very hard to recover).
- **Modifications within that constraint:**
  - **Small folded hand towel (5 cm thick)** tucked under the LOW BACK at L4–L5 level. Fills the natural curve — does NOT bend the knee.
  - **Calf pillow stack slightly higher** so the knees rest at about 5–10° soft flexion (NOT under the knee, but the pillow stops just before the knee). This tiny knee bend reduces low-back arch without creating a flexion contracture.
  - **Semi-Fowler's position (30–45° reclined)** using extra pillows behind the upper body is an excellent alternative — removes the low-back arch entirely while keeping knees straight. Use this for the first 5–7 nights if it's comfortable.
- **Side-lying: SAFE from Day 5–7 onward for 20–30 min periods**, with:
  - A **firm pillow between the knees from mid-thigh to ankle** (keeps knees near-straight AND reduces hip adduction pressure).
  - Knees slightly bent (20–30°) — NOT fetal position.
  - Sleep on either side (no "operative side" distinction since both are operative).
- **Daytime only — short-bout hook-lying (knees fully bent, feet flat)** for LBP relief: 10–20 minutes at a time, then straighten legs. This is a daytime REST position, NEVER overnight.
- **NEVER: sleeping with a pillow UNDER the knees overnight** (forbidden, flexion deformity risk).
- **NEVER: prone (face-down)** (both knee flexion problem AND low-back arch problem).

### Exercise 1 — Deep Belly Breathing (Diaphragmatic Breathing)

**Why this exercise**
After surgery, the lungs get a little lazy from lying down and anaesthesia. This exercise opens the bottom of the lungs. It also calms the body, lowers pain, and activates the deep core muscles that protect your low back. It is the foundation of every other exercise — if she can breathe correctly, every other exercise gets easier and safer.

**What you need**
- One thin pillow for the head.
- (Optional) A small rolled towel — 5 cm thick — for under the low back.
- A quiet room (no TV, no phone).

**Starting position**
1. Lie flat on the back on the bed.
2. Put one thin pillow under the head.
3. Place the small rolled towel under the low back so it fills the natural curve. This protects the low back.
4. Both legs stretched out long, feet relaxed, toes pointing to the ceiling.
5. Arms rest beside the body, palms up.
6. Place ONE hand flat on the belly, just below the belly button. Place the OTHER hand flat on the chest.
7. Close the mouth. Breathe gently through the nose for 2 normal breaths first — let her settle.

**Imagine this**
Imagine your belly is a slow balloon. When you breathe IN, the balloon fills and rises. When you breathe OUT, the balloon empties and falls. The balloon is BELOW the belly button, not in the chest.

**The movement — step by step**
1. **BREATHE IN** slowly through the nose. Count silently with her: "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand, three-one-thousand, four-one-thousand." The belly should rise like a balloon filling with air. The hand on the belly should go UP. The hand on the chest should stay almost still.
2. **PAUSE** for one second at the top of the breath (don't strain — just pause naturally).
3. **BREATHE OUT** slowly through the mouth, with lips softly pursed (like blowing on a hot cup of tea — NOT like blowing out a candle). Count: "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand, three-one-thousand, four-one-thousand, five-one-thousand, six-one-thousand." The belly should go DOWN as you exhale.
4. **PAUSE** for one second at the bottom.
5. Repeat from step 1. That's 1 breath.

**Breathing rule:** In through NOSE, out through MOUTH. The out-breath is always LONGER than the in-breath (count 6 out, 4 in). This is what calms the body.

**How it should FEEL**
- Belly rising and falling like a slow balloon.
- Shoulders stay soft and LOW. They do NOT rise toward ears.
- Chest almost still.
- A slight warmth in the belly as you continue.
- Feel calm, maybe a little sleepy — this is GOOD. The body is relaxing.
- Should NOT feel dizzy. If dizzy, STOP this exercise and breathe normally for 1 minute.

**Caregiver says (exact phrases to use)**
- At the start: *"Put your hand on your belly. Let me put my hand on top. Feel my hand go up and down with your breath."*
- During the breath in: *"Breathe into my hand. Make it go up."*
- If the chest is moving more than the belly: *"Breathe LOWER. Push the belly up, not the chest."*
- If the shoulders are lifting: *"Shoulders down. Soft shoulders."*
- If she's getting faster: *"Slow. Slow breath. Count with me — one, two, three, four."*
- At the end of each breath: *"Good. Let it all out. Belly goes down."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD rep looks like | BAD rep looks like |
|---|---|
| Belly hand rises clearly, chest hand barely moves | Chest hand moves more than belly hand (reverse breathing) |
| Shoulders relaxed, flat on bed | Shoulders lifting toward ears |
| Out-breath noticeably longer than in-breath | Both breaths the same length, rushed |
| 6–8 breaths take a full minute | 10 breaths done in under 30 seconds (too fast) |
| Face calm, body sinking into bed | Face tense, body rigid, breath held |

**Common mistakes and how to fix them**

| Problem | What to do |
|---|---|
| Chest rising more than belly | Have her place a small book on her belly. Breathing in must lift the book. |
| Breath held at the top | Say "1-second pause only — then let it out." Don't encourage long breath holds. |
| Getting dizzy after 3–4 breaths | Too deep or too fast. Take 3 normal breaths, then resume gently. |
| Shoulders lifting toward ears | Caregiver gently places hands on her shoulders, applies slight downward pressure as she breathes in. |
| Belly not moving at all | She's breath-holding. Say "Say 'haaaa' slowly as you breathe out." Speaking forces the breath to flow. |
| Nose blocked (cold/allergies) | Breathe in through a slightly open mouth. Still works. |

**You know it's working when...**
- After 10 breaths, she reports feeling calmer or a bit sleepy.
- Her resting heart rate drops 5–10 beats per minute.
- Her face color looks softer/pinker (blood flow improves).
- Knee pain score (0–10) drops by 1–2 points after a 10-breath set.

**Dose**
- 10 breaths × 4 sessions per day (morning, pre-lunch, evening, pre-bed). Total: 40 breaths daily.
- Can also do it any time she feels pain or anxiety — takes 2 minutes, no equipment needed.

**Time per session:** About 3 minutes for 10 breaths.

**Progression**
- Week 1: 4-second in, 6-second out. 10 breaths × 4 sessions.
- Week 2: 4-second in, 8-second out (longer exhale = deeper calming).
- Week 4+: If comfortable, hold breath at top 2 seconds → still 8-second out.

**When to stop**
- Feeling dizzy → breathe normally 1 minute, then resume slower.
- Coughing fit → sit up, sip water, rest.
- Chest pain or sudden sharp breathlessness → STOP and call doctor (rare).

### Exercise 2 — Ankle Pumps

**Why this exercise**
Moving feet up and down pumps blood back to the heart. This is called the "calf pump." When the calf muscles squeeze, they push the blood upwards out of the legs. This stops blood clots (DVT) — the most serious post-surgery complication. **Despite being on Apixaban, DVT can still happen.** This is the single most important exercise in the first 2 weeks.

**What you need**
- The bed (or a chair if sitting).
- (Optional) Small rolled towel under low back for comfort.

**Starting position**
1. Lie flat on the back.
2. Head on thin pillow.
3. Both legs long and straight.
4. Feet relaxed, toes to ceiling.
5. Small rolled towel under low back if it helps.
6. Hands resting beside the body or folded on belly — whatever is comfortable.

**Imagine this**
Imagine you are pressing a car brake pedal with the ball of your foot, then releasing it to pull your toes up toward your nose. Press. Release. Press. Release. Slow and rhythmic — about one full pump every 4 seconds.

**The movement — step by step**
1. **PULL UP**: Pull BOTH feet up toward the face, as if pointing toes at your own nose. Count: "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand." You should feel a gentle stretch in the back of the calves.
2. **POINT DOWN**: Point BOTH feet down and away, as if gently pressing a car brake pedal with the ball of the foot. Count: "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand."
3. **UP again** — "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand."
4. **DOWN again** — "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand."
5. Keep going. Only the feet move. Knees stay still. Thighs stay still.

**Breathing**
Breathe normally through the nose. If she holds the breath, tell her to hum softly — humming means breathing. Or ask her to say "haan" after every 5 pumps.

**How it should FEEL**
- Gentle stretch in back of calf when foot pulls up (toward nose).
- Gentle squeeze in front of shin when foot pulls up (the muscle by the shinbone).
- Soft stretch in top of foot when pointing down.
- A "warmth" sensation in the calves after 15–20 pumps — this is blood flowing, which is GOOD.
- NOTHING in the knee — knee stays quiet.
- NO cramp in the foot or calf.

**Caregiver says (exact phrases)**
- At the start: *"Pretend your feet are pressing a car brake. Press — release. Press — release. Only feet move."*
- If toes-only moving: *"Whole ankle. Move from the ankle, not just toes."*
- If knee moves: *"Knee stays still. I'll keep my hand here on your thigh — don't move the thigh."*
- If she's going fast: *"Slow. Say one-two with me. One — two. Count."*
- If she holds breath: *"Hum a song. Any song. Humming means breathing."*
- If she asks when to stop: *"20 pumps. Let's count — 1, 2, 3..."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD rep looks like | BAD rep looks like |
|---|---|
| Whole foot moves — pulls up and pushes down | Only toes wiggle; ankle doesn't move |
| Calf visibly hardens each time foot points down | Calf stays floppy |
| Knee and thigh stay flat and still | Leg lifts off bed during the pump |
| Slow and rhythmic, ~4 seconds per full pump | Fast and jerky, ~1 second per pump |
| Breathing normally | Breath held; face red |

**Common mistakes and how to fix them**

| Problem | What to do |
|---|---|
| Only toes move | Caregiver places flat hand on the foot; must feel the ANKLE joint moving, not toes |
| Knee moves with each pump | Place flat hand on front of thigh — thigh must stay still |
| Cramp in calf or foot | STOP. Gently rub the cramped muscle. Rest 2 min. Reduce next set to 10 pumps. |
| Getting bored/tired | Remind her: "This is the most important exercise. Every pump prevents a blood clot." |
| Pain in knee | Don't move the knee — feet ONLY. If pain persists, tell surgeon. |

**You know it's working when...**
- Calf muscle visibly hardens with each pump (can see it move under the skin).
- A gentle warmth feels in the legs after 20 pumps.
- Swelling gradually reduces over weeks.

**Dose**
- **EVERY 1–2 HOURS while awake for the first 2 weeks.** That's 8–12 times per day.
- Each session: **20 pumps per leg** (about 1.5 minutes).
- Can do both legs together or alternate.
- Total per day: ~200–240 pumps per leg.
- Continue at this frequency until ALL swelling has resolved (often 3+ months).

**⚠ Red flag during/after ankle pumps:**
- Sudden sharp pain in one calf (not both) = STOP, call doctor (possible DVT).
- One calf visibly larger/redder/warmer than the other = call doctor same day.
- Chest pain or sudden breathlessness = call 011-43033333 (possible pulmonary embolism — emergency).

### Exercise 3 — Ankle Circles

**Why this exercise**
Keeps ankle joints moving in all directions (not just up-down like ankle pumps). Prevents stiffness from prolonged bed rest. Also adds to the calf pump effect.

**What you need:** Same as ankle pumps — just bed, head pillow, optional lumbar roll.

**Starting position**
Same as ankle pumps (lying on back, legs long, feet relaxed).

**Imagine this**
Imagine your big toe is a pen, and you are drawing a big slow circle on the ceiling. The circle should be BIG (moving the whole foot, not just toes) and SLOW (one circle per 6 seconds).

**The movement — step by step**
1. **Start with the RIGHT foot.**
2. Move the foot to point toward the RIGHT (outside) — count "1."
3. Then move it DOWN (toes pointing away from face) — count "2."
4. Then move it to the LEFT (inside) — count "3."
5. Then UP (toes pointing toward face) — count "4."
6. That's one full circle. Repeat 9 more times going the SAME direction.
7. Reverse — make 10 circles the OTHER direction.
8. Switch to the LEFT foot. Same pattern.
9. Can do both feet together once comfortable (mirror each other, or same direction).

**Breathing:** Normal nasal breathing.

**How it should FEEL**
- Gentle pulling around the ankle in all directions.
- Some clicking or soft popping is NORMAL and safe — it's the joint fluid moving.
- NO pain in the knee.
- NO cramp in the foot arch.

**Caregiver says**
- At the start: *"Draw a big circle with your big toe. BIG circle, not small. Slow."*
- If circles are small: *"Bigger. Whole ankle moves."*
- If she gets dizzy (rare): *"Stop. Rest. Look at one spot on the ceiling."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Big, slow circle (one circle per 6 seconds) | Tiny fast circles (toe wiggles only) |
| Whole ankle joint moves | Only toes move |
| Clockwise first, then anticlockwise | Random direction changes |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Foot cramps | Stop, gently massage the arch of the foot, continue with smaller range |
| Knee moves with ankle | Place pillow under knee to keep thigh still — hand on thigh if needed |

**You know it's working when:** Ankle feels looser; blood flow warmth in the foot.

**Dose**
- 10 circles clockwise + 10 anticlockwise, each foot.
- 3 times per day.
- Can pair with ankle pump sessions (do circles for 2 min, then pumps for 2 min).

**Time per session:** ~5 min.

**Progression:** Not needed. Keeps circles slow and big throughout recovery.

**When to stop:** Sharp ankle pain; unilateral calf pain (possible DVT — see ankle pumps red flag).

### Exercise 4 — Leg Elevation (Rest Position — read carefully)

**Why this exercise**
Uses gravity to drain swelling out of the knees back toward the heart. Swelling causes pain and limits how much the knee can bend/straighten — controlling swelling is half the job of recovery. This is NOT really an "exercise" — it is a REST position that does work while she rests.

**What you need**
- 2–3 firm pillows OR one long bolster.
- One thin pillow for the head.
- (Optional) Small rolled towel for low back.
- Timer or phone (to end session after 20–30 min).

### ⚠ THE ONE BIG RULE — NEVER place a pillow DIRECTLY UNDER the knee.

A pillow under the knee bends the knee. Keeping the knee bent for hours/days makes the tissues behind the knee short. A short knee cannot straighten — ever. This is "flexion contracture" and is the #1 cause of permanent limping after knee surgery.

**TEST:** Look at her knee from the side. If you can see the back of the knee NOT touching anything (air between knee and pillow), correct. If you can see the pillow pressing INTO the back of the knee, WRONG — fix it.

### The CORRECT way — step by step

1. **Sit on the edge of the bed with husband's help.**
2. **Arrange the pillows in a gentle ramp on the bed:**
   - Pillow 1: flat in the middle of the bed. Supports the back of the THIGH (upper leg), NOT under the knee.
   - Pillow 2: stacked slightly lower down the bed. Supports the back of the CALF.
   - Pillow 3 (optional): smaller pillow at the end, under the ANKLE and HEEL.
   - Shape looks like an upward ramp: low at hips, higher at calves, highest at ankles.
3. **Swing both legs up onto the pillows** (husband supports calves).
4. **Lie back on the thin head pillow.**
5. **CHECK THE KNEE** (caregiver looks from the side of the bed):
   - Knee must look STRAIGHT — a clear straight line from thigh to shin.
   - Nothing directly under the back of the knee. The knee hangs in the air, supported above (thigh on pillow) and below (calf on pillow).
6. **CHECK THE HEIGHT:**
   - The foot should be HIGHER than the belly button.
7. **For her low back:** tuck the small rolled towel under the L4–L5 curve of the back (only if the back feels arched/uncomfortable without it).
8. **CHECK COMFORT:**
   - No numbness or pins-and-needles in the foot. If yes → pillow is pressing on a nerve. Move the pillow down so it's under the calf, not crossing the back of the knee.
   - No low back pain. If yes → pillows are too high; lower them slightly.

**Imagine this**
Imagine a gentle slide. Her hips are at the bottom (on the bed). Her feet are at the top (in the air, above the heart). Gravity pulls the swelling DOWNWARD off the feet and knees, back toward the heart. No muscle work — just gravity doing its job.

**Caregiver says**
- Setting up: *"Lie back. I'm going to put the pillows under your thighs and calves — nothing behind your knees."*
- Checking: *"Can I pass my flat hand behind your knee? If yes, position is perfect."*
- If she complains of pins-and-needles: *"I'm moving the pillow down a bit. Tell me if that helps."*
- If she complains of low back pain: *"I'm putting a small towel under your lower back. Better?"*

**GOOD position vs BAD position**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Side view: clear air gap behind knee | Side view: pillow pressing into back of knee |
| Foot clearly HIGHER than belly button | Foot at same level or lower than belly |
| Thigh on pillow, calf on pillow, heel on small pillow | Just one pillow under the knee (WRONG — causes contracture) |
| Low back feels neutral (not arched, not flat) | Low back arched and hurting |
| Toes pink, not tingly | Toes white/tingly (nerve compression) |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Foot tingles / goes numb | Pillow is pressing behind knee. Move pillow DOWN so it only touches calf. |
| Low back starts arching / hurting | Too much elevation, OR pillow placed too low (forcing back to arch). Lower height; add small towel at L4–L5. |
| She can't lie still for 20 min | Combine with an audio book, phone call, or music. Set timer. |
| Both knees drift outward (toes pointing outward) | Place small rolled towel between ankles to keep them aligned. |
| Gets cold lying still | Cover legs with a light blanket (not heavy — heavy = pressure). |

**You know it's working when:** Knees look less swollen after 30 min. Skin tension reduces. Pain drops by 1–2 points (0–10).

**Dose**
- **Weeks 1–2:** 20–30 min per session × 4–5 sessions per day (total 1.5–2 hours of elevation/day).
- **Weeks 3–4:** 20 min × 3 times per day.
- **From Week 5:** Only when swelling or pain is up (especially after walks).

**Combine with ankle pumps** every 5–10 minutes during elevation (rhythmic calf pump × elevation = even better drainage).

**Best times:**
- After every exercise session (cool-down).
- After every walker walk.
- Between meals.
- As a mid-afternoon rest.
- Before bed (30 min helps sleep).

**When to stop the session:**
- After 30 min (limit — longer is not better, and prolonged supine aggravates low back).
- If she gets numb toes → adjust pillows immediately.
- If low back pain starts → take a break, reset pillow/towel position.

### Exercise 5 — Quadriceps Sets (Thigh Muscle Squeeze)

**Why this exercise**
After knee surgery, the front thigh muscle (quadriceps) goes "to sleep" — this is called "arthrogenic muscular inhibition." The muscle can't fire properly because the swelling and pain signals tell it to relax. If this muscle stays asleep for too long, it shrinks fast (within 7 days there is measurable muscle loss). A sleepy quadriceps means a wobbly knee that buckles. This exercise is how we WAKE UP the quadriceps without moving the knee joint — safe from Day 1.

**What you need**
- The bed. One thin head pillow. 
- **NO rolled towel under the knees for this exercise** — the back of the knee must be able to press into the bed.
- (Optional) Small lumbar roll under low back if needed.

**Starting position**
1. Lie flat on back.
2. Thin pillow under head.
3. Both legs stretched out long, STRAIGHT. REMOVE any pillow under the knees.
4. Small rolled towel under low back if the back feels arched/uncomfortable.
5. Arms beside body.
6. Feet relaxed, toes up.

**Imagine this**
Imagine there is a small grape placed under the back of the right knee, and you have to squash it flat by pressing the back of your knee into the bed. At the same time, the muscle on the FRONT of your thigh becomes HARD like a stone.

Or: Imagine a magic string pulling the back of your kneecap DOWN into the bed, while the top of your kneecap slides UP toward your hip.

**The movement — step by step**
Start with the right leg. (If she has LBP, brace abs first — see Exercise 15 technique.)

1. **Set up the foot:** Pull the toes of the right foot UP toward the face (this "locks" the ankle and helps the quad engage).
2. **Start the squeeze:** Very slowly, press the BACK of the right knee DOWN into the bed. Squash the grape. Count "one-one-thousand."
3. **Feel the thigh harden:** At the same time, the FRONT of the right thigh becomes HARD like a stone. Caregiver can lightly place a flat hand on front of thigh and feel the muscle harden.
4. **Hold the squeeze:** Count "two-one-thousand, three-one-thousand, four-one-thousand, five-one-thousand." (5-second hold in Week 1.)
5. **Release slowly:** Let go gently. Count "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand." The muscle softens.
6. **Rest 2 seconds.** The knee is now back to resting.
7. **Repeat.** That's 1 rep.
8. After 10 right-leg squeezes, switch to left leg.

**Breathing**
Breathe OUT through the mouth while squeezing (as the thigh tightens). Breathe IN through the nose while releasing. **NEVER hold breath** — raises BP and bleeding risk on Apixaban.

**How it should FEEL**
- **Strong "working" feeling** in the front of the thigh, from just above the knee up toward the hip.
- **Thigh feels HARD like a stone** when touched by the caregiver.
- **Back of knee presses FIRMLY into the bed.**
- **Knee cap** (patella) slides slightly UP toward the hip — this is normal and shows the muscle is firing correctly.
- Should NOT feel pain INSIDE the knee joint. Small pulling behind the knee is okay.
- Should NOT feel anything in the low back. If yes → see troubleshoot.

**Caregiver says**
- At the start: *"Toes up to your nose. Now push the back of your knee DOWN, squash the grape."*
- While she squeezes: *"Hard like a stone. I feel it getting hard. Hold — 1, 2, 3, 4, 5."*
- To check: *"Let me see the muscle on the front of your thigh. Yes, it moved!"*
- If back arches: *"Gentle belly button toward spine — small belly squeeze."*
- If no thigh hardening: *"Think of pulling your kneecap UP to your hip at the same time."*
- If she's holding breath: *"Breathe OUT as you squeeze. Hiss like a snake: ssssss."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD rep | BAD rep |
|---|---|
| Thigh visibly hardens, kneecap slides UP | Thigh stays soft; no visible change |
| Back of knee presses down — can see it press into bed | Knee lifts off bed (leg rising) |
| Heel stays on bed | Heel lifts off bed |
| Slow 5-second hold | Quick tap-and-release |
| Breathing freely | Face red, breath held |
| Low back relaxed | Low back arches up off bed |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Can't get the thigh to harden at all | Try pulling toes up first (ankle set) — this often "turns on" the quad. Then try squeeze. |
| Thigh hardens but knee doesn't press down | Focus on squashing the grape under the knee. Caregiver lightly presses the top of the kneecap to give the sensation. |
| Low back arches | Bend non-exercising leg at the knee (foot flat on bed) — takes arch out of back. Or use lumbar roll towel. |
| Leg rises off bed (lifting instead of pressing) | Caregiver gently places hand on top of thigh — says "push my hand DOWN into the bed." |
| Knee pain inside the joint | Stop. Reduce hold time to 3 seconds. If still painful, delay by 1 day and retry. |
| Cramp in the thigh | Rest 1 minute. Gently rub the muscle. Resume with shorter hold. |
| Fatigue before finishing 10 reps | Reduce to 5 reps this session. Next session try 6. Build gradually. |

**You know it's working when...**
- After 1 week, she can hold the squeeze 10 seconds without fatigue.
- Caregiver can feel the thigh muscle has more "bulk" when touched vs Day 1.
- She can start the next exercise (SLR attempt) successfully.

**Dose**
- Week 1 (Day 1–7): **5–10 reps × 2 sets × 3 times per day.**
- Week 2 (Day 8–14): **10 reps × 3 sets × 3 times per day.**
- Week 3+: 10 reps × 3 sets × 3 sessions/day.
- Hold time: 5 sec Week 1 → 7 sec Week 2 → 10 sec Week 3+.

**Time per session:** ~10 min including rests.

### "SLR attempt" (same setup, different move) — for Days 1–9 only, as motor rehearsal

1. Do a quad set (as above) — thigh hardens, back of knee presses down.
2. **While the quad is tight,** try to lift the whole straight leg 1–2 inches off the bed. DO NOT LET THE KNEE BEND.
3. If the heel rises but the knee stays straight — GREAT, you're ready for true SLR soon.
4. If the heel rises and the knee DIPS first ("lag") — that's FINE, you are not ready for real SLR yet. Stop the attempt, just hold the quad set for 5 seconds and lower.
5. **Do NOT count reps.** Just attempt 3 times per side, once daily, to train the muscle memory.

**Why we don't force SLR early:** Forcing SLR when the quad isn't ready means other muscles take over (hip flexor, lower back) → the movement looks right but the quad isn't actually getting stronger. This "wrong motor pattern" can persist for weeks and DELAYS recovery. Better to do 9 days of quad sets first, then SLR emerges naturally around Day 10.

### True SLR (from Day 10 onwards) — ONLY if no lag

Same movement as SLR attempt, but now counted:
- 5 reps × 1 set per leg on Day 10 (first true attempt).
- 5 reps × 2 sets per leg Days 11–14.
- Hold at the top 3–5 sec.
- Slowly lower (count 3 seconds on the way down).
- Progress per Week 3+ plan.

**SLR red flag:** If any attempt causes sharp back pain, skip SLR for 3 days; focus on quad sets + 3-in-1 brace (Exercise 15) to strengthen core before retrying SLR.

### Exercise 6 — Gluteal Squeezes (Buttock Clench)

**Why this exercise**
Buttock muscles (gluteus maximus) help stand from chair, climb stairs, and SUPPORT THE LOW BACK. In post-partum women, these muscles often never fully reactivated after childbirth — meaning the low back muscles have been overworking for 30 years. This exercise wakes the glutes WITHOUT moving the knee — safe from Day 1 and directly addresses one root cause of her chronic LBP.

**What you need**
- Bed, thin head pillow.
- (Optional) Small rolled towel under low back.

**Starting position**
1. Lie on back.
2. Thin pillow under head.
3. Both legs straight OR slightly bent over small lumbar roll — her choice based on LBP comfort.
4. Arms beside body.
5. Feet relaxed.

**Imagine this**
Imagine there is a small one-rupee coin placed between your two buttocks, and you don't want it to fall. Squeeze both buttocks together firmly to hold the coin.

**The movement — step by step**
1. Breathe in through the nose (normal breath).
2. Start breathing OUT through the mouth, and as you breathe out, **slowly squeeze both buttocks together** — hold the imaginary coin tightly.
3. Count: "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand, three-one-thousand, four-one-thousand, five-one-thousand."
4. The whole buttock area becomes HARD. The hips may lift up by a tiny amount (maybe 1 cm) — this is fine.
5. Slowly release. Count: "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand."
6. Rest 2 seconds.
7. Repeat.

**Breathing:** OUT through mouth while squeezing. IN through nose while releasing. NEVER hold breath.

**How it should FEEL**
- A tight, working feeling in BOTH buttocks (not just one).
- Hands placed under the buttocks feel the muscle HARDEN like a stone.
- Some subtle warmth in the buttocks after 5 reps — good sign.
- Should NOT feel pain in the low back.
- Should NOT feel anything in the knee — knee stays completely quiet.
- Should NOT feel cramping.

**Caregiver says**
- At the start: *"Squeeze your bum together. Hold a coin between your buttocks."*
- During hold: *"Hold — 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Keep squeezing. Keep squeezing."*
- Breath reminder: *"Blow out while you squeeze. Don't hold your breath."*
- To check: *"I'll put my hand under your buttock — squeeze harder. I feel it tightening."*
- If only one side: *"Both sides. Left AND right squeeze together."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Both buttocks harden at the same time | Only one side squeezes |
| Heels stay flat on bed | Heels press down (making a bridge — wrong exercise) |
| 5-second hold feels strong | Quick tap (1-sec squeeze) |
| Face relaxed, breath flowing | Face red, breath held |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| One side squeezes more than other | Place hands under both buttocks; she can feel which side is weaker and focus on it. |
| Low back pain during squeeze | Bend both knees slightly (feet flat on bed). Also brace abs before squeezing. |
| Feet press into bed (making a bridge) | Caregiver places hand on top of foot — foot must stay RELAXED, not pressing. |
| Can't feel the buttocks harden | Try contracting buttocks and then release 5 times quickly first, then a slow 5-second hold. |
| Cramping in buttocks | Reduce hold to 3 sec. Rest 2 min between sets. |

**You know it's working when...**
- After 1 week, the buttocks feel firmer when touched.
- She can hold the squeeze 10 seconds without straining.
- Later (Week 4+), standing from a chair is noticeably easier.

**Dose**
- 10 reps × 2 sets × 3 times per day from Day 1.
- Hold: 5 sec Week 1 → 7 sec Week 2 → 10 sec Week 3+.
- Lifelong habit — do a few glute squeezes any time you're waiting in a chair.

### Exercise 7 — Heel Slides (Bending the Knee in Bed)

**Why this exercise**
Teaches the knee to bend again, safely, with the bed supporting the leg. This is the primary way to improve knee flexion (bending range) in the first 3 weeks. **Early flexion gains in these first 3 weeks predict your final 1-year ROM** — so do this exercise faithfully.

**What you need**
- Bed with smooth cotton sheet.
- Optional: plastic bag, polythene sheet, or silk cloth under the heel to reduce friction (very helpful in Week 1 when heel feels "stuck").
- (Optional) Small rolled towel under low back.

**Starting position**
1. Lie on back, both legs straight.
2. Thin pillow under head.
3. Arms beside body.
4. Small rolled towel under low back if it helps.
5. Place the plastic sheet or silk cloth under the right heel (if using).

**Imagine this**
Imagine you are slowly dragging your heel UP along the bed, bringing it closer to your buttock. The bed holds your heel — you don't lift the leg at all. Just slide, like drawing a slow line up the sheet.

**The movement — step by step**
Work on one leg at a time. Start with the **less-painful knee** first (easier — builds confidence).

1. Left leg stays straight and still.
2. Breathe IN.
3. As you breathe OUT, **slowly slide the right heel along the bed TOWARD the right buttock**. The knee bends as the heel slides up. Count: "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand, three-one-thousand, four-one-thousand."
4. **Stop at the FIRST strong stretch or pulling** — not at sharp pain. It should feel like "this is the edge," not "this hurts."
5. **Hold** at this maximum bent position for 5 seconds.
6. Breathe IN as you **slowly slide heel back DOWN** until leg is straight. Count 4 seconds.
7. Rest 3 seconds.
8. Repeat.
9. After 10 right-leg reps, do 10 left-leg reps.

**Breathing:** OUT while sliding heel UP (knee bends). IN while sliding heel DOWN (knee straightens). Never hold breath.

**How it should FEEL**
- Stretchy, pulling feeling at the FRONT of the knee and front of the thigh, just above the knee.
- Working feeling in the BACK of the thigh (hamstring is pulling the heel up).
- **Mild discomfort at end of bend is okay (2–3 out of 10).** Going past this into sharp pain is NOT okay.
- Should NOT feel sharp pain INSIDE the knee.
- Heel STAYS ON the bed the whole time — no lifting.

**Caregiver says**
- At the start: *"Slide your heel up toward your bum. Slow. Slide, don't lift."*
- If heel lifts: *"Heel down. Heel must touch bed."*
- If she goes too fast: *"Slow down. Count with me — 1, 2, 3, 4."*
- At the stretch point: *"Hold it there. Count — 1, 2, 3, 4, 5."*
- If she winces: *"Let it come back down. Don't force."*
- Encouragement: *"One more. Last one. Great."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Heel visibly drags along the bed | Heel lifts off the bed (turns into SLR, wrong exercise) |
| Slow 4-second slide up | Fast 1-second slide |
| Stops at first stretch, not pain | Forces through sharp pain |
| Toes and knee point straight UP | Knee falls outward or inward |
| 5-second hold at the top | No hold, immediate slide down |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Heel feels stuck on sheet | Use plastic bag / polythene sheet under the heel to reduce friction. |
| Heel lifts off bed | Caregiver places hand lightly on top of foot — "heel stays down." |
| Knee falls outward | Place a pillow on the outside of the thigh (prevents lateral drift). |
| Sharp pain at the top | You've gone too far. Come back, stop 10° earlier next time. |
| Low back arches during slide | Bend the OTHER leg at the knee (foot flat on bed) — this anchors the pelvis. |
| Muscle cramp in back of thigh | Stop. Gentle calf stretch. Rest 2 min. Resume with shorter slide. |
| Knee doesn't bend much (limited ROM) | This is expected early. Try doing it after a warm shower or heat pack (10 min heat pack on the knee before). |

**You know it's working when...**
- Each week, the heel reaches a little closer to the buttock.
- Week 1: heel reaches about 1/4 way up.
- Week 2: about 1/3 way up.
- Week 4: about halfway.
- Week 6: about 2/3 up.
- Mark the sheet with a pencil at the maximum heel position — track progress.

**Dose**
- Week 1: 5–10 reps × 1–2 sets × 2–3 times per day (start cautiously).
- Week 2: 10 reps × 2–3 sets × 3 times per day.
- Week 3: 10–15 reps × 3 sets × 3 times per day.
- Continue 3×/day until flexion goal is reached (typically Week 6–8).

**Best time to do heel slides:** Early afternoon (1:30–3:00 PM) when analgesia is at peak plasma level — bends feel easier, ROM gains are better.

**When to STOP** (red flags specific to heel slides)
- Loud pop or click with sudden pain
- Knee can't straighten back out after bending
- Sudden severe swelling after a session
- Pain > 5/10 during the slide

### Exercise 8 — Passive Knee Extension (Heel-Prop, Knee Unsupported)

**Why this exercise**
Uses gravity to slowly straighten the knee. Prevents the #1 long-term problem after TKR: **flexion deformity** (knee stuck in a slightly bent position forever, causing permanent limping). "Passive" means the muscle doesn't work — gravity does all the work. This is one of the most important exercises in the plan — do it faithfully.

**What you need**
- Bed. Thin head pillow.
- One rolled towel OR firm small cushion about **15 cm / 6 inches high** (a rolled-up bath towel, a small cushion, or a folded thin blanket rolled up works).
- **NO pillow under the knee — this is the whole point.**
- (Optional but recommended for her) Small lumbar roll under L4–L5.

**Starting position**
1. Lie flat on back.
2. Thin pillow under head.
3. **Place the 15 cm bolster under the HEEL ONLY** — not under calf, not under knee. Only under the heel and ankle.
4. The heel is LIFTED. The calf FLOATS (not touching anything). The back of the knee is IN THE AIR (nothing under it). The back of the THIGH rests on the bed.
5. Foot relaxed, toes pointing up to ceiling.
6. Small rolled towel under low back if needed (especially for her LBP).

**Imagine this**
Imagine a weight is pulling your kneecap DOWN toward the bed, gently stretching the back of your knee. You don't push — you just let gravity pull. The back of the knee slowly opens up as it reaches toward the bed.

**The movement — mostly stillness**

1. Once in position, **just stay still** and let gravity work.
2. Let the leg feel HEAVY — "like a sack of rice."
3. Do NOT push down with muscles. Do NOT try to force the knee.
4. **Every 30 seconds, do ONE quadriceps set** (5-second thigh squeeze as in Exercise 5). This boosts the stretch without forcing.
5. Stay in this position for 5 minutes initially (Week 1). Build up to 15 minutes by Week 3.
6. When done, slowly remove the bolster.

**Breathing:** Slow belly breaths throughout. Tight breathing = tight muscles = less stretch. Relax into the breath.

**How it should FEEL**
- **Strong stretch behind the knee** and down the back of the calf.
- A pulling/tight feeling at the BACK of the knee.
- **Discomfort 3–4 out of 10 is okay** — as long as it feels like a STRETCH, not a SHARP pain.
- Should NOT feel sharp pain INSIDE the knee.
- Should NOT feel pins-and-needles in the foot. If yes → bolster is pressing on a nerve behind the knee. Move it DOWN so it's only under the very end of the heel.

**Caregiver says**
- Setting up: *"I'm putting this rolled towel under your heel only. See the back of your knee? It's in the air — nothing underneath. That's right."*
- Starting: *"Let the leg go heavy. Don't push. Gravity will straighten the knee. Breathe slow."*
- Periodically: *"Every 30 seconds, squash the grape under your knee for 5 seconds — quick quad squeeze."*
- At 5 min: *"Halfway done. Breathe."*
- If she's tense: *"Relax the thigh. Let it sink. Gravity does the work."*

**GOOD setup vs BAD setup**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Heel on bolster, calf floats, back of knee in air | Calf resting on bolster OR back of knee touching pillow |
| Leg completely relaxed, thigh sinks into bed | Thigh lifted or held up by muscle tension |
| Stretch felt behind the knee | Pain inside the knee joint |
| Toes pointed up to ceiling | Foot rolling inward or outward |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Bolster too low — knee still bent | Add another folded towel under heel to increase height (try 20 cm if 15 cm isn't enough stretch). |
| Pins-and-needles in foot | Move bolster DOWN so it supports ONLY the tip of the heel, not the calf. |
| Low back starts arching (LBP flare) | **Critical for her:** Add small lumbar roll under L4–L5. If still flares, shorten session to 5 min + 2 min sitting break + 5 min again. |
| Foot flopping sideways | Place rolled towels on both sides of the foot to keep it upright. |
| Stretch too intense (5+/10 pain) | Lower the bolster height (use 10 cm instead of 15 cm). Work up gradually. |
| Stretch too weak (no sensation) | Add the 0.5 kg weight (water bottle) on the thigh just above the kneecap. From Week 3 onwards. |
| Can't relax — thigh stays tense | Try belly breathing in same position for 2 min first. Then attempt. |

**You know it's working when...**
- Week by week, the knee looks straighter when she lies flat.
- Eventually, the back of the knee touches the bed on its own (without bolster) — this means full extension is achieved. Goal: achieve this by Week 4–6.

**Dose (UPGRADED for her LBP to break up into shorter sessions)**
- Week 1: **3–5 min per leg × 2 sessions per day** (NOT more — low back can't tolerate long supine yet).
- Week 2: **5 min per leg × 3 sessions per day** (still in short sessions).
- Week 3: 7–10 min per leg × 3 sessions per day.
- Week 4+: 10–15 min per leg × 3 sessions per day.

**⚠ KEY FOR HER LBP:** Because long supine-legs-straight sessions aggravate low back, **BREAK UP** into shorter sessions. Rather than one 15-min session, do: 5 min heel-prop → sit up 2 min → 5 min heel-prop → sit up 2 min → 5 min heel-prop. Also **use the small lumbar roll** under L4–L5 throughout the whole session.

**Alternative for her (IF supine aggravates LBP even with lumbar roll):** **Long-sitting heel-prop** — sit up on the bed with back against wall / headboard, legs straight out in front, heel on the bolster, knee unsupported. Same gravity effect on the knee, but the trunk is vertical (no low back arch). Use this if classic supine flares her back.

**When to STOP the session:**
- Foot goes numb for more than 2 min after adjusting → stop and call doctor
- Severe sharp knee pain (not stretch) → stop, ice
- Low back pain that doesn't resolve with lumbar roll → switch to long-sitting alternative

### Exercise 9 — Seated Knee Extension (Long-Arc Quads / LAQ)

**Why this exercise**
Makes the front thigh muscle (quadriceps) strong through its full length. Teaches the knee to straighten against gravity. Key preparation for walking without a limp, climbing stairs, and getting out of a car.

**What you need**
- Firm chair (NOT sofa, NOT stool). Seat height at or above knee crease when she stands (chair is correct height if her hips are slightly HIGHER than knees when seated).
- **Lumbar roll / rolled towel behind L4–L5** — MANDATORY for her (protects her back).
- Non-slip floor or chappals on (so foot doesn't slide forward while knee bends).

**Starting position**
1. Sit well back in the chair, whole buttock supported against chair back.
2. Back rests against chair back, with lumbar roll in place at the curve of the low back.
3. Both feet flat on floor.
4. Knees bent at 90° (shape of letter L from the side — shin vertical, thigh horizontal).
5. Hands on thighs.
6. Look forward (not down). Chin gentle. Shoulders soft.

**Imagine this**
Imagine your lower leg is a door swinging open on a hinge. The knee is the hinge. The door opens slowly, fully, then closes slowly. The thigh doesn't move — only the lower leg swings out and back.

**The movement — step by step**
Start with the LESS painful leg first.

1. **Brace abs** (pull belly button gently toward spine). This protects the low back. Keep this brace through the whole exercise.
2. Breathe IN.
3. **Slowly lift the right foot off the floor** by straightening the right knee. Count "one, two, three, four." The thigh stays on the chair; only the lower leg rises. The foot ends up pointing slightly up/forward.
4. **At the TOP, pull toes toward the face** — this engages the quad fully. The front of the right thigh becomes HARD like a stone.
5. **Hold** at the top. Count "one, two, three, four, five."
6. **Slowly lower** the foot back to the floor. Count "one, two, three, four." Slow lowering builds MORE strength than lifting.
7. **Rest** 3 seconds with foot on floor.
8. Repeat.
9. After 10 reps on right, do 10 on left.

**Breathing:** OUT while lifting (knee straightens). IN while lowering. NEVER hold.

**How it should FEEL**
- Strong working feeling in the front of the thigh.
- Thigh becomes visibly firm/hard to touch.
- Small pulling feeling at the front of the knee — normal.
- Should NOT feel sharp pain INSIDE the knee.
- Should NOT feel pain in the low back (if yes → chair is wrong or she's leaning).
- Should NOT feel back of knee strongly stretching (that's hamstring; shouldn't dominate).

**Caregiver says**
- At start: *"Sit tall. Back against the chair. Feel the towel behind your back."*
- During lift: *"Lift slow. 1, 2, 3, 4. Toes to your nose."*
- At top: *"Hold. Squeeze the thigh. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5."*
- During lowering: *"Slow down. Don't drop. Control it — 1, 2, 3, 4."*
- If leaning back: *"Don't lean. Back stays against the chair."*
- If cheating: *"Quadriceps doing the work. Not the hip, not the back."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Back stays against chair throughout | Leaning back to lift (spine helps) |
| Slow 4-second lift, 4-second lower | Jerky, fast |
| Toes pulled up at the top | Toes dangling down |
| Knee fully straight at top (reaches 0° flexion) | Knee only half-straight |
| 5-second hold | Touch-and-go, no hold |
| Calm breathing | Breath held, red face |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Low back pain during lift | Sit farther back. Strengthen lumbar roll. Brace abs harder. |
| Can't fully straighten the knee | Normal early on — the range comes over weeks. Do whatever range you can with good form. |
| Foot slides forward | Wear non-slip footwear. Or place a non-slip mat under feet. |
| Shaking of the leg at the top | Fatigue — reduce reps this set by half. Rest, try again. |
| Can't do even 1 rep | Try "assisted" version: use hands under the thigh to help lift, and/or have the LESS operated leg help push up. |
| Knee pain (inside) during lift | Reduce lift height by 50% — only straighten the knee halfway. Build gradually. |

**You know it's working when...**
- Each week, she can lift the leg straighter (closer to horizontal).
- Thigh looks and feels firmer.
- Standing from a chair (Exercise 13) becomes easier.

**Dose**
- Week 1 (from Day 4): **Assisted version** (other leg helps push up if needed) — 5 reps × 1 set × once daily.
- Week 2: 8 reps × 2 sets × 2–3 times daily.
- Week 3: full range 0–90°, 10 reps × 2 sets.
- Week 4+: Add 0.5 kg ankle weight (500 ml water bottle taped above the ankle).
- Week 6+: Progress to 1 kg ankle weight.

**Best time:** Early afternoon (2:30–4:30 PM) when analgesia is at peak — strength work easier and less painful.

**When to STOP:**
- Sharp inside-knee pain
- Loud click with pain
- Knee feels unstable ("gives way" at any point)
- Low back pain doesn't resolve with better positioning

### Exercise 10 — Seated Knee Flexion (Heel Slide While Sitting)

**Why this exercise**
Teaches the knee to bend MORE than the heel slide on the bed can achieve, because gravity now helps pull the heel under the chair. Seated flexion is how we push toward the ROM goal (100–110°). Without this, knee bending plateaus around 80°.

**What you need**
- Firm chair + lumbar roll (same as Exercise 9).
- Smooth floor OR a cloth/plastic bag placed under the foot so it slides easily.
- Non-slip footwear (so the foot doesn't slip out).

**Starting position**
Same as Exercise 9: sit well back in chair, lumbar roll in place, feet flat on floor, knees at 90°.

**Imagine this**
Imagine you are trying to "tuck" your foot backward under the chair. Slide the foot backwards — like pulling it out of a rug — while keeping the heel on the floor. The knee folds more and more as the foot slides back.

**The movement — step by step**

1. Brace abs (belly button toward spine).
2. Breathe IN.
3. As you breathe OUT, **slowly slide the right foot BACKWARD along the floor, under the chair**. The knee bends more and more. Count: "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand, three-one-thousand, four-one-thousand, five-one-thousand."
4. **Stop at the first strong stretch** — not at sharp pain.
5. **Hold** this position for 5 seconds.
6. **Extra stretch (from Week 2):** Keep foot where it is, gently lean trunk 2 inches forward FROM THE HIPS (NOT from the waist). Hold 3 more seconds. Then sit back up.
7. Slide foot forward to start position. Count: "one, two, three, four."
8. Rest 3 seconds.
9. Repeat.
10. After 10 right-leg reps, do 10 left.

**Breathing:** OUT while sliding foot BACK (knee bends). IN while sliding foot FORWARD (knee straightens).

**How it should FEEL**
- Stretchy, pulling feeling at the FRONT of the knee, and front of the thigh just above the knee.
- A sense of the kneecap sliding back on the thigh bone.
- Mild discomfort at max bend (3–4 out of 10) is okay.
- Should NOT feel sharp pain inside the knee.
- Should NOT feel pain in low back.
- Heel stays on floor throughout.

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Sit tall. Back against the chair. Heel on the floor."*
- During slide: *"Slide slow — pull your foot back, under the chair. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5."*
- At max bend: *"Hold. Feel the stretch at the front of the knee. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5."*
- For extra stretch (Week 2+): *"Now lean forward from the hips. Small lean, 2 inches. Keep spine straight. Hold 3 seconds."*
- If she lifts the heel: *"Heel down. Heel on floor."*
- If back rounds: *"Back straight. Lean from hips, not from the waist."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Heel stays touching floor | Heel lifts during slide |
| Slow 5-second slide | Fast, jerky |
| Buttocks stay back on chair | Buttocks slide forward on chair |
| Spine stays straight | Spine rounds forward |
| Controlled return | Foot shoots forward |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Foot doesn't slide easily | Place plastic bag, silk cloth, or thin dupatta under the foot to reduce friction. |
| Knee pain at deep bend | Stop 10° earlier. Gradually increase. |
| Heel lifts off floor | Caregiver places hand on top of foot — foot slides under chair, but stays on floor. |
| Buttocks slide forward | Remind her to "stay on the sit-bones." Put a non-slip mat on the chair seat. |
| Back pain | Check lumbar roll is in place. Bracing abs before sliding. |

**You know it's working when...**
- Each week, the foot goes 2–3 cm further back.
- The heel reaches past the front chair leg eventually.
- Step-ups and stairs become easier (later weeks).

**Dose**
- Week 1 (Day 4 onwards): 5 reps × 1 set × once daily.
- Week 2: 10 reps × 2 sets × 2 times daily.
- Week 3+: 10 reps × 3 sets × 3 times daily.

**Progression to add more bend:**
- Week 2: Once at max bend, use OTHER ankle to hook over the top of the foot and gently press the foot further back (extra 5°).
- Week 4: Hold at max bend for 10 sec instead of 5.

**When to STOP:**
- Sharp pain inside knee
- Loud click with pain
- Knee feels stuck (can't straighten back)
- Low back pain that doesn't resolve

### Exercise 11 — Incentive Spirometry

**Why this exercise**
Opens the tiny air sacs (alveoli) at the bottom of the lungs. After anaesthesia, these air sacs can collapse and fill with fluid, causing **chest infection and pneumonia** — one of the most serious post-surgical complications. The spirometer is a simple mechanical device that makes you take very deep slow breaths, which keeps the air sacs open.

**What you need**
- The incentive spirometer given by the hospital (usually a clear plastic tube with 3 coloured balls, called "Triflo" or "Tri-Ball").
- Tissue or handkerchief (for possible cough afterwards).
- Upright sitting position (chair OR bed with back raised to 75°).

**Starting position**
1. Sit UPRIGHT — this is very important. Lying down doesn't work. The spirometer works only when the lungs can fully expand.
2. Back supported (lumbar roll if needed).
3. Hold the spirometer UPRIGHT (straight up, not tilted) in one hand. If the device is tilted, the balls don't register airflow correctly.
4. Check if there's a target marker on the side (set by hospital). If not, aim to raise the first ball and then the second.

**Imagine this**
Imagine you are sipping a thick milkshake through a very narrow straw — slowly, deeply, smoothly. The more slowly you inhale, the more the balls rise. This is the opposite of a fast gulp of air.

**The movement — step by step**
1. **Put the mouthpiece in the mouth.** Seal lips tightly around it — no air should leak from the sides.
2. **Breathe OUT gently through the NOSE first** (empty your lungs a little — not forcefully, just a normal exhale).
3. **Now breathe IN slowly and deeply through the mouthpiece.** The balls inside should rise SLOWLY. Count: "one, two, three, four, five."
   - For a 3-ball Triflo: in Week 1, try to lift the FIRST ball and hold it up. In Week 2, try to lift the first AND second balls. By Week 3, try for all three.
4. **When the lungs feel full**, remove the mouthpiece from the mouth.
5. **Hold the breath for 3 seconds.** Count: "one, two, three." This is the KEY part — it gives the air sacs time to open fully.
6. **Breathe OUT slowly through the mouth**, lips softly pursed (like blowing on hot tea).
7. **Rest with 2 normal breaths** (to avoid dizziness).
8. Repeat.

**How it should FEEL**
- A strong stretching sensation deep in the chest and on the sides of the ribs.
- A "full" feeling in the lungs at the top.
- Sometimes a tickle or urge to cough at the end — this is GOOD. Coughing clears the lungs. Hold a pillow gently across the chest when coughing to reduce pain.
- Should NOT feel dizzy. If dizzy, breathe normally for 1 min, then resume slower.

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Sit up straight. Hold the device upright — straight up, not tilted."*
- Before the breath: *"Seal your lips tight around the mouthpiece. No air from the sides."*
- During inhale: *"Sip the air slowly. Slow. Like drinking through a narrow straw. Make the ball rise and STAY."*
- At top: *"Remove the device. Hold your breath — 1, 2, 3."*
- Release: *"Slow out-breath. Pursed lips."*
- Encouraging: *"Next one, try to lift two balls."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Ball rises slowly and stays up | Ball jumps up fast and drops immediately |
| Full 5-second inhale | 1-second gulp |
| 3-second breath hold at top | No breath hold |
| Device held STRAIGHT up | Device tilted sideways |
| Tight lip seal — no air leak | Air whistling around the lips |
| Relaxed shoulders | Shoulders raised up toward ears |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Can't lift even the first ball | She's breathing too fast. Slow it down — 5 full seconds inhale. If still can't, try 3 seconds. Build up slowly. |
| Gets dizzy | Rest 1 min, then take smaller breaths. Reduce to 5 breaths per set, do more sets. |
| Feels like coughing constantly | GOOD — means lungs are opening. Support chest with a pillow, cough gently 1–2 times, then resume. |
| Air leaks around the lips | Remind her "lips tight like a kiss" around the mouthpiece. |
| Shoulders rise up with each breath | Put caregiver's hands on her shoulders — keep them down. Focus on the belly and ribs expanding, not the chest. |
| Device feels contaminated | Wash mouthpiece with warm soapy water once a week. Air-dry. NEVER boil (plastic melts). |

**You know it's working when...**
- Day 1: she can lift the first ball for 3 seconds.
- Day 3: lift first ball for 5 seconds.
- Day 7: lift first two balls.
- Day 14: all three balls stay up for 2–3 seconds.
- No fever, no wet productive cough = chest infection prevented.

**Dose**
- First 3 days (Day 1–3): 10 deep breaths every 1–2 hours while awake (about 8–10 sessions/day).
- Day 4–Day 14: 10 breaths × 5 sessions/day.
- Week 3–4: 10 breaths × 3 sessions/day.
- After Day 14: Can taper to once daily if no productive cough, full mobility, SpO₂ ≥ 95%.

**Time per session:** ~5 min.

**Daytime only** — do NOT wake her at night for IS.

**When to stop a session:**
- Severe dizziness
- Chest pain (stop and call doctor — could be PE)
- Coughing up blood (call doctor)

### Exercise 12 — Walker-Supported Walking

**Why this exercise**
Walking is the best medicine. It builds muscles, prevents blood clots, helps the bowels move, and teaches the new knees how to work. The walker keeps her safe from falls. For bilateral TKR, walker use continues for 4–6 weeks — don't rush to a cane.

**What you need**
- **Standard 4-legged walker** (NOT wheeled — too unstable for bilateral TKR). Height-adjusted correctly.
- **How to check walker height:** She stands up straight, arms relaxed by her sides. The TOP of the walker handle should be at the level of her WRIST CREASE (where the hand bends). When she then puts her hands on the grips, her elbows bend 20–30° (a gentle bend, not straight, not deeply bent).
- **Closed-toe non-slip shoes** (NOT hawai chappal — they slip off).
- Husband on one side, slightly behind her shoulder (for safety catch).

**Starting position**
1. Stand with walker in front, facing forward.
2. Both hands firmly on walker grips.
3. Feet INSIDE the walker frame, hip-width apart.
4. Stand TALL — shoulders back and down, chest lifted, NOT hunched over walker.
5. Look forward (at the wall ahead), NOT at your feet.
6. Weight evenly on both legs.

**Imagine this**
Imagine you are walking across a room with your hands resting on a kitchen table that you can lift and move. You lift the table a small distance ahead, set it down firmly on all 4 legs, then take one small step, then another. The "table" (walker) is always in front as your support.

**The movement — for BILATERAL TKR (no "good leg")**

Sequence: **"Walker → first leg → second leg."**

1. **Lift the walker** forward about 25 cm (one shoe-length). Place ALL 4 walker legs flat on the floor.
   - Do NOT push or slide the walker (it's not wheeled — this tips it).
   - All 4 legs must be on the floor BEFORE you step.
2. **Press down** on the walker grips with BOTH hands.
3. **Step forward with the LESS-PAINFUL leg** first. Foot lands INSIDE the walker frame, NOT past the front legs.
4. **Bring the other leg** forward to meet it (or slightly ahead). Foot lands balanced.
5. **Stand balanced** for 2 seconds. Breathe.
6. **Repeat**: walker first, then first leg, then second leg.

**Turning with walker:**
- Come to a FULL STOP.
- Take SMALL shuffling steps in a WIDE arc (not pivoting on one foot).
- Move walker a few cm in the turn direction, then step, then move walker again.
- A U-turn should take 8–10 small steps.
- **NEVER pivot on a fixed foot** — twists the knee implants.

**Breathing:** Breathe naturally. If she holds breath, say a rhythm out loud with her: "walker, step, step, walker, step, step."

**How it should FEEL**
- Some pulling and working in the thighs and around the knees.
- Pressure in the hands from leaning on the walker (30–40% body weight on hands is normal).
- Mild tiredness after 2–3 minutes — normal.
- A sense of "her body is learning the pattern again."
- Should NOT feel sharp knee pain.
- Should NOT feel the knee "give way" — if yes, sit down IMMEDIATELY.
- Should NOT feel dizzy — if yes, sit down IMMEDIATELY.

**Caregiver says**
- At start: *"Walker first. Lift, place. All four legs on floor."*
- During step: *"Now step. Foot inside the walker."*
- If she hunches: *"Tall like a queen. Chest up."*
- If she looks down: *"Eyes on the wall ahead."*
- If she steps past walker: *"Step inside. Don't go past the front bar — you'll fall."*
- If she rolls the walker: *"Lift it. All four legs off the floor together."*
- Rhythm call: *"Walker — step — step. Walker — step — step."*
- If she's tired: *"Stop. Rest. Come back when ready."*

**GOOD walking vs BAD walking**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| All 4 walker legs touching floor when stepping | Walker tilted or only 2 legs down |
| Foot lands INSIDE the walker frame | Foot steps past the front bar |
| Tall posture | Hunched over walker |
| Looking ahead | Looking at feet |
| Small controlled steps | Long lunging steps |
| Both hands pushing DOWN on grips | One hand off, carrying something |
| Rest when tired | Pushing through exhaustion |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Walker feels too low (hunching) | Measure walker height — should be at wrist crease. Adjust upward. |
| Walker feels too high (shoulders up) | Adjust downward. |
| Walker tips forward | She's stepping past the front bar. Step inside only. |
| Knee buckles or gives way | Stop immediately. Sit down. If repeated, call doctor. |
| Fatigue fast | Increase rest breaks. Shorter walks more often. |
| Slippery floor | Stop on wet floors. Dry the floor before walking. |
| Narrow doorways catching walker | Use narrow walker or temporarily remove doors. |
| Holding breath | Sing or hum a tune — forces breathing. |

**You know it's working when...**
- Week 1: She can walk from bed to bathroom independently (with walker).
- Week 2: Can walk around the bedroom for 5+ min without needing to sit.
- Week 4: Can walk the length of the verandah with walker.
- Week 6: Can progress to cane/two sticks.
- Week 10–12: Indoor walking without aid possible.

**Dose**
- Day 1–3: 5 min per walk, 3–4 walks per day (bed → bathroom → back).
- Day 4–7: 8–10 min per walk, 4 walks per day.
- Week 2: 10–15 min per walk, 4 walks per day.
- Week 3–4: 15–20 min per walk, 3–4 walks per day.
- Week 6+: Outdoor walks on smooth surfaces with walker.

**Best timing:** After morning NSAID has absorbed (10:30 AM onwards). Not within 3 hours of Ultracet (drowsiness).

**When to STOP immediately:**
- Knee "gives way" or buckles
- Dizziness / feeling faint
- Sharp knee pain > 5/10
- Chest pain or breathlessness
- Calf pain (possible DVT)
- Feet go numb or cold

### Exercise 13 — Sit-to-Stand (Getting Up From a Chair)

**Why this exercise**
The most-repeated transfer of the day (15–25 times). Done right, it's free therapy for quad strength. Done wrong, it's the #1 fall risk in Week 1 — pulling up on a walker tips it forward and drops the patient onto the just-operated knees.

**What you need**
- **Firm chair with armrests, seat 46–52 cm tall** (dining chair with cushion, NOT sofa, NOT low stool).
- **Test the chair:** when she sits, her hips should be slightly HIGHER than her knees, feet flat on floor. If knees are higher than hips, chair is too low — raise with cushion.
- Walker placed in FRONT of chair, within reach but NOT used for pushing up.
- Closed-toe non-slip shoes.

**Starting position — for STANDING UP**
1. Sit well back in the chair first.
2. Slide FORWARD until buttocks are on the **front third** of the chair.
3. **Bring feet BACK** — heels under or slightly BEHIND the front chair edge. Feet hip-width apart.
4. If both legs equally strong: weight evenly on both feet. If one leg stronger: bring the stronger foot slightly further back (it will do more work lifting).
5. Walker is in front, within reach.
6. Hands on the CHAIR ARMRESTS (not walker).

**Imagine this**
Imagine you are about to lift a light box from your lap and place it on a high shelf. To do this, you first LEAN forward (nose over toes), then push UP through your arms and legs together. The arms help; the legs lift. The walker is just for balance at the top — never for pushing up.

**The movement — STANDING UP (step by step)**
1. **Brace abs** (belly button gently toward spine — protects low back).
2. **Breathe IN**, prepare.
3. **Lean trunk FORWARD from the hips** — "nose over toes." Shoulders pass in FRONT of knees. This feels like you're about to fall forward, but it's CORRECT. This shifts your weight over the feet, making standing up possible.
4. **Breathe OUT** as you push.
5. At the same time, **push DOWN with BOTH hands on the CHAIR ARMRESTS** (NOT the walker — walker CAN TIP FORWARD, causing a fall).
6. **Push through both feet** and straighten both knees smoothly. Count: "one, two, three." Exhale through the push.
7. **Once standing** (knees straight), stand TALL and stable.
8. **Transfer hands ONE AT A TIME from armrests to walker grips** (never let go of all supports at once).
9. **Pause 5 seconds** before taking any step, to check balance and avoid orthostatic dizziness.

**The movement — SITTING DOWN (step by step)**

Sitting DOWN is actually more dangerous than standing up — requires slow controlled knee bending.

1. **Back up slowly** using walker until back of both calves touch the front edge of the chair seat ("feel the chair behind you" — NEVER try to sit without feeling the chair first).
2. **Transfer hands ONE AT A TIME** from walker to chair armrests (keep one hand on walker, one on armrest, then switch).
3. **Lean trunk slightly forward.**
4. **Brace abs.**
5. **Slowly** bend both knees and lower. Count: "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand, three-one-thousand, four-one-thousand." Slow = 3–4 seconds descent.
6. **Sit gently** on the front of the seat — don't "flop down" (jars the implants).
7. Slide backward into the chair to sit properly.

**Breathing:** OUT during standing up push. Breathe naturally once standing. OUT during sitting down.

**How it should FEEL**
- Standing up: strong working feeling in BOTH thighs and buttocks.
- Arms do significant work pushing down on armrests.
- Small pulling at front of knee — normal.
- Sitting down: strong working feeling in thighs (eccentric quad control).
- Should NOT feel knee "collapse" at any point.
- Should NOT feel pain in low back (brace abs more, lean less).

**Caregiver says**
- Before start: *"Feet BACK under the chair. Hips on the front edge."*
- Beginning standing: *"Brace your belly. Lean forward — NOSE over toes. Yes!"*
- Push: *"Push on the armrests. Push — DOWN. Up you go — 1, 2, 3."*
- Standing: *"Stand tall. Pause 5 seconds. Find your balance."*
- Transfer hands: *"One hand to walker. Other hand to walker. Good."*
- Sitting: *"Back up to the chair. Slow. Feel the chair behind you?"*
- Descent: *"Slow down. 1, 2, 3, 4. Don't drop."*
- If she pulls on walker: *"No! Hands on ARMRESTS. Walker will tip!"*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Feet UNDER / BEHIND knees | Feet OUT in front of knees (can't lever up) |
| Nose leans forward OVER toes | Sitting straight, trying to stand straight up (won't work) |
| Push on chair armrests | Pull on walker (walker tips → fall) |
| Slow 3-sec rise | Fast jerky rise |
| Transfer hands to walker one at a time | Let go of everything before grabbing walker |
| Slow 4-sec descent | "Plop" down into chair |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Can't stand up on first try | Rock forward 3 times to build momentum. Then push on third rock. |
| Knee buckles during rise | Sit back down slowly. Need more quad strength first (more Exercise 5). |
| Dizzy when standing | Pause longer (10 sec) before stepping. Take Ultracet if pain is the driver. |
| Low back pain | Brace abs harder. Don't lean too far forward. |
| Feet slide forward | Non-slip chappals. Non-slip mat under chair. |
| Chair too low | Add firm cushion. Seat must be at knee crease when standing. |
| Can't sit down slowly | Caregiver stands behind, hands under her arms for gentle assist during first week. |

**You know it's working when...**
- Week 1: She can do 5 sit-to-stands in a row without rest.
- Week 3: She can do 10 in a row with rests.
- Week 5: She can do 10 with NO armrests (hands on seat beside hips).
- Week 7: She can do 10 with arms folded across chest — **major milestone**.

**Dose**
- Week 1: 5 repetitions × 2 sets × 3 times per day.
- Week 3: 10 reps × 2 sets.
- Week 5: Try with no armrests (hands on seat beside hips), 10 reps.
- Week 7: No hands at all (arms folded across chest), 10 reps. This is a huge milestone.
- **Practice EVERY TIME** she gets up from bed, chair, or toilet — real-life practice.

**When to STOP:**
- Sharp knee pain
- Knee buckles / gives way
- Dizziness (sit back down, breathe, wait)
- Chest pain (call doctor)
- Back pain that doesn't improve with abs brace

### Exercise 14 — Pelvic Tilts

**Why this exercise**
**One of the best exercises for her 30-year low back pain.** Flattens the low-back curve (reduces facet joint loading), wakes up deep belly muscles (transverse abdominis), and reduces pain. Also trains her to control the pelvis during other exercises. Safe post-TKR (no knee load) and double-benefit for knee + back.

**What you need**
- Bed with firm mattress, thin head pillow.
- NO extra pillows (the bed surface does the work).

**Starting position**
1. Lie flat on back.
2. Thin pillow under head.
3. **Bend both knees** by sliding heels up toward buttocks — feet FLAT on bed, hip-width apart. Bend only as much as is comfortable (even a small bend is fine if knees are sore).
4. Arms beside body.
5. Notice: there is usually a small gap under the low back. Slide your flat hand under her lower back — you can feel the small gap.

**Imagine this**
Imagine there is a small handprint under your lower back that you want to press flat into the bed. Squash it. The pubic bone rolls up toward your chin (like tucking your tailbone under). The whole lower back sinks into the mattress.

**The movement — step by step**

1. **Breathe IN** through the nose normally.
2. **Breathe OUT** slowly through the mouth.
3. **As you breathe OUT, gently press the LOW BACK DOWN into the bed.** Imagine squashing the flat hand you slid underneath. Pull belly button toward spine. The pubic bone rolls up toward the chin.
4. **Hold** this flattened position for 5 seconds. Keep breathing (don't hold breath).
5. **Slowly release** — count 2 seconds. Feel the gap return under the low back.
6. Rest 3 seconds.
7. Repeat.

**Breathing:** OUT through mouth while pressing back down. IN through nose while releasing. NEVER hold breath.

**How it should FEEL**
- Low back pressing FLAT against the bed.
- Gentle working sensation in the DEEP belly (below belly button, not upper belly).
- Soft squeeze of the buttocks (some squeeze is OK — excessive is not).
- Should NOT feel sharp pull in low back.
- Should NOT feel anything in the knee — knee stays quiet.
- Should NOT feel the neck or shoulders tensing.

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Bend both knees. Feet flat on bed. I'll slide my hand under your lower back — feel the gap?"*
- Start: *"Breathe out slow. As you breathe out, squash my hand flat."*
- Check: *"Can you squash my hand? Yes! That's perfect."*
- Hold: *"Keep it squashed. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Keep breathing."*
- Release: *"Now let go. Feel the gap come back."*
- If she makes a bridge: *"Buttocks stay on the bed. Don't lift the hips. Only the belly pulls in."*
- If she pushes belly out: *"Belly goes IN toward spine, not OUT. Gentle zipper closing."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Lower back flat against bed (hand gets squashed) | Lower back stays arched (hand not squashed) |
| Buttocks STAY on bed | Buttocks LIFT (making a bridge — wrong) |
| Belly pulls IN | Belly pushes OUT |
| Breathing flows | Face red, breath held |
| Slow 5-second hold | Quick tap |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Can't flatten the back at all | Try smaller motion — just 10% of the way. Feel the intent of pressing down. Builds over 1 week. |
| Buttocks lift (bridging) | Caregiver places hand on hip — "stays here, don't lift." |
| Belly pushes out (not in) | Say "zip up a tight trouser from the hips" — gives the right image. |
| Neck tenses | Tuck chin slightly, look at the ceiling, relax shoulders. |
| Doesn't feel anything | Her TrA (deep belly muscle) isn't firing yet. Try Kegel first (Exercise 16), then combine. |

**You know it's working when...**
- Her low back feels less stiff in the morning.
- She can hold the tilt 10 seconds without tiring.
- Later, she uses this skill to protect her back during transfers.

**Dose (UPGRADED for her LBP)**
- Week 1 (from Day 2): **10 reps × 3 sets × 3–4 sessions per day.** (Standard protocol is 2 sessions — we double for her LBP benefit.)
- Week 2+: 15 reps × 3 sets × 3 sessions per day.
- Lifelong habit — do 5 before getting out of bed every morning to "wake up" the core.

**Best time:** Any time, especially morning (reduces morning back stiffness) and before transfers.

**When to STOP:**
- Sharp shooting pain in low back
- Pain going down the leg (sciatica — call physiotherapist)
- Knee pain from the bent knee position → bend knees less

### Exercise 15 — Abdominal 3-in-1 Brace (TrA + Diaphragm + Kegel)

**Why this exercise**
**The single best-evidence exercise for her 30-year low back pain.** It combines three deep "core corset" muscles into one action: the breath muscle (diaphragm), the deep belly muscle (transverse abdominis / TrA), and the pelvic floor (Kegels). Meta-analyses show post-partum women get the LARGEST benefit from this type of training compared to any other LBP group. The brace also protects her back during every transfer (sit-to-stand, bed mobility, walker walking).

**What you need**
- Bed, thin head pillow.
- Quiet space (she needs to concentrate).

**Starting position**
1. Lie on back.
2. Thin pillow under head.
3. **Knees bent** (hook-lying), feet FLAT on bed, hip-width apart. This protects the low back.
4. One hand on lower belly (below belly button), other hand on lower ribs.
5. Relax shoulders.

**Imagine this**
Three things happen at the same time:
1. **Like zipping up a tight trouser** — lower belly pulls IN toward spine (NOT a crunch).
2. **Like stopping yourself from passing urine** — pelvic floor muscles lift UP and IN.
3. **Like deflating a balloon slowly** — breath flows OUT.

All three happen together. Not one, then another — ALL at once.

**The movement — step by step**

1. **Breathe IN** through nose normally.
2. **Start breathing OUT slowly through pursed lips** (like blowing on tea). Count: "one, two, three, four, five, six."
3. **As the out-breath continues, pull NAVEL toward SPINE** — gentle, not forceful. Think "zip up a tight pant" — lower belly hand goes slightly DOWN (IN).
4. **At the same time, lift the pelvic floor** (Kegel — squeeze as if stopping urine mid-stream, AND lift upward inside).
5. **Hold this 3-in-1 brace for 10 seconds**, breathing normally.
6. **Fully release.** 2 seconds rest.
7. Repeat.

**Breathing:** Starts on the out-breath of a regular breath cycle. Once the brace is held, breathe normally. Don't hold breath during the 10-second hold.

**How it should FEEL**
- Lower belly slightly FIRM, not rigid or pushed out.
- Upper belly stays relaxed (ribs don't flare).
- Pelvic floor "lifted and squeezed."
- Low back stays NEUTRAL (not arched, not flattened).
- Shoulders relaxed.
- Should NOT feel tension in low back, neck, or upper shoulders.
- Should NOT feel "crunch" in the abs (too much = wrong, it becomes a sit-up).

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Knees bent. Feet flat. One hand on your lower belly, other hand on lower ribs."*
- Start: *"Slow breath out — 6 seconds. As you breathe out..."*
- At end of exhale: *"Pull belly button toward spine — zip up a tight pant. Gentle."*
- Add Kegel: *"At the same time, lift your pelvic floor — like stopping urine."*
- Hold: *"Hold everything in. 10 seconds. Breathe normally. 1, 2, 3... 10."*
- Release: *"Let everything go."*
- If she sucks in too hard: *"Gentle. Not forceful. Just 30% squeeze, not 100%."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Lower belly hand moves slightly DOWN (IN) | Lower belly hand moves UP (OUT — bloating, wrong) |
| Upper ribs stay still | Upper ribs flare out |
| Back stays neutral | Back flattens hard or arches |
| Breathing flows during hold | Breath held during 10 seconds |
| Pelvic floor clearly lifted | Can't feel pelvic floor activation |
| Shoulders relaxed | Shoulders tense, lifted |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Can't feel pelvic floor | Practice Kegels separately (Exercise 16) first, then combine. |
| Belly pushes OUT instead of IN | She's doing "bracing" wrong. Try: exhale fully first, THEN pull in as you exhale more. |
| Breath holds automatically | Say "count out loud 1-10 while holding" — talking forces breathing. |
| Low back rises off bed | Gentle pelvic tilt first (Exercise 14) — then add brace. |
| Upper ribs flare | Keep lower ribs DOWN — don't let them pop up during the brace. |
| Feels hard to hold 10 sec | Start with 5 sec, build to 10 over 2 weeks. |

**You know it's working when...**
- She can brace without thinking before every transfer.
- Her low back feels "held up" during activities.
- Chronic LBP gradually reduces over 4–6 weeks of consistent practice.

**Dose**
- Week 1 (from Day 3): 5 reps × 1 set × once daily.
- Week 2: 10 reps × 2 sets × 2 sessions daily.
- Week 3+: 10 reps × 3 sets × 3 sessions daily.
- **CRITICAL: Do the brace BEFORE EVERY TRANSFER** (before sit-to-stand, before rolling in bed, before walker push-off). This makes it a lifelong habit and permanently protects her back.

**Progression:**
- Week 1: Learn the 3-in-1 in hook-lying only.
- Week 3: Practice the brace while SITTING (firm chair, lumbar roll).
- Week 5: Practice the brace while STANDING (inside walker).
- Week 8: Use brace automatically during walking.

**When to STOP:** Sharp low back pain (rare). Pelvic floor cramp (rest, hydrate).

### Exercise 16 — Kegels (Pelvic Floor Muscles)

**Why this exercise**
**Strongest evidence of all LBP exercises for postpartum women** — meta-analysis of 19 RCTs showed the LARGEST pain-reduction effect size in this specific group. After childbirth, pelvic floor muscles often weaken; 30 years later, if never retrained, they're still weak. Weak pelvic floor = poor core support = chronic low back strain. Also good for bladder control (fewer urgent bathroom trips = fewer night falls on the way to toilet).

**What you need**
- Any position works — lying, sitting, standing.
- Start LYING DOWN in hook-lying for learning (easier to feel the muscles).

**Starting position (learning phase)**
1. Lie on back, knees bent (hook-lying).
2. Feet flat on bed.
3. Thin pillow under head.
4. Hands resting beside body or on belly.
5. Relaxed shoulders.

**Imagine this**

For the right feeling, imagine one of these:
- **"Stopping urine":** As if you're on the toilet and suddenly need to stop the urine stream mid-flow. The muscles that squeeze to stop the urine = pelvic floor.
- **"Hiding a fart":** Tighten inside as if holding in gas in a room full of people. Same muscle group.
- **"Lifting a berry with the inside":** Imagine you are gently lifting a small berry inside you — up and in.
- **"Drawing up":** The whole area between the sit-bones lifts UP inside the body.

**The movement — SLOW Kegels (start here)**

1. Breathe normally.
2. **Squeeze** the muscles around the vagina and anus. Lift them UP and IN (inside the body).
3. **Hold** the squeeze for 5 seconds. Count: "one, two, three, four, five."
4. **Fully RELEASE.** Let everything go loose for 5 seconds.
5. Repeat.

**The movement — FAST Kegels (add from Day 4)**

1. Quick SQUEEZE (1 second).
2. Quick RELEASE (1 second).
3. Repeat 10 times in a row.
4. Rest 30 seconds.
5. Repeat.

**Breathing:** Breathe normally throughout. Do NOT hold the breath. If she holds breath, say "breathe with me — in, out, in, out" while she does the squeeze.

**How it should FEEL**
- A sense of "lifting UP" inside the body.
- Subtle tightening of the perineum (area between vagina and anus).
- Nothing visible from outside.
- Should NOT feel the buttocks clenching (that's a glute squeeze, wrong muscle).
- Should NOT feel the thighs tightening.
- Should NOT feel the belly pushing out (that's abdominal pressure, wrong).
- Should NOT cause low back pain.

**Caregiver says (this is a private exercise — use gentle, respectful language)**
- *"This is a small inside muscle. Nobody can see it working — it's invisible."*
- *"Squeeze like you're stopping urine. Hold it. 5 seconds. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5."*
- *"Fully relax. Let go everything."*
- *"Again. Squeeze, hold, release."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Only pelvic floor moves — invisible from outside | Buttocks clench (visible buttock tightening) |
| Thighs stay relaxed | Thighs squeeze together |
| Breath flows normally | Breath held |
| Belly stays relaxed | Belly pushes OUT (bearing down — WRONG, opposite direction) |
| 5-second hold steady | Tap-and-release with no hold |
| Full release between reps | Partial release (stays partially tight) |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Can't feel the pelvic floor | Next time you urinate, try to stop the flow mid-stream for 1 second. THAT's the muscle. (Do this only once to learn — don't make a habit of stopping urine mid-flow.) |
| Squeezes the buttocks instead | Caregiver says: "I'll put my hand on your buttock. If your buttock tightens, you're doing it wrong. Pelvic floor is INSIDE only." |
| Breath held automatically | Count out loud while squeezing — forces breathing. |
| Belly pushes out | She's bearing down, which is opposite to what we want. Say "lift UP inside, don't push DOWN." |
| Too tired to hold 5 sec | Start with 2-second holds, build to 5. |

**You know it's working when...**
- After 2 weeks, fewer urgent bathroom trips (bladder control improves).
- Low back feels "supported from underneath."
- She can do 10 slow Kegels without fatigue.

**Dose**
- **Slow Kegels:** 10 reps × 3 sessions per day, from Day 3.
- **Fast Kegels:** 10 reps × 2 sessions per day, from Day 4.
- **Lifelong habit.** Can be done any time — in a chair, in bed, waiting for tea. Nobody sees, nobody knows. Can be done during other exercises.

**Best times:**
- Morning before getting out of bed.
- Mid-afternoon when sitting.
- Evening before bed.

**Can be done anywhere:** Chair, bed, standing, sitting on commode. Very convenient.

**Progression:**
- Week 1: 5-sec hold × 10 reps × 3 sessions.
- Week 2: 8-sec hold × 10 reps × 3 sessions.
- Week 3+: 10-sec hold × 10 reps × 3 sessions.
- Also practice during standing (harder — higher gravity challenge).

**When to STOP:** Pelvic floor cramping (hydrate, rest 1 min). No other red flags specific to Kegels.

### Exercise 17 — Hamstring Sets (Isometric — "Dig Heel Down")

**Why this exercise**
The hamstrings are the big muscles on the BACK of the thigh. They BALANCE the quadriceps (front thigh). If the quads are strong but hamstrings are weak, the knee can hyperextend (bend backwards), stressing the implant. This exercise wakes the hamstrings without moving the knee. "Isometric" means the muscle contracts without the joint moving.

**What you need**
- Bed, thin head pillow.
- Small rolled towel under the knee being exercised (just enough to create a tiny knee bend of 10–20°).

**Starting position**
1. Lie on back.
2. Thin pillow under head.
3. Exercising leg: knee slightly bent (10–20°) over a small rolled towel (this creates the angle needed for the muscle to fire).
4. Other leg: straight.
5. Arms beside body.

**Imagine this**
Imagine the heel of the exercising leg is trying to DRAG ITSELF along the bed TOWARDS the buttock — but the bed surface is sticky, and the heel can't actually move. The muscle is working hard, but no movement happens.

**The movement — step by step**

1. Breathe IN.
2. Breathe OUT through mouth.
3. **Dig the heel DOWN into the bed.**
4. At the same time, **try to drag the heel along the bed toward the buttock** — BUT the heel DOES NOT actually move. The muscle works, but nothing moves visibly.
5. The BACK of the thigh (hamstring) should feel HARD.
6. Hold 5 sec.
7. Release 2 sec.
8. Repeat.

**Breathing:** OUT while contracting. IN while releasing. Never hold.

**How it should FEEL**
- Back of thigh (hamstring) feels HARD.
- Slight pressure of the heel into the bed.
- Nothing moves visibly (pure isometric).
- Should NOT feel in the knee.
- Should NOT feel the leg move.
- Should NOT feel cramp (if cramps, stop, gently stretch, rehydrate).

**Caregiver says**
- *"Dig your heel into the bed. Don't move it. Just dig down."*
- *"Pretend you're trying to drag the heel to your bum — but the bed is sticky."*
- *"Back of thigh hard — I'll put my hand there. Yes, hard!"*
- *"Hold — 5 seconds."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Heel stays still; back of thigh hardens | Heel actually moves (not isometric) |
| 5-second steady hold | Tap-and-release |
| Breathing flows | Breath held |
| Hamstring visibly/palpably hard | No hamstring activation |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Can't feel hamstring working | Place towel roll slightly larger (more knee bend) — hamstring needs some angle to fire. |
| Knee hurts | Reduce towel roll size (less knee bend). |
| Cramp in back of thigh | Gentle calf stretch, rest 1 min, hydrate. Next session use smaller hold (3 sec). |
| Whole leg moves | Caregiver holds the foot still — "foot doesn't move." |

**You know it's working when:** Hamstring fires reliably; caregiver can feel muscle hardening; contributes to stable walking later.

**Dose**
- Week 1 (from Day 3): 5 reps × 1 set × once daily.
- Week 2: 8 reps × 2 sets × 2 sessions daily.
- Week 3+: 10 reps × 3 sets × 2 sessions daily.

**When to STOP:** Pain in back of knee, sharp pain in thigh, persistent cramp.

### Exercise 18 — Assisted Heel Slide (With Towel)

**Why this exercise**
Some patients plateau at their "active" heel slide ROM — they can only bend the knee so far on their own. This exercise uses a towel to GENTLY pull the heel a few cm further, stretching the knee into slightly deeper flexion. Very helpful if flexion isn't progressing as expected by Week 2+.

**⚠ KEY RULE: Very gentle. Forcing = worse stiffness next day.** Stop at the first strong stretch, never push into sharp pain.

**What you need**
- A long towel, dupatta, bedsheet strip, or yoga strap — at least 2 meters long.
- Bed, thin head pillow.

**Starting position**
1. Lie on back.
2. Thin head pillow.
3. Loop the towel around the ball of the foot (the wider part just under the toes — NOT around the heel, NOT around the ankle).
4. Hold both ends of the towel with both hands, one hand on each side.
5. Other leg stays straight.

**Imagine this**
Imagine you have a leash on your foot. You ACTIVELY slide the heel up (using the hamstring), AND at the end, the leash helps pull the foot a tiny bit further — like a gentle assist from a friend.

**The movement — step by step**

1. **Brace abs** (belly button toward spine — protects low back).
2. **Actively slide heel up** toward buttock (just like Exercise 7 — active heel slide).
3. At the end of your ACTIVE range, **GENTLY pull the towel** to help the heel go a little further. **Only 1–2 cm extra** — not more.
4. **Hold** at this assisted-stretched position for 10 seconds.
5. **Gently release** the towel — but keep the heel where it is briefly.
6. **Slowly slide heel back down** to starting position.
7. Rest 3 sec. Repeat.

**Breathing:** OUT during the active slide up. Breathe normally during the 10-sec hold. IN during slide down.

**How it should FEEL**
- A gentle extra stretch at the FRONT of the knee and front of the thigh.
- NOT a sharp pain.
- NOT a "muscle guarding" sensation (like the leg tightening to resist).
- Should feel you CAN release if needed (not trapped).

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Loop the towel under the ball of the foot. Don't put it on the ankle."*
- During active slide: *"Slide up as far as you can on your own first."*
- At the assist: *"Now gently pull. Just 1–2 cm extra. Don't force."*
- Hold: *"10 seconds. Breathing. Let the muscle relax."*
- Release: *"Let go of the towel. Slide the heel back slowly."*
- Warning: *"If sharp pain, stop pulling — let go of the towel."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Towel loop under BALL of foot | Towel around ankle (wrong anchor) |
| Active slide first, THEN gentle assist | Yanking the towel hard from the start |
| 1–2 cm extra bend | 5+ cm extra (forcing) |
| Muscle stays relaxed during stretch | Muscle "guards" (tightens to resist) |
| 10-sec held stretch | Quick pulse |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Knee pain increases with stretch | Reduce pull. Shorter hold (5 sec). If still pain, stop this exercise for 2 days. |
| Muscle guards / tightens during stretch | Stretch is too aggressive. Back off. Try heat pack on the knee 10 min before. |
| Can't hold towel — arms tired | Place towel wider to both hands, or just loop over one shoulder. |
| Next day knee is stiffer | You forced too much yesterday. Reduce dose by half today. |

**You know it's working when...**
- You can record 2–3° more knee flexion than active heel slide alone.
- Active heel slide improves each week (the assist helps unlock the range).

**Dose**
- Week 2 onwards: 5 reps per leg × 2 sessions per day.
- NOT in Week 1 (active heel slide first, then add assist once technique is safe).
- Best time: 30 min after a warm shower, or after 10 min of a warm compress on the knee — warmth reduces muscle guarding.

**When to STOP:** Sharp pain inside knee. Next-day increased stiffness. Loud click with pain.

### Exercise 19 — Single Knee-to-Chest Stretch

**Why this exercise**
A classic Williams-flexion exercise — one of the most effective positions for relieving chronic low back pain. It also gives GENTLE gravity-assisted knee flexion, contributing to knee ROM recovery. Double-benefit exercise for her.

**What you need**
- Bed with firm mattress.
- Thin head pillow.
- (Optional) Small lumbar roll under low back.

**Starting position**
1. Lie on back.
2. Thin pillow under head.
3. Both knees bent (hook-lying), feet flat on bed.
4. Arms beside body.
5. Small lumbar roll if it helps.

**Imagine this**
Imagine you are hugging your right thigh (not the knee itself — the thigh) and gently bringing your knee toward your chest. The leg folds, the thigh gets closer to the body, the low back gets a gentle stretch.

**The movement — step by step**

1. **Place both hands BEHIND the THIGH** (hamstring area, back of upper leg) of the right leg. **⚠ NEVER place hands behind the knee** — pressure behind the knee stresses the new knee joint and can damage the posterior capsule.
2. **Gently pull the right thigh toward the chest** — both hands doing the work, knee folds naturally.
3. The other leg stays bent with foot on bed (or gently straighten it if straightening doesn't hurt her low back).
4. **Hold 20 seconds.**
5. **Release slowly** — return the leg to start position.
6. Switch sides.
7. Rest 10 sec between sides.

**Breathing:** Relaxed. Breathe IN through nose, OUT through mouth — slow and long. The stretch deepens naturally on each exhale.

**How it should FEEL**
- Gentle stretch in LOW BACK (this is the main target).
- Soft stretch in buttock.
- Gentle knee bend (bonus effect).
- Should NOT feel sharp pulling INSIDE the knee.
- Should NOT feel "pinching" at the front of the hip.
- Should feel her low back gradually sinking into the bed.

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Hands BEHIND your THIGH — not behind the knee. Grab the back of your leg above the knee."*
- During pull: *"Gently bring the thigh toward your chest. Only as far as is comfortable."*
- Hold: *"20 seconds. Breathe. Let it deepen."*
- Warning: *"If you feel the KNEE pulling, stop. It should be BACK pain relief only."*
- Release: *"Let go slowly. Put the foot back on bed."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Hands behind the THIGH (hamstring area) | Hands behind the KNEE (WRONG — stresses knee capsule) |
| Gentle pull, stops at first stretch | Forcing the thigh hard to chest |
| Low back stretch felt | Sharp knee or hip pain felt |
| Other leg stays comfortable (bent OR straight) | Other leg lifts off bed |
| 20-sec sustained hold | Repeated pulses (bouncing) |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Knee pain during pull | Hand placement is too close to knee. Move hands further up the thigh (mid-thigh). |
| Low back doesn't stretch | Pull the thigh further (but gently). Or: keep the other leg straight instead of bent. |
| Hip pinch | She's pulling too far into deep hip flexion. Back off 10°. |
| Both legs want to come up | Caregiver holds the OTHER foot down on the bed with one hand. |

**You know it's working when:** Low back feels more relaxed after the stretch; morning back stiffness reduces.

**Dose**
- Week 2 onwards (from Day 8): 3 reps per leg × 20-sec hold × 1–2 sessions per day.
- Week 3: 3 reps × 30-sec hold × 2 sessions per day.
- Week 4: 3 reps × 45-sec hold × 2 sessions per day.

**Best time:** Morning (before getting out of bed) — reduces overnight back stiffness. Also evening before bed.

**When to STOP:** Sharp knee pain. Pain going down the leg (sciatica). Hip "catches" or clicks painfully.

### Exercise 20 — Seated Hamstring Stretch

**Why this exercise**
The hamstrings are the big muscles on the back of the thigh. When they're TIGHT, they pull the pelvis BACKWARDS when you bend forward, which overloads the low back. 2024 meta-analysis of 14 RCTs showed that stretching the hamstrings significantly reduces chronic low back pain. For her: **this is a double-benefit exercise** — stretches hamstrings (helps LBP) AND maintains knee extension (helps knees).

**What you need**
- Firm bed edge OR firm chair.
- Lumbar roll at low back.
- Non-slip chappals on (so other foot doesn't slip).

**Starting position**
1. Sit at the edge of the bed with ONE leg extended straight along the bed (knee as straight as is comfortable).
2. Other foot flat on the floor (knee bent at ~90°).
3. Hands resting on thighs.
4. Lumbar roll at low back.
5. Sit tall — chest up, shoulders back.

**Imagine this**
Imagine your hip joints are hinges. You tip FORWARD from these hinges (like a drawbridge lowering) — the whole upper body stays straight like a plank, just tilting at the hip. The spine does NOT bend at the waist.

**The movement — step by step**

1. **Brace abs.**
2. Breathe IN.
3. As you breathe OUT, **slowly LEAN FORWARD from the HIPS** (not from the waist).
4. Keep spine STRAIGHT — like a broomstick running from your hips to your head. Chest stays UP.
5. Lean only until you feel a **gentle pull at the BACK of the extended thigh.**
6. **Hold 30 seconds.**
7. Breathe slowly throughout — the stretch deepens on exhale.
8. Slowly sit back up.
9. Switch legs.

**Breathing:** Relaxed throughout. Never hold breath. Stretch deepens on exhale.

**How it should FEEL**
- Gentle pulling sensation at the BACK of the extended thigh (hamstring).
- Spine feels long and straight.
- Low back feels neutral, not rounded.
- Should NOT feel sharp pain at the back of the knee.
- Should NOT feel the spine rounding.
- Should NOT feel pain in the front of the extended knee.

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Sit tall. Chest up. One leg on the bed — straight. Lumbar roll behind you."*
- During lean: *"Lean FORWARD from the hips — like a drawbridge. Keep your chest UP."*
- To check: *"Imagine a broomstick running down your back. Is it straight?"*
- Hold: *"Gentle pull in the back of your thigh. 30 seconds. Breathe slow."*
- Warning if spine rounds: *"No! Chest up. Don't bend your waist. Bend ONLY from the hips."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Spine straight, leaning from hips | Spine rounded, curled forward |
| Chest stays UP | Chest drops toward knee |
| Gentle held stretch | Bouncing in the stretch |
| 30-second sustained hold | 5-second quick touch-and-go |
| Hands on thighs, not pulling on foot | Grabbing the foot and pulling |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Low back rounds when leaning | She's bending the spine, not hinging at hips. Place hand on her low back — tell her to keep it flat. |
| Can't feel stretch at all | Lean slightly more — but keep spine straight. If still no stretch, try supine version (lying on back with towel around foot). |
| Sharp pain behind knee | Overstretching. Come back 20%. Extended leg might be too straight — allow slight bend. |
| Knee of extended leg hurts at front | Support the knee with a rolled towel (to keep it in slight flex). |
| Other foot slips | Non-slip footwear. Non-slip mat on floor. |

**You know it's working when:** Hamstrings feel less tight over weeks; low back feels freer; forward reach tasks (dressing, reaching feet) get easier.

**Dose**
- Week 1 (from Day 6): 3 reps per leg × 20-sec hold × once daily.
- Week 2: 5 reps × 30-sec hold × 2 sessions.
- Week 4: 5 reps × 45-sec hold × 2 sessions.

**Best time:** Evening, after a warm shower — muscles are warm, stretch easier.

**When to STOP:** Sharp pain behind knee. Pain going down the back of the leg (sciatica — call physiotherapist).

### Exercise 21 — Seated Cat-Cow (Spinal Mobility)

**Why this exercise**
Standard yoga "Cat-Cow" requires being on hands and knees — **FORBIDDEN post-TKR** (kneeling damages the implant). This seated version gives ~80% of the spinal-mobility benefit with ZERO knee loading. It mobilizes the low and mid spine in BOTH directions (flexion and extension), breaking up the stiffness from prolonged sitting/lying. Good for her low back.

**What you need**
- Firm chair with a flat seat. Feet flat on floor.

**Starting position**
1. Sit on firm chair — no lumbar roll for this exercise (need spine to move freely).
2. Feet flat on floor, knees 90°.
3. Hands on thighs, lightly.
4. Sit tall to start — "neutral spine."

**Imagine this**
Imagine two yoga poses done while sitting:
- **COW**: Chest puffed out, tailbone lifted, looking up — arching the spine.
- **CAT**: Chest collapsed, tailbone tucked, looking down — rounding the spine.

**The movement — step by step**

1. **COW (inhale, arch):**
   - Breathe IN through nose.
   - Gently tilt the pelvis FORWARD (tailbone lifts, pubic bone drops).
   - Chest lifts UP.
   - Look slightly up toward the ceiling (not all the way, just a gentle chin lift).
   - Spine arches slightly.
2. **CAT (exhale, round):**
   - Breathe OUT through mouth.
   - Gently tilt the pelvis BACKWARD (tailbone tucks under).
   - Pull belly button toward spine.
   - Chest drops slightly, upper back rounds.
   - Look slightly down toward lap (chin gently drops).
3. One COW + one CAT = 1 cycle.
4. Do 5 cycles slowly — each cycle takes about 8 seconds (4 sec inhale, 4 sec exhale).
5. Movement comes from the PELVIS (and spine), NOT just the neck.

**Breathing:** IN during Cow. OUT during Cat. Smooth breathing drives the movement.

**How it should FEEL**
- Gentle wave-like movement traveling through the whole spine.
- Low back, mid-back, and upper-back all move.
- Breathing deepens.
- Should NOT feel sharp pain anywhere.
- Should NOT feel "stuck" — movement should flow.

**Caregiver says**
- Cow: *"Breathe in. Chest up. Tailbone back. Look slightly up."*
- Cat: *"Breathe out. Tailbone tuck. Belly in. Chin down."*
- Rhythm: *"In — cow. Out — cat. In — cow. Out — cat."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Movement from pelvis and spine | Only neck moves (head nodding) |
| Breath synced with movement | Breath holding |
| Slow smooth wave | Jerky |
| Subtle range | Extreme arching or rounding |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Neck sore after cycles | Don't tip head as far. Keep neck gentle. |
| Doesn't feel the pelvic movement | Place hands on the sides of the hips — feel the bones tilt forward and back. |
| Low back pinch on Cow | Reduce arch — just a small movement. |
| Feels too small a movement | Good! This is a gentle spinal mobility exercise, not a big stretch. |

**You know it's working when:** Low back feels less stiff mid-day; sitting for long periods becomes easier.

**Dose**
- Week 2 (from Day 12): 5 cycles × 2 sessions per day.
- Week 3+: 10 cycles × 2 sessions per day.
- Can do anytime she's been sitting for > 20 min (break up sitting stiffness).

**Best time:** Mid-morning and mid-afternoon breaks from sitting.

**When to STOP:** Sharp back pain. Dizziness (rare).

### Exercise 22 — Supine Hip Abduction (Isometric)

**Why this exercise**
The gluteus medius is a hip muscle on the side of the buttock that STABILIZES the pelvis when you walk. When it's weak, the pelvis drops sideways with each step, causing a "Trendelenburg" limp — common after bilateral TKR. This muscle also SUPPORTS the sacroiliac joint (directly helps her LBP). This "isometric" version wakes up the muscle safely in bed — no knee loading, no stress on the SI joint.

**What you need**
- Bed.
- Two pillows placed on either side of the thighs (or caregiver's hands).

**Starting position**
1. Lie on back.
2. Thin head pillow.
3. Both knees slightly bent over a small rolled towel (10° bend — not much).
4. Place one firm pillow on the OUTSIDE of each thigh — so the thighs are flanked by pillows. (Or husband uses his hands on the outsides of her thighs.)

**Imagine this**
Imagine your thighs are a pair of doors trying to OPEN OUTWARD — pushing OUTWARD against the pillows. The pillows resist, so the thighs don't actually move. Your hip muscles on the OUTSIDE of your buttocks work hard.

**The movement — step by step**

1. Breathe IN.
2. As you breathe OUT, **press the OUTSIDE of BOTH thighs OUTWARD against the pillows** — as if trying to push the pillows apart.
3. The pillows resist — thighs don't actually move.
4. The muscles on the SIDE of the buttocks (gluteus medius) feel hard.
5. Hold 5 sec.
6. Release 2 sec.
7. Repeat.

**Breathing:** OUT during the push. IN during release.

**How it should FEEL**
- Muscles on the SIDE of the buttocks (not back of buttocks) feel working/hard.
- Caregiver can place hand on the side of the buttock and feel the muscle harden.
- Should NOT feel anything in the knees.
- Should NOT feel pain in the low back.

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Pillow on the outside of each thigh. Push the pillows OUT with the OUTSIDE of your thighs."*
- Alternative: *"I'll use my hands. Push against my hands."*
- Feel check: *"I'll feel the side of your buttock. Yes — it's hard. That's the muscle working."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Pillows stay in place (isometric — no movement) | Thighs actually move outward (turns into dynamic exercise — wrong for Week 2) |
| Side of buttock hard | Only back of buttock hard (wrong muscle) |
| 5-sec hold | Quick pulse |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Can't feel the side of the buttock | Caregiver places hand on the side of the hip — she pushes into the hand. |
| Back of buttock works instead | She's doing gluteal squeeze, not abduction. Cue: "push the THIGH outward, not the buttock." |

**Dose**
- Week 2 (from Day 9): 8 reps, 5-sec hold × 1 set × once daily.
- Week 3: 10 reps × 2 sets × 2 sessions.
- Week 4+: Progress to side-lying version (true dynamic hip abduction — see Week 4 plan in §5).

### Exercise 23 — Standing Weight Shift (With Walker)

**Why this exercise**
Teaches the knees to TAKE FULL WEIGHT again, one at a time, without the fear of falling. Builds confidence. Also prepares for eventual walker-free walking — you need to be able to shift weight one leg to the other smoothly to walk normally.

**What you need**
- Standard walker.
- Non-slip chappals.
- Flat floor area.
- Husband beside her for safety.

**Starting position**
1. Standing INSIDE the walker frame.
2. Both hands on walker grips.
3. Feet shoulder-width apart.
4. Stand TALL — chest up, shoulders back.
5. Weight evenly on both legs initially.

**Imagine this**
Imagine you are pouring water from one foot to the other — slowly, the water flows into the left foot (left foot feels heavy), then slowly flows back to the right foot (right foot feels heavy). Both feet stay on the floor the entire time — only the WEIGHT shifts.

**The movement — step by step**

1. **Brace abs.**
2. **Slowly shift weight to LEFT foot.** Don't lift the right foot — just shift weight. The right foot becomes light on the floor.
3. Count: "one, two, three." (3-sec shift in Week 1, 5-sec Week 2+.)
4. **Hold** with weight on left for 3 seconds.
5. **Slowly shift weight BACK to BOTH feet** (centered).
6. **Slowly shift weight to RIGHT foot.** Left foot becomes light.
7. Hold 3 sec.
8. Return to center.
9. That's 1 cycle. Repeat.

**Breathing:** Slow, calm, continuous.

**How it should FEEL**
- Pressure in the weighted foot's sole.
- Knee of weighted leg bears more force — a gentle working sensation.
- Pelvis stays LEVEL (not dropping on the unweighted side).
- Should NOT feel knee "give way."
- Should NOT feel dizziness.

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Stand inside the walker. Both hands on grips. Tall."*
- Shift: *"Shift weight to your LEFT foot. Slow. 1, 2, 3. Hold."*
- Check: *"Can you lift your right foot just a tiny bit? No — don't lift, just lighten."*
- Return: *"Back to center. Now to the RIGHT. 1, 2, 3. Hold."*
- If knee buckles: *"Sit down on the walker seat. Rest."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Both feet stay on floor | Unweighted foot lifts (too much shift) |
| Slow 3-sec controlled shift | Quick wobbly shift |
| Pelvis stays level | Hip drops on unweighted side |
| Hands lightly resting on walker | Hands gripping tightly (means she's unsafe) |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Wobbles | Reduce shift (only 30% of weight onto one side initially). Build confidence over a week. |
| Knee of weighted leg hurts | Shift less weight. Reduce hold time. |
| Pelvis drops | Gluteus medius weak — continue Exercise 22 isometric abduction first. |

**You know it's working when:** After 2 weeks, she can confidently take full weight on each leg one at a time; pelvis stays level; less walker dependence.

**Dose**
- Week 1 (from Day 6): 10 shifts each way × 1 set × once daily.
- Week 2: 10 each way × 2 sets × 2 sessions.
- Week 3+: Increase hold to 5 sec; try with ONE hand off walker.
- Week 5+: Precursor to single-leg stance (Exercise 30).

### Exercise 24 — Supported Standing Hip Extension

**Why this exercise**
Wakes up the gluteus maximus (big buttock muscle). This muscle is needed for normal walking — pushing the leg BACKWARD as you walk. Weak glutes = knee hyperextension while walking (bad for implant). Also directly supports her low back (glutes unload the spine).

**What you need**
- Sturdy counter, heavy table, or wall.
- Walker beside her for safety.
- Non-slip chappals.

**Starting position**
1. Stand facing a sturdy counter, heavy table, or wall.
2. Both hands on the counter/table/wall at hip height.
3. Walker beside her (within reach for safety).
4. Feet hip-width apart.
5. Both knees slightly bent (5–10° — not locked straight).
6. Brace abs.

**Imagine this**
Imagine you are gently kicking a small balloon BEHIND you with one leg. You don't bend the leg — you lift the WHOLE straight leg backward from the hip. Only 15–20° back — small movement, big benefit.

**The movement — step by step**

1. Brace abs.
2. Breathe OUT.
3. **Lift ONE straight leg BACKWARD** — only 15–20° (imagine just unsticking your heel from the floor by a few cm, then lifting).
4. **Trunk stays upright** — do NOT lean forward. The chest stays OVER the toes. If the chest goes over the front knee, it's a SPINE movement, not a HIP movement. Wrong.
5. Hold 2 sec at the back.
6. Slowly lower the leg.
7. Switch legs.

**Breathing:** OUT while lifting back. IN while lowering.

**How it should FEEL**
- Working feeling in the BUTTOCK of the leg being lifted.
- Slight stretch in the front of the hip of that leg.
- Standing leg knee feels slightly engaged (bearing weight).
- Should NOT feel in the low back.
- Should NOT feel the spine arching.

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Face the counter. Hands on the counter. Walker beside you."*
- Movement: *"Lift your leg straight back. Small lift — 15 degrees."*
- Watch from side: *"I'm watching from the side. Chest stays over your toes. Don't lean forward."*
- Check: *"Feel the buttock working? I'll put my hand there — yes, hard!"*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Trunk stays upright | Leaning forward (spine extends instead) |
| Leg lifts 15–20° back | Leg lifts 40°+ (spine helps) |
| Knee stays slightly bent | Standing knee locks straight |
| Buttock visibly works | Low back arches to "lift" the leg |
| Slow 2-sec hold | Quick flick |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Leans forward to lift leg | Caregiver stands behind, hand on the low back — tell her "my hand stays flat on your back, don't let it arch." |
| Can't feel buttock | Leg lift too high (spine helps). Reduce range. Focus on "squeeze the buttock FIRST, then lift." |
| Low back arches | Reduce range. Brace abs harder. |
| Loses balance | Both hands on counter, feet slightly wider apart. |

**Dose**
- Week 2 (from Day 9): 5 reps per leg × 1 set × once daily.
- Week 3: 6 reps × 2 sets.
- Week 4: 8 reps × 2 sets.
- Week 6+: Add light ankle weight (0.5 kg).

### Exercise 25 — Mini-Bridge (Small Hip Lift)

**Why this exercise**
Wakes up glute maximus (big buttock muscle). Helps both knee recovery (prepares for standing up from chair, stair climbing) AND low back (glute max stabilizes the sacroiliac joint via the sacrotuberous ligament — directly addresses a root cause of her chronic LBP).

**⚠ CRITICAL: This exercise must be done with PERFECT technique or it WILL flare her low back pain.** If done wrong (arching back, no glute squeeze first, pushing feet forward), it is the #1 exercise that causes low-back flares. Done right, it's one of the best exercises for her.

**What you need**
- Bed (firm mattress is better than soft).
- Thin head pillow.

**Starting position**
1. Lie on back (supine).
2. Thin head pillow.
3. **Both knees BENT (hook-lying), feet FLAT on bed**, hip-width apart.
4. **Feet hip-width, HEELS DIRECTLY UNDER THE KNEES** — NOT pushed forward. This position is critical.
   - Test: when you look straight down, your heels and knees should line up vertically.
   - Feet too far forward = back works instead of glutes.
5. Arms beside body.

**Imagine this**
Imagine you are squeezing a coin between your buttocks (glute squeeze), THEN your hips gently rise as if being lifted by strings attached to your belt buckle. Only 3–5 cm up. Don't go higher. The line from shoulders to knees is straight — like a "plank."

**The movement — step by step**

1. **FIRST: Brace abs** (3-in-1 brace — breathe out, pull belly IN, Kegel UP).
2. **NEXT: Squeeze BOTH buttocks HARD** — glutes fire FIRST, BEFORE any movement. This is the most critical step.
3. **THEN: Gently lift hips** — only 3–5 cm off the bed. Body line: shoulders — hips — knees all in a straight line, NOT higher (if higher = arching back).
4. **Hold 3 seconds** — keep glutes squeezed, keep abs braced.
5. **Slowly lower** with control (count 3 sec). Don't just drop.
6. Rest 2 sec.
7. Repeat.

**Breathing:** OUT (with glute squeeze + abs brace) during the lift. IN during the lowering.

**How it should FEEL**
- Strong working feeling in the BUTTOCKS (glute max).
- Moderate working in the back of the thighs (hamstrings).
- Low back feels NEUTRAL — not arched, not pressed down.
- Should NOT feel the low back working.
- Should NOT feel sharp pain anywhere.

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Bend both knees. Feet flat. Heels UNDER your knees — not in front."*
- FIRST: *"Brace your belly. Tight abs."*
- SECOND: *"Squeeze your buttocks HARD. Like holding a coin."*
- THIRD: *"Now lift — only 3 cm. Small. Small!"*
- Check: *"Body is a straight line. Not arched."*
- Lowering: *"Slow. 1, 2, 3. Don't drop."*
- If back arches: *"STOP. Come down. You lifted too high."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Glutes squeeze FIRST, then hips lift | Hips lift with no glute squeeze (back does the work) |
| 3–5 cm lift only (barely off the bed) | 10+ cm lift (full bridge — back arches) |
| Shoulders — hips — knees in straight line | Hips lift higher than shoulders (back arch) |
| Heels under knees | Feet pushed forward |
| Abs stay braced | Belly pushes out |
| Breath flows | Breath held |
| Low back stays neutral | Low back arches (PAIN RISK) |

**Troubleshoot (STOP if any of these happen)**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Low back pain during lift | STOP immediately. Reduce lift to 2 cm. If still pain, stop this exercise for 3 days. Do Exercise 6 (glute squeeze) and Exercise 14 (pelvic tilt) instead. |
| Can't feel glutes working | Squeeze buttocks HARD before lifting — fully clench coin between them. |
| Hamstring cramp | Rest 2 min. Stretch. Feet slightly further forward next time. |
| Hips wobble | Feet too wide. Bring feet closer together (hip-width). |
| Tired after 3 reps | Normal early. Rest 1 min, try 2 more. |

**You know it's working when:** Week 2+ she can do 10 reps without fatigue. Glutes are visibly engaging during everyday tasks (standing up, stairs).

**Dose**
- Week 1 (Day 7 only, cautious attempt): 5 reps × 1 set. CHECK FOR LBP NEXT DAY.
- Week 2: 6–8 reps × 2 sets × once daily.
- Week 3: 10 reps, 5-sec hold × 2 sets.
- Week 4+: Add small pillow between knees; squeeze pillow during the lift (activates glute-medius + stabilizes SI joint).

**Best time:** Mid-afternoon (2:30–4:00 PM) when analgesia is at peak.

**When to STOP this exercise (not just session):**
- Low back pain after 2 consecutive sessions
- Hamstring cramp 2 days in a row
- Knee pain during the lift
- Pelvic pain (SI joint irritated)

### Exercise 26 — Short-Arc Quads (SAQ, Rolled Towel Under Knee)

**Why this exercise**
Intermediate exercise between quad sets (no movement) and full seated knee extension (full range). The range is SMALL (last 30° of knee extension only), so it's safer early on. This is the exercise that often helps the quad "wake up" when SLR is not yet possible — it builds the critical last-bit-of-extension strength needed for a stable knee.

**What you need**
- Rolled towel ~15 cm thick, placed under the knee being exercised.
- **⚠ IMPORTANT:** This is the ONLY exercise where a towel goes UNDER the knee. Normally, no pillow under the knee. For SAQ only, for the duration of this exercise only, it is correct. REMOVE the towel after the set.

**Starting position**
1. Lie on back.
2. Thin head pillow.
3. Place rolled towel under the knee of the exercising leg (knee is slightly bent over the towel, ~30° flex).
4. Heel of exercising leg is on the bed (not lifted yet).
5. Other leg straight.

**Imagine this**
Imagine your lower leg is like a lever. The towel is the pivot point (fulcrum). When you tighten the thigh, the heel LIFTS, and the lower leg straightens. The back of the thigh stays resting on the towel.

**The movement — step by step**

1. Brace abs.
2. Breathe OUT.
3. **Tighten the front thigh** (quad squeeze).
4. **Slowly lift the HEEL 2–3 inches (5–8 cm) off the bed.** The lower leg straightens. The BACK of the thigh stays resting on the towel.
5. **Hold 5 seconds** at the top — leg fully straight at the knee.
6. Slowly lower heel back to the bed (count 3 sec).
7. Rest 2 sec.
8. Repeat.

**After the set, REMOVE the towel before any other exercise.**

**Breathing:** OUT while lifting. IN while lowering.

**How it should FEEL**
- Strong work in the front of the thigh (near the knee).
- Knee goes fully straight at the top.
- Should NOT feel pain inside the knee.
- Should NOT feel the low back.

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Place rolled towel under your knee. Heel on bed."*
- During lift: *"Tighten your thigh. Lift the heel — just a few inches. Knee goes STRAIGHT at the top."*
- Hold: *"Hold 5. Keep the thigh on the towel."*
- Lower: *"Slow down."*
- After set: *"Now remove the towel for other exercises."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Back of thigh stays on towel | Whole leg lifts off towel (turns into SLR — different exercise) |
| Knee fully straight at top | Knee still bent at top |
| Heel rises 2–3 inches | Heel jumps up 8 inches |
| 5-sec hold | Quick tap |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Whole leg lifts off towel | Caregiver presses lightly on the thigh to keep it on the towel. Only lower leg rises. |
| Knee doesn't fully straighten at top | Continue quad sets (Exercise 5) more. The last-bit of extension improves over time. |
| Thigh cramps | Rest, gentle stretch. Reduce reps this session. |
| Towel rolls away | Use a bigger/firmer towel or wrap tightly with string. |

**You know it's working when:** She can fully extend the knee at the top with no "lag" (heel stays in line with the thigh). Standing up from chair becomes easier.

**Dose**
- Week 1 (from Day 5): 5 reps × 2 sets × once daily.
- Week 2: 8 reps × 2 sets × 2 sessions.
- Week 3+: 8 reps × 3 sets; range can increase (use taller towel for deeper bend).

**When to STOP:** Sharp knee pain. Click with pain.

### Exercise 27 — Mini-Squats (With Walker)

**Why this exercise**
Functional strengthening — the movement is the same as getting up from a chair, climbing stairs, or standing from a low surface. Builds quad + glute strength together. **⚠ Critical:** "Mini" means SHALLOW — never go deep, even if she feels strong. Deep squats damage the implant.

**What you need**
- Standard walker on firm floor.
- Non-slip footwear.
- Husband beside her for safety.

**Starting position**
1. Standing INSIDE walker frame.
2. Both hands on walker grips.
3. Feet shoulder-width apart, toes pointing forward.
4. Walker firmly on floor — all 4 legs flat.
5. Stand TALL.
6. Brace abs.

**Imagine this**
Imagine you are sitting down onto a tall chair RIGHT behind you — but the chair is only 30 cm behind your buttocks, not far. So you only lower a SHORT distance. Back stays STRAIGHT (not bending forward). Knees bend. Just a small dip, then stand back up.

**The movement — step by step**

1. Brace abs.
2. Breathe IN.
3. **Slowly bend both knees** — SHALLOW squat:
   - Week 2 (Day 10+): **ONLY 30° bend** (a very small dip).
   - Week 4: **40° bend.**
   - Week 6: **60° bend** — maximum allowed. **NEVER deeper.**
4. **Back stays STRAIGHT** — do NOT hunch forward. Chest up.
5. **Knees stay ABOVE the ankles** — NOT jutting forward past the toes (damaging to knees).
6. **Hold 3 seconds at the bottom.**
7. Breathe OUT.
8. **Slowly rise** back to standing.
9. Pause 2 sec. Repeat.

**Breathing:** IN while bending down. OUT while rising.

**How it should FEEL**
- Strong working feeling in BOTH thighs and buttocks.
- Weight mostly in the heels (not on the balls of the feet).
- Pressure in the hands from the walker.
- Should NOT feel sharp knee pain.
- Should NOT feel low back pain.
- Should NOT feel dizzy (if dizzy, stop).

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Inside the walker. Both hands on grips. Feet shoulder-width."*
- Descent: *"Slow bend. Shallow. Just 30 degrees in Week 2."*
- Watch from side: *"Chest UP. Not leaning forward. Straight back."*
- Watch knees: *"Knees over ankles. Not past the toes."*
- Hold: *"Hold — 3 seconds."*
- Rise: *"Slow up. Breathe out. Push through heels."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Shallow bend (30° Week 2) | Deep squat (60°+ early on — damages implant) |
| Back straight, chest up | Back hunched forward |
| Knees over ankles | Knees past the toes |
| Weight in heels | Weight on balls of feet (knees forward) |
| Slow 3-sec hold | Bouncing |
| Rising on heels | Rising on toes |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Knees hurt at the bottom | Going too deep. Reduce depth. |
| Knees jut past toes | Push hips BACKWARD more — "sit back into a chair." |
| Back hunches forward | Chest up. Imagine a ceiling above your head — don't bring your face toward the floor. |
| Can't rise | Caregiver assists by putting hands under her arms and helping lift. Reduce depth next time. |
| Loses balance | Both hands firmly on walker. Feet slightly wider. |

**Dose**
- Week 2 (Day 10 onwards): 5 reps × 1 set × once daily.
- Week 3: 6 reps × 2 sets.
- Week 4: 8 reps × 2 sets, 40° depth.
- Week 6: 10 reps × 3 sets, 60° depth — **max depth forever.**

**⚠ PERMANENT RULE: NEVER deeper than 60° knee bend during a squat, even after full recovery.** Deeper squats damage the implant over time.

**Best time:** Mid-afternoon when analgesia is at peak.

**When to STOP:**
- Sharp knee pain
- Knee buckles
- Low back pain
- Dizzy on rising

### Exercise 29 — Step Rehearsal (4 cm Block)

**Why this exercise**
A preparation exercise for stair climbing. Starting with a very small step (4 cm — thickness of a thick book) lets her practice the movement pattern safely before tackling real stairs. Real stairs are 17 cm high — too much for Week 2.

**What you need**
- Thick book, yoga block, or small wooden board — about 4 cm (1.5 inches) tall.
- Sturdy counter, heavy table, or wall for one-hand support.
- Non-slip footwear.

**Starting position**
1. Standing facing the book/block.
2. One hand on a counter or wall for balance.
3. Walker beside as safety (husband may place his hand under her elbow).
4. Stand tall. Brace abs.

**Imagine this**
Imagine you are about to step up onto a low step. Place one foot on the step, shift weight, step back down. Rehearsal only — don't try to push up with force yet.

**The movement — step by step (Week 2 — rehearsal only, NO push-up)**

1. **Place one foot FULLY on the book** (whole foot, not just toes).
2. **Shift weight slightly forward** onto that foot.
3. **Step back down** — no push-up, no standing on the book yet.
4. Repeat with same leg.
5. After 5 reps, switch legs.

**Progression — Week 3 (real step-up with push-up)**

1. Place one foot on the book.
2. Lean forward onto that foot.
3. **Push up through that foot's HEEL** — rise onto the book. Stand fully on top.
4. Bring other foot up to stand beside.
5. Step down one foot at a time.

**Breathing:** Normal throughout.

**How it should FEEL**
- Weight shift clearly onto one foot.
- Slight work in the front of the thigh of the leading leg.
- Balance OK with hand on counter.
- Should NOT feel knee pain.
- Should NOT feel balance loss.

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Book on the floor in front of you. Hand on the counter. Ready."*
- Movement: *"Place right foot on the book. Lean weight forward. Now step back down."*
- Rehearsal Week 2: *"We are NOT pushing up yet. Just practice placing the foot."*
- Week 3: *"Now try to stand ON the book. Push up through your heel."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Foot fully on block | Only toes or half-foot |
| Hand stays on counter for safety | Hand not on support |
| Slow controlled step | Hopping or bouncing |
| Weight pushes through heel | Weight on ball of foot (knee forward) |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Balance lost | Use both walker + wall for support in early tries. |
| Knee pain on push-up | Go back to rehearsal (no push-up) another week. |
| Can't lift body up onto block | Normal in Week 2. Rehearsal is enough. Strength builds. |

**Dose**
- Week 2 (Day 12): 5 reps per leg × 1 set × once daily (rehearsal only).
- Week 3: 6 reps × 2 sets (now with small push-up — stand fully on book).
- Week 4+: Progress to 10 cm book, then 15 cm (real stair height).
- Week 6+: Real stairs with handrail.
- Week 10+: Lateral step-ups (sideways onto block).

**When to STOP:** Sharp knee pain. Balance loss. Foot slips off block.

### Exercise 30 — Single-Leg Stance

**Why this exercise**
The ultimate balance training — standing on one leg at a time. Needed for eventual walker-free walking. BUT: **This is the riskiest exercise in the whole program for bilateral TKR**, because when she stands on one leg, ALL her body weight goes onto the OTHER new knee. If that knee isn't strong enough, it can buckle. For this reason, we DELAY this exercise until Week 5.

**⚠ EARLIEST SAFE DAY: Week 5 (Day 29+).** Before this, both knees are too weak for safe one-leg stance.

**Pre-requisite checklist (all must be YES before trying):**
- [ ] Can stand with walker for > 30 seconds without trembling
- [ ] Full knee extension (0°) achieved on both sides
- [ ] Knee flexion ≥ 100° both sides
- [ ] SLR (straight leg raise) without any lag — both sides
- [ ] No swelling at end of day
- [ ] Apixaban already stopped (after 3-May)
- [ ] Husband able to stand behind as active spotter

If any unchecked → wait another week.

**What you need**
- Standard walker on firm floor.
- **Husband stands BEHIND her, hands at her hip level** (active spotter — not just nearby).
- Clear space (no furniture, no rugs).

**Starting position**
1. Stand INSIDE the walker frame.
2. Both hands on walker grips.
3. Feet shoulder-width apart.
4. Stand tall. Brace abs.
5. Look forward (not at feet).
6. Husband positioned behind, hands ready at hip level.

**Imagine this**
Imagine you are standing tall like a tree. When you lift one foot, the other foot is the "root" — steady, strong. The whole body stays upright, trunk centered over the standing leg.

**The movement — step by step**

1. Brace abs.
2. **Slowly lift ONE foot 5 cm off the floor** (only 2 inches — not higher).
3. **Hold 3 seconds.**
4. Lower the foot to the floor.
5. Pause 5 sec (regain stability).
6. Switch legs.

**Breathing:** Normal, slow.

**How it should FEEL**
- Strong work in the standing leg (hip, thigh, calf, foot).
- Pelvis STAYS LEVEL (doesn't drop on the lifted side).
- Trunk stays centered over the standing foot.
- Should NOT feel the standing knee "buckle" or "shake."
- Should NOT feel she's about to fall.

**Caregiver says**
- Position check: *"I'm standing right behind you, hands at your hips. If you lose balance, I'll catch you."*
- Setup: *"Tall in the walker. Both hands on grips."*
- Lift: *"Lift your right foot — just 2 inches. Hold — 1, 2, 3."*
- Encourage: *"Standing leg is strong. Trust it."*
- Lower: *"Foot back down. Rest."*
- If she wobbles: *"It's OK. Put it down. Rest. Try again."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Only 5 cm lift | Big lift (easier to lose balance) |
| Pelvis stays level | Pelvis drops on lifted side (Trendelenburg — glute weak) |
| Standing leg quiet, stable | Standing knee shakes or buckles |
| Hands lightly on walker | Clutching walker (means unsafe) |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Knee buckles | STOP. Sit down. Not ready yet. Continue Exercise 22 (hip abduction) + glute strengthening for another week. |
| Pelvis drops | Gluteus medius weak. Continue Exercise 22 (side-lying hip abduction) for 2 more weeks. |
| Wobbles after 1 sec | Start with just 1-sec holds. Build to 3 sec. |
| Can't lift foot at all | Not ready. Wait 1–2 more weeks. |
| Fear of falling | Husband stands beside her WITH hand firmly on hip. Extra security. |

**Dose**
- Week 5: 3 reps per leg × 1 set × once daily.
- Week 8: 5 reps × 10-sec hold.
- Week 12: 5 reps × 30-sec hold.
- Progress to: hands off walker (but walker beside) → eyes closed (advanced) → standing on an uneven surface.

**When to STOP this exercise entirely:**
- Fall during the exercise
- Knee buckle repeatedly
- Persistent Trendelenburg drop (means pelvis muscles still too weak)

### Exercise 31 — Tandem Stance (Heel-to-Toe)

**Why this exercise**
Advanced balance training. Standing with one foot directly in front of the other narrows the base of support and challenges balance. Prepares for walking on uneven surfaces (rural mud paths in her context) and improves dynamic balance for fall prevention.

**What you need**
- A sturdy counter, wall, or kitchen platform for initial support.
- Walker beside as safety.
- Clear space.
- Non-slip footwear.

**Starting position**
1. Stand facing a counter (or kitchen platform), within arm's reach.
2. **One foot directly in FRONT of the other** — heel of front foot touching the toes of back foot (like standing on a balance beam).
3. Both hands lightly on the counter for support.
4. Walker beside as safety (within reach if needed).
5. Stand tall. Brace abs.

**Imagine this**
Imagine you are walking along a narrow plank or tightrope. Both feet are in a line. You are standing still on this line — not falling to either side.

**The movement — mostly stillness**

1. Brace abs.
2. **Hold the heel-to-toe position** — stay still and balanced.
3. Look forward (not at feet — causes wobble).
4. Week 2: 10 sec hold.
5. Week 4: 15 sec.
6. Week 10+: 30 sec.
7. Rest. Swap which foot is in front. Repeat.

**Breathing:** Normal, slow.

**How it should FEEL**
- A balance challenge — slight sway is OK.
- Muscles around the hips and ankles working to stabilize.
- Should NOT feel panic or fall risk.
- Should NOT feel knee pain.

**Caregiver says**
- Setup: *"Heel of your front foot touches toes of back foot. Hands on counter."*
- Hold: *"Stay still. Look forward, not at your feet. 10 seconds."*
- Swap: *"Now switch — right foot back, left foot in front."*

**GOOD rep vs BAD rep**

| GOOD | BAD |
|---|---|
| Feet in a line, heel touching toes | Feet too far apart (not tandem) |
| Hands lightly on counter | Clutching for dear life |
| Looking forward | Looking at feet (increases wobble) |
| Still posture | Wobbling side to side |

**Troubleshoot**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Keeps losing balance | Partial tandem — front foot 10 cm in front (not full heel-to-toe). Build up. |
| Knee pain in front leg | Reduce hold time. |
| Dizzy | Don't close eyes. Look at a fixed point on the wall. |

**Dose**
- Week 2 (Day 13): At counter with both hands, **10-sec hold × 3 reps per foot-forward × once daily.**
- Week 4: Free-standing with walker beside as safety, 15-sec × 3 reps.
- Week 10+: 30-sec × 3 reps.
- Advanced (Month 3+): Eyes closed (only on soft surface, caregiver behind).

**Progression to tandem walking (Week 10+):** Take slow heel-to-toe steps along a straight line. 10 steps × 2 times. Walker beside.

**When to STOP:** Fall risk. Severe dizziness. Knee pain.

## SECTION 7 — HOW TO USE ICE, ELEVATION, AND PILLOWS

### Ice — detailed technique

**What you need**
- Zip-lock plastic bag OR clean cloth bag.
- Ice cubes or crushed ice (~2 cups).
- 2–3 spoons water (optional — helps bag mold).
- Thin cotton cloth or thin towel for wrapping.

**Never**
- Direct ice on skin.
- Ice on open wound.
- More than 20 min per session.

**How to make**
1. Ice + water in bag.
2. Push air out, seal.
3. Wrap bag in ONE thin cloth.

**How to use**
1. Lie on back, legs elevated (see Exercise 4).
2. Place ice pack on knee — cover front, sides, behind if possible.
3. Timer: 15–20 minutes.
4. Remove. Check skin — should be pink and cold, not white or with red blotches.
5. Pat dry.

**When**
- After every exercise session — 15 min.
- Any time the knee feels hot, throbbing, or swollen.
- Before sleep (helps sleep).
- Weeks 1–2: 4–6 times per day.
- Weeks 3–4: 2–3 times per day.

**Stop if:** Skin white/waxy, red blotches (sensitivity), itching/burning.

## SECTION 8 — OPTIMAL TIMING & INTEGRATION WITH MEDICATIONS

### Peak Analgesia Windows — when is she "best covered" for harder exercises?

This table shows when each medication is in her system vs when she's at risk from sedation / side effects. Use it to plan harder exercise sessions.

| Time | Medication status | Exercise readiness |
|---|---|---|
| 6:00–7:00 AM | Pantocid just taken; no pain coverage yet; morning stiffness high | ❌ Avoid exercise. Rest, hydrate. |
| 7:30–8:30 AM | Breakfast + post-breakfast cluster taken; NSAID (NAC-SR) absorbing | 🟡 Only gentle bed exercises (breathing, ankle pumps, isometrics) |
| **9:30–11:30 AM** | NSAID climbing toward peak; Gabapin sedation peak passing | ✅ **GOOD window for moderate ROM work** (heel slides, seated extensions, pelvic tilts) |
| 11:30 AM–1:30 PM | NSAID near peak plateau | ✅ **GOOD window for strengthening** (quad sets, bridging attempts, seated extensions) |
| **1:30–2:30 PM** | NSAID AT PEAK PLASMA level (Tmax ~5 h for diclofenac SR) | 🟢 **BEST window — hardest session of the day** — do new/progressed exercises here if possible |
| 2:30–3:00 PM | Lunch digestion | 🟡 Wait 30 min after lunch before strenuous work |
| **3:00–5:00 PM** | NSAID plateau continues; body still alert | ✅ **2nd best window** — repeat best exercises if needed |
| 5:00–7:00 PM | NSAID slowly falling; Gabapin dose approaching | 🟡 Light exercise only (gentle stretches, repeat ROM sessions) |
| 7:00–8:00 PM | Within 1 h of dinner | ❌ No strenuous work |
| 8:30–9:00 PM | Post-dinner cluster + Gabapin rising | ❌ No balance or strength work (fall risk) |
| 9:00–9:15 PM | **Terifrac injection window** | ❌ Patient must be SEATED, not exercising |
| 9:15–10:30 PM | Gabapin peaking; body winding down | 🟡 Gentle bed-based exercises only (breathing, Kegels) |
| 10:30 PM – 7 AM | Gabapin-NT sedation | ❌ **NO EXERCISE — fall risk highest** |
| After Ultracet dose (if taken) | Tramadol peak 1–3 h; sedation and dizziness | ❌ **NO exercise for 3–4 hours after Ultracet** |

**Key correction (from pharmacokinetic research):** Earlier versions assumed diclofenac SR peaks at 2 hours — this is WRONG. Sustained-release diclofenac has **Tmax ~5 hours**. So the 8:30 AM dose peaks at **1:30–2:30 PM**, not 10:30 AM. This makes **early afternoon the single best window** for hard exercises, not mid-morning.

### Best times to exercise — and why

**GREEN WINDOWS (best for harder exercises):**

| Time | Why it's good |
|---|---|
| **10:00–11:30 AM** | Morning NSAID absorbed (8:30 AM dose rising to peak); post-breakfast recovery; not yet peak drowsiness from Gabapin |
| **2:30–4:30 PM** | NAC-SR peak plasma (~1:30–2:30 PM) — maximum analgesia plateau; lunch digested; mid-afternoon wakefulness |
| **6:00–7:00 PM** | Evening slot BEFORE the evening Gabapin dose makes her drowsy |

**RED WINDOWS (do NOT do harder exercises):**

| Time | Why not |
|---|---|
| **Before 10 AM** | Morning NSAID not yet at full effect; stiffness from overnight |
| **Within 1 hour of any meal** | GI distribution of blood; discomfort |
| **Within 3–4 hours AFTER any Ultracet dose** | Peak drowsiness; fall risk |
| **After 8 PM** | Evening NSAID rising but Gabapin already kicking in; approaching Gabapin-NT 11 PM — NO balance or strength work |
| **During Terifrac seated-rest window (9:00–9:15 PM)** | Orthostatic hypotension risk |

### Pre-exercise pain plan

1. **Scheduled NAC-SR (diclofenac SR) twice daily at 8:30 AM and 8:30 PM — never skip.** This provides the background analgesia.
2. **Do NOT take extra NSAID before exercise** — steady-state plasma levels are already high enough.
3. **Ultracet BEFORE exercise — only if:**
   - Pain at rest is > 4/10, OR
   - The session is the day's hardest (introducing new exercises, bridging, stretching, etc.), AND
   - Session is at least 60 min later (Ultracet onset ~30 min), AND
   - Session is ≥ 4 hours before 7 PM cutoff.
4. **Never combine Ultracet with Gabapin-NT at 11 PM.**
5. **Last Ultracet of the day ≤ 7:00 PM.**

### Post-exercise routine (every session)

1. **Immediately (0–5 min):** Sit, drink water, check pulse (should be < 110 beats/min within 5 min).
2. **Ice 15–20 min** with leg elevated (wrap ice in thin cloth).
3. **2 hours later:** Check pain. If > 2 points above pre-session baseline and still rising → dose was too heavy. Cut next session 30 %.
4. **24 hours later:** Check knee circumference with tape measure (same time as morning baseline). If > 2 cm larger → rest day tomorrow, then resume at HALF.

## SECTION 10 — WARNING SIGNS OF DOING TOO MUCH

### The 5 signs

1. **Knee swells more than usual.** Normal: slightly fuller after exercise. Warning: tight shiny skin, visibly bigger than morning.
2. **Knee becomes HOT to touch.** Normal: warm. Warning: noticeably hotter than the rest of the body.
3. **Pain does not settle 2 hours after rest + ice.** Normal: 1-hour soreness. Warning: > 2 hours, strong pain.
4. **Sleep is disturbed by knee pain.** Warning: can't fall asleep, keeps waking up.
5. **Extreme tiredness next day.** Warning: can't get out of bed, no appetite, feels drained.

### Back-off protocol

- **Day 1 of flare:** Rest day. Only ankle pumps, breathing, elevation, ice. No strengthening, no ROM.
- **Day 2:** Resume at HALF previous volume. Re-check 2 hours after.
- **Days 3–5:** Add one set or 2 reps daily, IF 2-2-2 rule stays green.
- **Do NOT try to "catch up." The knee cannot absorb it.**
